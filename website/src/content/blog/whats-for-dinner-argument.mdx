## The question nobody wants to answer

It is 6:15 on a Tuesday. Neither of you has thought about dinner since lunch, and now somebody has to. The conversation that follows is predictable: "I don't mind, you pick." "No, you pick." "What do we have?" "Not much." And so it goes, until one of you gives in, orders takeout, or cobble together something neither of you actually wanted.

This is not an argument about food. It is an argument about decisions — specifically, who is responsible for making the four hundredth decision of the day when both of you are already running on empty. Psychologists call it decision fatigue, and it is the reason the "what's for dinner?" question lands so hard at the end of a workday. By 6 pm, the part of your brain that weighs options and commits to one has already made dozens of choices — work emails, meeting replies, what to say to a colleague, whether to take the stairs — and it is spent.

The result is the same stand-off, night after night, in millions of kitchens. It is also the single most common reason couples stumble into the broader [one meal, two plates](/one-meal-two-plates) problem: even before you get to the plating maths, you cannot agree on what to cook in the first place.

## Why it keeps happening

The "what's for dinner?" argument is a systems problem disguised as a communication problem. Most couples try to solve it by talking — assigning whose turn it is, making a rota, taking turns choosing — and the fix works for a week or two before old patterns return. The reason it falls apart is that the system still requires someone to make a decision at 6 pm, when decision energy is at its lowest.

A meal planning system that depends on daily willpower is a meal planning system that will fail. The fix is not a better conversation about whose turn it is to choose. The fix is removing the daily decision entirely.

<Callout variant="info" title="The core insight">
You do not need to make better dinner decisions. You need to make fewer of them. A weekly plan that is already decided removes the nightly stand-off completely — there is nothing to argue about when the answer is already on the fridge.
</Callout>

## The fix: a shared weekly plan

The system that ends the argument has three parts. None of them require an app, a subscription, or more than thirty minutes a week.

### 1. Plan once, on the same day each week

Pick a day — Sunday morning, Friday evening, whenever you both have fifteen free minutes — and sit down together to decide the week's dinners. All of them. Five to seven meals, written down, with a rough ingredient list. This is the only dinner decision you make all week. Every other night, the answer is already decided.

The plan does not need to be elaborate. A list on the fridge is fine:

### 2. Shop from the plan, once

Once the week's meals are decided, the grocery list writes itself — you already know what you are cooking. One shop, one trip, and the ingredients are in the kitchen before Monday. No more 6 pm realisation that you are missing the one thing the recipe needs. If you want to take it further, a [pantry-first approach](/pantry-first-cooking) builds the plan around what you already have, cutting the shop even shorter.

### 3. Plate for two goals, not one

Here is where most weekly plans fall apart for couples with mismatched goals: the recipe serves four identical portions, but you are not four identical people. One of you is eating 1,500 calories; the other, 2,800. A plan that ignores this means one of you is either overeating or going hungry every night.

The fix is the [one meal, two plates](/one-meal-two-plates) method: cook the shared recipe once, then plate it two ways. The plan decides *what* you eat; the plating decides *how much*. Neither of you has to compromise on the recipe or on your goal.

<Callout variant="tip" title="The 30-second version">
Plan once on Sunday. Shop once from the plan. Cook one meal each night and plate it two ways. The nightly "what's for dinner?" question disappears — it was never about the food, it was about the unmade decision.
</Callout>

## What changes when the argument stops

Couples who move to a shared weekly plan report the same pattern: the first week feels like effort, the second week feels like relief, and by the third week the nightly stand-off is gone. The energy that was going into deciding what to eat goes into actually cooking it — or, more often, into not thinking about food at all until it is time to eat.

The mental load shifts too. In most households, one partner carries the invisible work of planning, shopping and deciding — what the sociologists who study household labour call the "mental load" or "cognitive labour." A shared plan, decided together, distributes that work across both people. Nobody is silently running the meal plan inside their head.

And when the plan is paired with the one-meal-two-plates method, the other common friction — "you eat too much" or "that's not enough for me" — resolves at the same time. Both plates are sized for their goal. Both people eat the same meal. The argument runs out of things to be about.
