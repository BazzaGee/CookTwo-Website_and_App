## The hardest pairing in the kitchen

One partner is cutting — eating 1,400–1,600 calories to shed fat, tracking every gram, training hard. The other is bulking — eating 2,800–3,200 to build muscle, chasing surplus protein, never missing a meal. The gap between your plates is the widest it can be: sometimes 1,500 calories or more in a single day.

This is the pairing that breaks most couples' meal plans. The conventional advice — "just cook separate meals" — means two of everything: two recipes, two sets of pans, two shopping lists, twice the time. Most couples last about a week before one of them gives up and eats whatever the other one is having, goals be damned.

It does not have to be that way. The [one meal, two plates](/one-meal-two-plates) method was built for exactly this scenario. You cook one set of food, and the two plates diverge so widely that they look like different meals — but they come from the same kitchen, the same session, the same effort. This post is the end-to-end system: targets, shopping, prep, and the daily plating for a cut-and-bulk couple.

![Two plates in dramatic contrast — a small cutting plate with chicken and greens next to a large bulking plate with extra chicken, rice, sweet potato, cheese and nuts](/images/blog/cut-bulk-couple-1.webp)

## Step 1: Set the two targets

Before you shop or cook, you need the numbers. The cut partner and the bulk partner each need a daily calorie target and a macro split. The [Mifflin–St Jeor equation](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5852756/) gives you the resting rate; from there, subtract 300–500 for a cut and add 300–500 for a bulk.

## Step 2: Shop for one kitchen, not two

The shopping list is shared. You are buying the same ingredients for both of you — the difference is purely in how much ends up on each plate. For a five-day prep:

Notice the pattern: the shared ingredients form the base of every meal. The surplus add-ons are calorie-dense extras that only go on Partner B's plate — they never touch Partner A's food. This is the key to cooking once and serving two wildly different targets.

## Step 3: The Sunday cook

Two hours. One kitchen. Here is the batch:

1. **Oven: 1.5 kg chicken breast** (seasoned, baked at 200°C for 25 min)
2. **Stovetop: 1 kg beef mince** (browned with onion and garlic for chilli base)
3. **Rice cooker: 2 kg rice** (enough for both partners all week)
4. **Oven tray 2: roasted vegetables** (peppers, courgette, red onion, 30 min)
5. **Morning: overnight oats** (Partner A: 40 g oats; Partner B: 80 g oats + banana + peanut butter)

<Callout variant="tip" title="The bulk plate's secret weapon">
Keep a jar of peanut butter and a bottle of olive oil next to the plating station. One tablespoon of peanut butter adds 95 calories and 4 g of protein in three seconds. A drizzle of olive oil adds 120 calories instantly. These are the fastest levers for closing the gap on Partner B's surplus target — and they never touch Partner A's plate.
</Callout>

## The levers that close a 1,500-calorie gap

When the gap is this wide, you need more than portion size to bridge it. Here are the levers, in order of impact:

- **Carb volume.** Partner B gets 2–3× the rice, oats and sweet potato. This alone adds 500+ calories.
- **Calorie-dense extras.** Peanut butter, nuts, olive oil, cheese, avocado — added only to the bulk plate. Each adds 100–200 calories per serving.
- **Protein scale.** Both plates get adequate protein, but Partner B's portions are 60–80% larger.
- **Liquid calories.** A mass gainer shake or smoothie with banana, oats and peanut butter can add 500–800 calories without making the bulk partner feel overfull.
