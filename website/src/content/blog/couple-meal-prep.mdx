## Sunday is the secret

The hardest part of cooking for two different goals is not the maths. It is the repetition. Knowing how to split one meal into two plates is one thing; doing it from scratch, five nights a week, with fresh ingredients and a clean pan each time, is where most couples quietly give up.

Meal prep Sunday solves this. You cook once — a single two-hour session on the weekend — and emerge with five nights of dinners portioned, stored, and ready to reheat. The [one meal, two plates](/one-meal-two-plates) method is baked in: each container is labelled and plated for its specific goal, so weekday dinner becomes a three-minute microwave job, not a forty-minute cook.

This post is the practical guide: what to cook, how to portion it, how to store it, and how to keep it from feeling like you are eating the same thing for five straight nights.

## The framework: cook bases, not meals

The mistake most people make with meal prep is cooking five finished meals — five different recipes, five different pans, five hours in the kitchen. The fix is to cook *bases* instead: large batches of modular components that combine into different meals throughout the week.

A base is a protein, a carbohydrate, and a vegetable — cooked separately, stored separately, and combined at plating time. This is the same modular structure that makes [one meal, two plates](/one-meal-two-plates) work, and it is what makes meal prep manageable.

## Step 2: Portion for two different goals

This is where the one-meal-two-plates method meets meal prep. When you box up the containers, you plate for each person's target — not identical portions. CookTwo's [adaptive cooking](/features/adaptive-cooking) engine can calculate the exact portions for both of you; if you are doing it by hand, the [macro maths post](/blog/one-meal-two-macros) has the full method.

The key principle: **protein stays adequate on both plates; carbs and fats move.**

Label each container. Five for Partner A, five for Partner B. Stack them in the fridge. Weekday dinner is now: open fridge, grab your container, microwave three minutes, eat.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Container system">
Use the same colour lid for Partner A and a different colour for Partner B. No label needed after week one — you know which stack is yours. Glass containers reheat better than plastic and do not absorb sauce smells.
</Callout>

## Step 3: Store it properly

Food safety matters more than convenience. The [USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service](https://www.fsis.usda.gov/) recommends refrigerating cooked food within two hours and consuming it within three to four days. For a five-day prep, this means:

- **Days 1–3:** refrigerated containers, reheated as normal.
- **Days 4–5:** frozen on Sunday, moved to the fridge Thursday morning to thaw.

Most bases freeze well — chicken, rice, roasted vegetables, and sauces all survive a freezer and reheat without losing texture. Sweet potato and potato can go slightly mealy, so eat those earlier in the week.

## Step 4: Reheat without ruining it

The goal is a meal that tastes freshly cooked, not like leftovers. A few techniques:

- **Reheat rice with a splash of water** and a lid on the container. The steam rehydrates the grains and stops them from going crunchy.
- **Reheat chicken separately** from the sauce, then combine at the end. This stops the chicken from going mushy.
- **Add fresh elements after reheating:** a handful of baby spinach, a squeeze of lemon, a sprinkle of fresh herbs. Thirty seconds of effort, and the meal tastes new.

## The Sunday timeline

Here is what a two-hour meal prep Sunday actually looks like:

| Time | Task |
|------|------|
| 0:00 | Oven on. Rice on. Sweet potatoes in. |
| 0:10 | Chicken prepped, seasoned, in the oven. |
| 0:15 | Vegetables chopped, on trays, in the oven. |
| 0:30 | Sauces started (stovetop). |
| 0:50 | Everything cooked. Start cooling. |
| 1:00 | Portion into containers — two plates per night. |
| 1:20 | Containers labelled and stored. Wipe down. |
| 1:30 | Done. Five nights of dinner handled. |
