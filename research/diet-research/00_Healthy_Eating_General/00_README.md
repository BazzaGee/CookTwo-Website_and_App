# Healthy Eating — General (Non-Dietary) Research Library

## What This Folder Is

This folder is the **foundational nutrition science library** for the Cupla app. It documents what the evidence says about healthy eating **for the general population** — that is, eating well without adhering to any named, restrictive, or branded diet.

Where the diet-specific folders under `diets/` (Mediterranean, Keto, DASH, Vegan, Carnivore, etc.) each answer the question *"What does the evidence say about THIS particular way of eating?"*, this folder answers a more fundamental question:

> **"What does the evidence say about eating well, full stop — independent of any single dietary philosophy?"**

It is the scientific bedrock on which the individual diet folders rest. Every claim in every diet folder is ultimately measured against the baseline established here.

---

## The Key Distinction: This Is NOT a Diet

This is the single most important point to understand about this folder.

**A diet** is a defined, prescriptive eating pattern — usually named, usually rule-based, usually restrictive of at least one macronutrient or food group, and usually promoted by an author, organization, or community. Examples in the Cupla library include Mediterranean, Keto, Paleo, Whole30, Carnivore, DASH, and 80/10/10.

**General healthy eating is not a diet.** It is the accumulated scientific understanding of how humans nourish themselves for health, longevity, and function. It does not have an author, a brand, a hashtag, or a book to sell. It has no "forbidden foods" list and no macro target you must hit to within a gram. It is simply what the world's major health authorities and the weight of nutrition research converge on as the way most people should eat most of the time.

This matters because the most common real-world scenario in nutrition is a person who does not want to follow a named diet — they just want to "eat healthier." For those users, the diet-specific folders are not the right reference. This folder is.

## Core Principles

The research summarized in this folder rests on three core, evidence-based principles. They are stated briefly here and developed at length in the files that follow.

### 1. Balanced Macronutrient Intake

Humans require all three macronutrients — **carbohydrate, fat, and protein** — in meaningful amounts. No macronutrient is inherently "bad," and no healthy population in recorded history has thrived long-term by eliminating any one of the three. The scientific consensus, reflected in the guidelines of every major health authority worldwide, is that health is supported by a **balance** of all three macronutrients within broad, flexible ranges — not by the elimination of any of them.

### 2. Food Quality Over Macronutrient Restriction

When researchers compare populations and dietary patterns, **food quality is a far stronger predictor of health outcomes than macronutrient ratio.** A diet of 50% carbohydrate from whole grains, legumes, vegetables, and fruit is not remotely the same thing as a diet of 50% carbohydrate from sugar-sweetened beverages and refined flour — even though the macronutrient percentage is identical. The single most consistent finding across modern nutrition science is that **whole, minimally-processed foods are associated with better health outcomes regardless of the precise macro split.** Restricting a macronutrient matters far less than improving the *quality* of the foods that make up the diet.

### 3. Global Dietary Diversity

Healthy traditional populations around the world thrive on **radically different macronutrient ratios.** The traditional Okinawan diet runs roughly 85% carbohydrate; the traditional Inuit diet historically ran 70–80% fat; the Maasai pastoralist diet is heavily animal-protein and fat based. Yet all of these populations — when eating their traditional, whole-food diets and living active lifestyles — demonstrated low rates of the chronic diseases that plague modern industrialized societies. This is among the most powerful evidence in all of nutrition: **there is no single "correct" macronutrient ratio for human health.** What these diverse diets share is whole-food quality, not a shared macro profile.

## Evidence Standards

This library uses the **same A–D evidence grading** system as the diet-specific folders. Every substantive claim about health outcomes is graded, and every landmark study is cited in full with DOI.

| Grade | Label | Meaning |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **A** | Strong | Multiple high-quality RCTs and/or large prospective cohorts and meta-analyses with consistent results. Gold standard. |
| **B** | Moderate–Strong | Supported by RCTs, prospective cohorts, or meta-analyses, but with some limitations (e.g., mostly observational, short duration, or mixed results). |
| **C** | Limited | Indirect, inferred, or primarily theoretical evidence; few or no direct studies; small samples. |
| **D** | Insufficient | No clinical trials; evidence is anecdotal, theoretical, or absent. |

**Overall evidence grade for the general healthy eating thesis: A (Strong).** The core claims in this folder — that all three macronutrients are essential, that balance is superior to elimination, and that food quality predicts health better than macro ratio — are supported by the largest and most methodologically rigorous bodies of evidence in nutrition science, including the PURE study (>135,000 participants across 18 countries), the Seidelmann et al. (2018) cohort analysis (>432,000 participants), and the 2024 Ma et al. umbrella review (124 studies, >5 million participants). Details and full citations are in `01_Macronutrients_Overview.md` and `08_Balanced_Eating_Consensus.md`.

## Who This Folder Is For (In the App)

This is the reference for any Cupla user who, when asked about their dietary preference, selects:

> **"No specific diet / just eat healthy"**

These users are not interested in the rules, restrictions, or macro targets of a named diet. They want straightforward, evidence-based guidance on how to eat well. This folder is the source of that guidance. It is also the appropriate fallback whenever a user has not explicitly chosen a diet — the principles here represent the safe, consensus, evidence-based default for the general population.

Because this guidance is non-restrictive, it is also the **most adherable** option for most people. As the evidence in `10_Sustainable_Healthy_Habits.md` and `08_Balanced_Eating_Consensus.md` makes clear, the single strongest predictor of long-term dietary success is not the specific diet chosen — it is **whether the person can sustain the pattern over years.** A balanced, whole-food, non-restrictive approach is, for most people, the most sustainable choice of all.

## A Note on Tone and Purpose

This library is deliberately **non-dogmatic.** It does not advocate for or against any named diet. It takes no side in the "macronutrient wars." Its only commitment is to representing the weight of the scientific evidence as accurately and fairly as possible.

Where the evidence is strong and consistent, it says so. Where it is uncertain or contested, it says that too. The goal is to give Cupla users — and the app's logic — a reliable, honest foundation for thinking about food, so that whatever dietary path a user ultimately chooses (including "no diet at all"), it is chosen on the basis of evidence rather than marketing, mythology, or fear.
