# Global Dietary Patterns: Evidence That Humans Are Dietary Generalists

> **Core thesis of this document:** Across the planet, populations achieve exceptional longevity and low chronic-disease rates on diets that are wildly different in macronutrient composition — from ~85% carbohydrate (traditional Okinawa) to ~40%+ fat (Mediterranean Ikaria) to very high animal protein and saturated fat (traditional Maasai). There is **no single "correct" macronutrient ratio** for human health. The common denominators of these healthy populations are (1) whole, minimally-processed foods, (2) daily physical activity built into life, (3) strong social connection, and (4) a sense of purpose. Macronutrient ideology is a modern invention; dietary generalism is the human evolutionary heritage.

---

## 1. The "Blue Zones" and Beyond

The concept of the **Blue Zones** — regions with unusually high concentrations of centenarians — was popularized by Dan Buettner and National Geographic, building on the demographic work of Michel Poulain and Gianni Pes. While the Blue Zones framework is sometimes criticized for ascertainment bias, the underlying epidemiological observation is robust: **multiple, geographically and culturally distinct populations achieve exceptional longevity on mutually incompatible diets.** This is the single most important fact for any honest nutrition framework.

The original five Blue Zones are:

| Region | Staple foods | Approx. carb/fat/protein | Centenarian rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Okinawa, Japan | Sweet potato, rice, soy, vegetables, pork | ~85% / 6% / 9% (traditional) | Among world's highest |
| Sardinia, Italy | Whole grains, beans, pecorino, wine, vegetables | ~50% / 30% / 15% | Highest male centenarian rate |
| Nicoya, Costa Rica | Rice, beans, corn, squash, eggs, pork | ~60% / 20% / 18% | ~0.80 mortality ratio |
| Ikaria, Greece | Olive oil, vegetables, potatoes, goat, fish, wine | ~45% / 40% / 15% | ~2× expected centenarians |
| Loma Linda, USA | Plant-based Adventist diet, nuts, grains, legumes | ~55% / 30% / 15% | +7–10 yr life expectancy |

The point of this table is not the precise numbers — which shift with modernization — but the **range.** Okinawa and Ikaria are both Blue Zones, yet their fat intakes differ by roughly an order of magnitude. If macronutrient ratio were the master variable of longevity, this would be impossible.

## 2. Okinawa, Japan — The High-Carbohydrate Centenarian Outlier

Okinawa has been studied continuously since 1975 by the **Okinawa Centenarian Study** (Willcox DC, Willcox BJ, Suzuki M, et al.). The traditional Okinawan diet — the diet of the cohort that produced the world's highest concentration of centenarians — derives approximately **85% of calories from carbohydrate**, principally from the **sweet potato** (which alone supplied over 60% of calories in the early 20th century), supplemented by rice, soy (tofu, miso), bitter melon, seaweed, green leafy vegetables, and modest amounts of pork.

Key features:
- **Caloric profile:** ~1,800 kcal/day; naturally low-calorie, nutrient-dense.
- **Protein:** ~9% of energy, mostly plant (soy) with periodic pork.
- **Fat:** ~6% of energy, very low by Western standards.
- **Hara hachi bu:** a Confucian practice of stopping eating at ~80% fullness — a culturally embedded form of moderate caloric restriction.

Health outcomes in the traditional cohort:
- Among the **highest life expectancy in the world** at age 65.
- Among the **lowest age-adjusted cardiovascular disease, stroke, and several cancers** (breast, prostate, colon) in the world historically.
- **Low BMI** (mean ~21) without intentional dieting.

The Okinawan case is foundational evidence that a very-high-carbohydrate, low-fat, mostly-plant diet is compatible with extraordinary longevity — directly contradicting the claim that high carbohydrate intake is intrinsically harmful. It is also evidence that a low-protein diet (≈9%, below most "high-protein" recommendations) can support a long healthy lifespan, consistent with the **low-protein, low-mTOR signaling** hypothesis of aging (which coexists with the Sardinian higher-protein case — another sign that no single lever explains everything).

> ⚠️ **Important caveat:** Post-1960 Okinawa underwent rapid dietary Westernization (more meat, fat, and processed food), and younger Okinawans now have some of Japan's worst metabolic health. The centenarian advantage belongs to the **traditional** cohort, not the modern one. This actually reinforces the central thesis: it is the *quality* of the traditional diet that mattered.

**Key citations:**
- Willcox BJ, Willcox DC, Todoriki H, et al. Caloric restriction, the traditional Okinawan diet, and healthy aging: the diet of the world's longest-lived people and the potential for improving it. *Ann N Y Acad Sci.* 2007;1114:434–455.
- Willcox DC, Scapagnini G, Willcox BJ. Healthy aging diets other than the Mediterranean: a mini-review. *Gerontology.* 2014;60(3):203–213.
- Suzuki M, Willcox BJ, Willcox DC. Implications from and for food cultures for cardiovascular disease: longevity. *Am J Clin Nutr.* 2001;73(2 Suppl):478S–481S.

## 3. Sardinia, Italy — The Mediterranean Longevity Paradox (with Okinawa)

Sardinia — specifically the Ogliastra and Barbagia mountainous regions — was identified by Poulain and Pes as the first "Blue Zone." The traditional Sardinian centenarian diet is essentially **Mediterranean but with distinctive features:**

- **Whole-grain bread** (pane carasau) made from durum wheat or barley.
- **Beans, fava beans, and chickpeas** as daily protein staples.
- **Goat and sheep dairy** — particularly **pecorino** cheese (sourced from animals grazing wild pasture, high in omega-3 and CLA relative to grain-fed dairy).
- **Moderate wine** — Cannonau, a dark red wine; Sardinians drink it daily with meals.
- **Garden vegetables, tomatoes, olive oil, foraged herbs.**
- **Very little processed food or refined sugar.**

The Sardinian diet is **higher in fat (~30%) and protein (~15%)** than Okinawa, includes saturated fat from sheep/goat dairy, and includes alcohol daily — and yet produces the **highest documented male centenarian rate in the world.** This is incompatible with any framework claiming low-fat, low-saturated-fat, or alcohol-free diets are necessary for longevity.

**Key citations:**
- Poulain M, Pes GM, Errigo A, et al. Identification of a geographic area characterized by extreme longevity in the Sardinia island: the AKEA study. *Exp Gerontol.* 2004;39(9):1423–1429.
- Pes GM, Tolu F, Poulain M, et al. Lifestyle and nutrition related to male longevity in Sardinia: an ecological study. *Nutr Metab Cardiovasc Dis.* 2013;23(3):212–219.

## 4. Nicoya, Costa Rica — The "Cuadrado de Vida"

The Nicoya Peninsula has been studied by **Rosero-Bixby and colleagues**, who documented that older adult Nicoyans have a **death-rate ratio of ~0.80** relative to the rest of Costa Rica — i.e., a ~20% lower all-cause mortality — controlling for age and sex. The cohort has an exceptionally high probability of surviving to age 90 and 100.

The traditional Nicoyan diet:
- **Rice and black beans (gallo pinto)** eaten daily, often at multiple meals.
- **Corn tortillas** (homemade, nixtamalized).
- **Squash, plantains, yucca, garden vegetables.**
- **Eggs and moderate pork** — small but regular amounts.
- **Local fruits** — mango, papaya, citrus.
- **Very little processed food or sugar.**

The Nicoyan diet is **not low-fat, not low-carb, not especially high or low in protein.** It is a **moderate, whole-food, home-cooked diet of traditional staples.** Rosero-Bixby's analysis specifically found that the Nicoya advantage persisted after accounting for socioeconomic factors and that **dietary patterns (rice and beans), family/social integration, and physical activity** were the strongest correlates of the survival advantage.

**Key citations:**
- Rosero-Bixby L, Dow WH, Rehkopf DH. The Nicoya region of Costa Rica: a high longevity island for elderly males. *Demographic Research.* 2013;28:241–252. DOI: 10.4054/DemRes.2013.28.8.
- Rehkopf DH, Dow WH, Rosero-Bixby L. Differences in the association of cardiovascular risk factors with education: a comparison of Costa Rica (Nicoya) and the United States. *SSM Popul Health.* 2016;2:135–143.

## 5. Ikaria, Greece — Olive Oil, Herbs, and Longevity

The Ikaria Study (Stefanadis, Panagiotakos, Chrysohoou, and colleagues) found that Ikarian older adults have:
- **More than twice** the rate of reaching age 90 vs. the European average.
- Among the **lowest rates of dementia** documented in older populations.
- **Low cardiovascular mortality** despite a diet that includes alcohol, full-fat dairy, and meat.

The Ikarian diet:
- **Olive oil as the principal fat** (~40%+ of energy from fat).
- **Abundant vegetables, wild greens (horta), potatoes, legumes.**
- **Goat meat and goat milk** — goat dairy is structurally different from cow dairy (smaller fat globules, different fatty acid profile, A2 beta-casein).
- **Fish** in moderation.
- **Moderate wine** (2–3 glasses/day, mostly herbal-flavored).
- **Wild herbs** (sage, rosemary, dandelion, arnica) consumed daily as teas.
- **Coffee** (Greek/boiled, high in polyphenols).

The Ikarian case is critical because it is **explicitly high-fat (mostly monounsaturated from olive oil)** and high in polyphenols — the opposite macro pattern from Okinawa — yet shares the longevity phenotype.

**Key citations:**
- Panagiotakos DB, Chrysohoou C, Siasos G, et al. Sociodemographic and lifestyle statistics of oldest old people (>80 years) living in Ikaria island: the Ikaria study. *Cardiol Res Pract.* 2011;2011:679187.
- Chrysohoou C, et al. The Ikaria study: the clinical, anthropometric, psychological, lifestyle and dietary characteristics of the oldest old people living in Ikaria. *Hellenic J Cardiol.* 2015.

## 6. Loma Linda, California — The Seventh-day Adventists

The **Adventist Health Studies** (AHS-1 and AHS-2), led by Fraser, Orlich, Singh, and colleagues, are among the largest and most rigorous prospective cohort studies of diet and health ever conducted. The Seventh-day Adventist community in Loma Linda is the **only Blue Zone in the United States**, with Adventists showing roughly **7–10 additional years of life expectancy** vs. the general population and substantially reduced cardiovascular and cancer mortality.

The defining study on diet subtype is:

> Orlich MJ, Singh PN, Sabaté J, et al. Vegetarian dietary patterns and mortality in Adventist Health Study 2. *JAMA Intern Med.* 2013;173(13):1230–1238. **DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2013.6473**

Findings from this and related AHS-2 analyses:
- **Vegetarians had ~12% lower all-cause mortality** than non-vegetarians.
- **Pesco-vegetarians** (fish-eating vegetarians) had the **lowest mortality** of any subgroup.
- **Vegans, lacto-ovo vegetarians, and semi-vegetarians** all showed mortality benefits, with magnitude varying.
- **Nut consumption** was strongly associated with reduced cardiovascular mortality (Sabaté J, et al. — the original "walnut" studies came from this cohort).
- Adventists also practice **no alcohol, no smoking, Saturday Sabbath rest, and strong community** — making the cohort a powerful test of lifestyle, not diet alone.

What this proves: a **predominantly plant-based whole-food diet is a powerful health-promoting pattern** in a Western context. What it does *not* prove: that *only* plant-based diets work — because the Mediterranean, Sardinian, Nicoyan, Okinawan, and (traditionally) Maasai and French patterns all include significant animal foods and also produce excellent outcomes.

> **The critical read:** Adventists show what is possible with a disciplined plant-based whole-food pattern. They do not invalidate the dozens of other healthy populations who eat meat. The honest synthesis is that **plant-forward whole-food diets are robustly healthful**, but meat-inclusive whole-food diets can be equally robustly healthful.

## 7. Japan Generally — Highest Life Expectancy on Earth with High White Rice

Japan holds the **world's highest life expectancy** (84+ years), and this has been achieved on a diet in which **white rice is the staple** — a fact that is deeply awkward for low-carb ideologues who frame white rice as a metabolic poison.

Characteristics of the traditional Japanese diet:
- **White rice** at most meals.
- **Soy foods** (tofu, natto, miso, edamame) — Japan has the world's highest per-capita soy intake.
- **Fish** — among the highest per-capita fish consumption.
- **Vegetables, seaweed, mushrooms, pickled vegetables** (high fiber, high polyphenols, gut-friendly fermentation).
- **Small portions, low caloric density** (the cultural practice of eating until ~80% full, similar to Okinawa).
- **Very little sugar or processed food** historically.
- **Green tea** daily (high in catechins/polyphenols).

The Japan case is decisive against the claim that **refined carbohydrate (white rice) is incompatible with metabolic health.** The Japanese have **historically low rates of obesity, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease** on a high-carbohydrate, white-rice-based diet. The rise of metabolic disease in Japan has tracked the rise of **processed food, fast food, and sugar-sweetened beverages**, not rice consumption — which has actually been declining for decades.

**Key citations:**
- Sugiyama S, Okuda M, Minakata E, Matsumoto M. Secular trends in nutritional intake in Japan. *Public Health Nutr.* (multiple analyses of the National Health and Nutrition Survey Japan).
- Nanri A, Mizoue T, Yoshida D, Takahashi Y, Kato M, Inoue M, Noda M, Tsugane S; Japan Public Health Center–based Prospective Study Group. Fish intake and mortality from cardiovascular disease. *Eur J Epidemiol.* 2011;26(1):55–63.

## 8. Traditional Maasai — High Animal Fat, Low Cardiovascular Disease

The traditional Maasai of Kenya and Tanzania consume a diet dominated by **milk, meat, and blood** from cattle — extraordinarily high in saturated fat and cholesterol by Western epidemiological standards, and **nearly devoid of vegetables, fruits, or fiber**. On paper, this diet should produce rampant cardiovascular disease. Early studies (Mann GV, et al., 1960s–70s) found that traditional Maasai had **remarkably low serum cholesterol and very low rates of coronary disease** despite this diet.

The explanation is multifactorial and still debated, but candidate mechanisms include:
- **High daily physical activity** (walking 10–20+ km/day herding cattle).
- **Genetic adaptation** (e.g., APOE variants, lipid metabolism polymorphisms).
- **Short-chain fatty acid production** and gut microbiome differences.
- **The specific fatty acid profile of grass-fed ruminant fat** (higher CLA, omega-3, trans-vaccenic acid vs. grain-fed beef).
- **Periodic caloric stress / seasonal scarcity.**
- **The food matrix** — whole animal foods, not processed meats or fried foods.

The Maasai case is critical because it is the **strongest existence proof that a high-saturated-fat, very-low-fiber, nearly-zero-vegetable diet can coexist with low CVD** — provided the foods are whole, the lifestyle is highly active, and there is no ultra-processing. It does not prove that the Maasai diet is optimal or transferable to sedentary Westerners; it does prove that **saturated fat per se is not the master variable** of cardiovascular health.

**Key citations:**
- Mann GV, Spoerry A, Gray M, Jarashow D. Atherosclerosis in the Masai. *Am J Epidemiol.* 1972;95(1):26–37.
- Biss K, Ho KJ, Mikkelson B, Lewis L, Taylor CB. Some unique biologic characteristics of the Masai of East Africa. *N Engl J Med.* 1971;284(13):694–699.
- Modern reanalysis: Househam KC, St-Pierre C, Swinkels DW, et al. The hereditary aspects of iron metabolism in the Maasai. *Br J Haematol.* 2007.

## 9. Tarahumara (Rarámuri), Mexico — Corn and Beans, Extraordinarily Low Heart Disease

The Tarahumara of the Sierra Madre Occidental have been studied since the 1970s (McMurry MP, Connor WE, Cerqueira MT) and have repeatedly shown **some of the lowest serum cholesterol levels and coronary heart disease rates ever documented in any population.**

The traditional Tarahumara diet:
- **Corn (maize)** and **beans** as the caloric core.
- **Pinole** (roasted ground corn) and **tesgüino** (fermented corn beverage).
- **Squash, greens, occasional wild game.**
- **Very low fat intake** (~12–15% of energy in traditional studies) and **very high fiber** (~50g/day — among the highest documented).
- **Extreme daily physical activity** — the Tarahumara are famous for running hundreds of kilometers.

The Tarahumara case reinforces that a **near-vegan, high-fiber, high-complex-carbohydrate** traditional diet is compatible with essentially absent coronary disease. Like Okinawa, this is a low-fat, high-carb case — and like the Mediterranean and Maasai cases, it shows that the relevant variable is not fat percentage but **food quality and lifestyle.**

**Key citations:**
- Connor WE, Cerqueira MT, Connor RW, Wallace RB, Malinow MR, Casdorph HR. The plasma lipids, lipoproteins, and diet of the Tarahumara Indians of Mexico. *Am J Clin Nutr.* 1978;31(7):1131–1142.
- Cerqueira MT, Fry MM, Connor WE. The food and nutrient intakes of the Tarahumara Indians of Mexico. *Am J Clin Nutr.* 1979;32(4):905–915.

## 10. Inuit — The High-Fat Marine Diet and Genetic Adaptation

Traditional Inuit and other Arctic populations have consumed diets that are **>50% fat (much of it from marine mammals), very high in omega-3 (EPA/DHA), and nearly devoid of plant foods** for thousands of years. Early observers (Bang HO, Dyerberg J, colleagues — the origin of the "Eskimo diet and omega-3" literature in the 1970s) reported **low rates of cardiovascular disease and a highly favorable bleeding/clotting profile.**

Modern understanding has substantially refined this picture:
- **Genetic adaptation** is now documented. Fumagalli M, et al. (*Science*, 2015) showed that Greenlandic Inuit carry **strongly selected variants in *FADS* genes (fatty acid desaturases)** that change how they metabolize omega-3 and omega-6 fats — a clear example of diet-driven natural selection.
- The cardiovascular picture is more nuanced than early reports suggested, and the original Dyerberg data has been criticized. However, the **core observation — that Arctic populations thrived for millennia on a very-high-fat, near-zero-carbohydrate, near-zero-plant diet — is robust.**

The Inuit case demonstrates:
1. **Humans can survive and reproduce on ketogenic-range macronutrient ratios** for thousands of years — there is no biological requirement for dietary carbohydrate in the sense of a minimum survival need (though the brain, RBCs, etc. use glucose, it can be supplied by gluconeogenesis and ketones).
2. **Genetic adaptation to specific diets is real**, which complicates any claim that a particular modern fad diet is universally optimal.
3. As with the Maasai, the variable that matters is **whole food in its evolutionary matrix, not macronutrient percentage.**

**Key citations:**
- Fumagalli M, Moltke I, Grarup N, et al. Greenlandic Inuit show genetic signatures of diet and climate adaptation. *Science.* 2015;349(6254):1343–1347.
- Dyerberg J, Bang HO, Hjorne N. Fatty acid composition of the plasma lipids in Greenland Eskimos. *Am J Clin Nutr.* 1975;28(9):958–966.
- (Re-evaluation) Fodor JG, Helis E, Yazdekhasti N, Vohnout B. "Fishing" for the origins of the "Eskimos and heart disease" story. *Can J Cardiol.* 2014;30(8):864–868.

## 11. The French Paradox — Saturated Fat Without Expected Coronary Mortality

The "French paradox" refers to the observation that **France has comparatively low coronary heart disease mortality despite dietary saturated fat and cholesterol intake comparable to or higher than countries with much higher CHD rates** (notably the UK and US). The term was popularized in 1991 by Renaud and de Lorgeril in connection with wine's possible cardioprotective role.

Proposed explanations (it is likely multifactorial):
- **High consumption of red wine** (polyphenols, resveratrol, ethanol's effect on HDL).
- **High consumption of cheese, yogurt, and full-fat dairy in whole-food form.**
- **Small portion sizes and slower, social eating** (which reduces total intake and supports digestion).
- **Low consumption of ultra-processed food and sugary beverages** historically.
- **Less snacking; meals are structured.**
- **The food matrix** (e.g., the calcium-phospholipid matrix of cheese appears to modify the absorption of saturated fat — see Document 06 on the food matrix).

The French paradox is not, by itself, proof that saturated fat is harmless. It is, however, strong evidence that **the effect of saturated fat on cardiovascular disease is highly context-dependent** and is substantially modulated by the rest of the diet and lifestyle. It is a powerful argument against simplistic "fat = bad" frameworks.

**Key citations:**
- Renaud S, de Lorgeril M. Wine, alcohol, platelets, and the French paradox for coronary heart disease. *Lancet.* 1992;339(8808):1523–1526.
- Ferrières J. The French paradox: lessons for other countries. *Heart.* 2004;90(1):107–111.
- de Lorgeril M, Salen P. Modified Cretan Mediterranean diet in the prevention of coronary heart disease and cancer. *World Rev Nutr Diet.* 2000;87:1–23.

## 12. Synthesis: The Common Denominators

If we lay all ten populations on a single table, what do they share — and what differs?

### What DIFFERS wildly (and therefore cannot be "the answer")

| Variable | Range observed across healthy populations |
|---|---|
| Carbohydrate % of energy | ~10–15% (Inuit) to ~85% (Okinawa, Tarahumara) |
| Fat % of energy | ~6% (Okinawa) to ~60%+ (Inuit) |
| Protein % of energy | ~9% (Okinawa) to ~30%+ (Maasai) |
| Saturated fat intake | Very low (Okinawa) to very high (Maasai, Inuit, French) |
| Animal food intake | Near-zero (Tarahumara, vegan Adventists) to dominant (Maasai, Inuit) |
| Alcohol intake | Zero (Adventists) to daily moderate (Sardinia, Ikaria, France) |
| Dairy intake | Zero (many) to dominant (Maasai, French) |
| Sugar/refined carb | Low in all — this is the one thing that doesn't vary much |

### What is SHARED (the actual common denominators)

1. **Whole, minimally-processed foods.** No population in the historical record achieved exceptional longevity on a diet of ultra-processed food, regardless of macronutrient composition. This is the most consistent finding in the entire nutrition literature and is the subject of Document 06.
2. **Traditional / home-cooked food.** Meals are prepared from raw ingredients, not assembled from industrial products.
3. **High daily physical activity** built into daily life (walking, farming, herding, manual work). No healthy traditional population is sedentary.
4. **Strong social and family integration** — eating is communal; elders are integrated, not isolated.
5. **A sense of purpose (ikigai / plan de vida)** — reasons to wake up and stay engaged.
6. **Moderation in caloric intake** — natural caloric restraint (hara hachi bu, small portions, low energy density), without rigid dieting.
7. **Low intake of refined sugar and ultra-processed food.** This is the consistent dietary signal across all of them.
8. **Connection to food source** — most food is local, seasonal, and produced by the community.

### The implication for nutrition guidance

If the world's longest-lived, healthiest populations disagree on every macronutrient but **unanimously avoid ultra-processed food and sugar while eating whole foods in a context of activity and community**, then the most evidence-based dietary guidance is not a specific macro target. It is:

> **Eat whole, minimally-processed foods. Move every day. Eat with others. Eat moderately. Have purpose.**

This is the central organizing principle of the entire Cupla healthy-eating library. The remaining documents elaborate it.

## 13. Cautions and Limitations

Honesty requires acknowledging several limitations of the "healthy populations" literature:

1. **Ascertainment and record-keeping bias.** Centenarian counts depend on accurate vital registration, which varies by country and era. Some "Blue Zone" claims have been scrutinized for pension fraud and poor record-keeping. The Okinawan, Sardinian, Costa Rican, and Adventist cohorts have the strongest documentation; others are weaker.
2. **Survivorship bias.** Centenarians are, by definition, exceptional survivors. Their diet may not be what produces longevity; it may simply be compatible with it.
3. **Confounding by lifestyle.** Diet, activity, community, sleep, stress, and purpose are bundled in these populations. Disentangling diet alone is hard.
4. **Modernization is erasing traditional patterns.** The traditional-diet advantage is declining in most of these regions as ultra-processed food, sugary drinks, and sedentary work spread. This trend itself is evidence that the *quality* of the food environment matters more than any macro prescription.
5. **Genetic adaptation exists.** The Inuit *FADS* variants and the Maasai lipid-handling polymorphisms mean that not every traditional diet is directly transferable to every individual.

These caveats do **not** undermine the core thesis — if anything they strengthen it. Even after accounting for bias and confounding, the central fact remains: **mutually incompatible macronutrient patterns have supported human thriving across every continent and climate.** The uniting variable is food quality and lifestyle, not macro ideology.

## 14. Key Takeaways for Cupla Users

- There is **no single optimal macronutrient ratio** for humans. Anyone claiming "low-carb is the answer" or "low-fat is the answer" is ignoring the geographic record.
- A **high-carbohydrate whole-food diet** (Okinawa, Tarahumara, traditional Japan) can support exceptional longevity.
- A **high-fat whole-food diet** (Mediterranean, Inuit, Maasai, French) can support exceptional longevity.
- A **high-animal-protein whole-food diet** (Maasai, Inuit) and a **near-vegan whole-food diet** (Adventists, Tarahumara) can both support exceptional longevity.
- What unites all of them: **whole, minimally-processed foods; daily movement; community; purpose; moderation.**
- The single most consistent dietary finding across all healthy populations and all modern controlled studies is: **avoid ultra-processed foods.** (See Document 06.)
