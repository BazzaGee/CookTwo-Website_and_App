# Paleo Diet — Overview

## Definition

The Paleo (Paleolithic) diet is a dietary pattern based on eating foods presumed to have been available to human ancestors during the Paleolithic era — a period spanning roughly **2.5 million years to approximately 10,000 years ago** (before the advent of agriculture). Also known as the **Caveman Diet**, **Stone Age Diet**, or **Hunter-Gatherer Diet**, it is defined by:

- Lean meats (especially grass-fed, wild game)
- Fish and seafood
- Fresh fruits and vegetables
- Eggs
- Nuts and seeds
- Healthy oils (olive, avocado, coconut)

It strictly **excludes** all foods that became available after the Agricultural Revolution (~10,000 years ago):

- All grains (wheat, rice, oats, corn, barley, etc.)
- All legumes (beans, lentils, peanuts, soy)
- Dairy products
- Refined sugar
- Potatoes (in strict versions)
- Added salt
- Highly processed foods
- Refined vegetable oils (canola, soybean, sunflower, corn)

The core premise is that **human genetics evolved to thrive on pre-agricultural foods**, and that the rapid introduction of grains, dairy, legumes, and processed foods during the Neolithic Revolution created a **mismatch** between our genome and modern diet — a mismatch that underlies many chronic "diseases of civilization" (obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, autoimmune conditions).

## History & Origins

### The Evolutionary Hypothesis (1980s–1990s)

The intellectual roots of the Paleo diet trace to the field of **evolutionary medicine**. In 1985, **S. Boyd Eaton** and **Melvin Konner** published a seminal paper in the *New England Journal of Medicine* titled *"Paleolithic Nutrition: A Consideration of Its Nature and Current Implications,"* which argued that human nutritional requirements were established during the Paleolithic era and that modern diets deviate significantly from the conditions under which our metabolism evolved.

> **Key reference:** Eaton SB, Konner M. "Paleolithic nutrition. A consideration of its nature and current implications." *New England Journal of Medicine*. 1985;312(5):283-289.

Eaton further developed these ideas in subsequent publications throughout the 1990s and 2000s, arguing that Paleolithic humans consumed more protein, more fiber, more micronutrients, and less sodium and refined carbohydrate than modern populations.

### Loren Cordain and the Modern Paleo Movement (2002)

**Loren Cordain, PhD** (Colorado State University) is credited with popularizing the Paleo diet for the general public. His 2002 book **"The Paleo Diet: Lose Weight and Get Healthy by Eating the Foods You Were Designed to Eat"** became the foundational text of the modern Paleo movement. Cordain's academic background in exercise physiology and nutrition gave the diet a veneer of scientific credibility, and he went on to publish dozens of peer-reviewed papers on Paleolithic nutrition.

Key elements of Cordain's framework:
- Emphasis on **lean meats** (Cordain's original version favored very lean meats and was relatively low in saturated fat)
- Exclusion of all post-agricultural foods
- Framework based on estimated macronutrient ratios of hunter-gatherer diets
- The "evolutionary discordance hypothesis" — modern diet as the root cause of chronic disease

### Robb Wolf and the Primal Movement

**Robb Wolf**, a former research biochemist and student of Cordain, further popularized the diet through his 2010 book **"The Paleo Solution: The Original Human Diet"** and his influential podcast. Wolf framed the diet in more accessible terms, connecting it to fitness, athletic performance, and autoimmune management.

**Mark Sisson** offered a more flexible variant called **"Primal"** through his 2009 book *"The Primal Blueprint,"* which permitted full-fat dairy, certain starches, and a more liberal attitude toward saturated fat — blurring the lines between strict Paleo and more moderate approaches.

### The Ancestral Health Movement

The Paleo diet spawned a broader **ancestral health movement**, formalized through:
- The **Ancestral Health Symposium** (first held in 2011)
- The **Ancestral Health Society** and its journal
- CrossFit culture, which adopted Paleo eating as its de facto nutrition philosophy
- A proliferation of Paleo cookbooks, blogs, meal delivery services, and packaged foods

## Core Philosophy

The Paleo diet is built on several philosophical pillars:

1. **Evolutionary discordance** — The human genome has not had sufficient time to adapt to agricultural and industrial foods. 10,000 years is an evolutionary blink; selective pressures cannot have fully adapted us to grains, dairy, and processed foods.
2. **Food quality over quantity** — Whole, unprocessed foods are inherently superior to processed foods, regardless of macronutrient ratios.
3. **Elimination as a diagnostic tool** — Removing potentially problematic food groups (grains, dairy, legumes) eliminates common irritants, allergens, and antinutrients (lectins, phytates, gluten).
4. **Nutrient density** — Animal foods (especially organ meats, bone broth) and plant foods in their whole form provide higher micronutrient density per calorie than refined agricultural products.
5. **Satiety and blood sugar stability** — Higher protein and fat intake, combined with elimination of refined carbohydrates, promotes stable blood sugar and reduced hunger.
6. **Anti-inflammatory eating** — By eliminating seed oils, refined sugar, and gluten-containing grains, the diet reduces dietary drivers of systemic inflammation.

## Key Components (The Paleo Hierarchy)

### Eat Liberally (Core Foods)
- **Lean meats:** Grass-fed beef, bison, lamb, wild game, poultry (skinless in strict versions)
- **Fish and seafood:** Especially fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring)
- **Eggs:** Pasture-raised preferred
- **Vegetables:** All non-starchy vegetables (leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower, peppers, zucchini, tomatoes, etc.)
- **Fruits:** All fresh fruits (moderate amounts of high-sugar fruits if weight loss is a goal)
- **Nuts and seeds:** Almonds, walnuts, macadamia, pecans, Brazil nuts, pumpkin seeds, sunflower seeds, flaxseeds, chia seeds
- **Healthy fats:** Olive oil, avocado oil, coconut oil, tallow, lard (in some versions), ghee (in some versions)
- **Tubers:** Sweet potatoes, yams, cassava (in moderate or "modified" versions; excluded in strict Cordain Paleo)

### Strictly Avoid (Eliminated Foods)
- **All grains:** Wheat, rice, oats, corn, barley, rye, millet, sorghum, and all products made from them (bread, pasta, cereal, tortillas)
- **All legumes:** Beans, lentils, chickpeas, peanuts (technically a legume), soy and all soy products, peas
- **Dairy:** Milk, cheese, yogurt, butter, cream (some modified versions allow full-fat dairy or ghee)
- **Refined sugar:** White sugar, brown sugar, high-fructose corn syrup, agave nectar (honey and maple syrup permitted in moderation in most versions)
- **Refined/seed oils:** Canola, soybean, sunflower, safflower, corn oil, margarine
- **Added salt** (strict versions)
- **White potatoes** (strict versions; modified versions allow them)
- **All processed foods:** Anything with artificial ingredients, preservatives, or that comes in a package with unrecognizable ingredients

### Foods That Are Debated Within Paleo
| Food | Status | Rationale |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **Honey, maple syrup** | Usually allowed in moderation | Natural sweeteners available pre-agriculture |
| **Coconut flour, almond flour** | Usually allowed | Used to create "Paleo" versions of baked goods; some purists reject them |
| **Ghee (clarified butter)** | Often allowed | Milk solids removed; some tolerate it well |
| **White potatoes** | Excluded in strict Paleo; allowed in modified | Technically a whole food, but excluded by Cordain for high glycemic load |
| **Quinoa, buckwheat** | Usually excluded | Pseudo-cereals; excluded under the "no grains" rule despite not being true grains |
| **Coffee, alcohol** | Excluded by purists; allowed in moderation by most practitioners | Neither would have been available pre-agriculture; widely consumed by modern Paleo adherents |

## Variations & Sub-Types

### Strict / Classic Paleo (Cordain)
- Very lean meats emphasized
- Low saturated fat
- Limited starchy vegetables
- No dairy, no added salt, no honey/sweeteners
- ~25% carb, ~30% protein, ~45% fat

### Primal / Modified Paleo (Sisson)
- Full-fat dairy permitted (especially raw, fermented)
- Saturated fat embraced (butter, coconut oil, fatty cuts of meat)
- More relaxed about sweeteners (honey, maple syrup)
- White potatoes and rice sometimes included
- Greater emphasis on lifestyle factors (sleep, sunlight, play)

### Autoimmune Paleo (AIP)
- An even stricter elimination protocol designed for people with autoimmune conditions
- Additionally removes: eggs, nightshades (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, potatoes, paprika), nuts, seeds, and seed-based spices
- Reintroduces foods gradually, monitoring symptoms
- Functions as a therapeutic elimination diet, not a permanent lifestyle

### Pegan (Mark Hyman)
- A hybrid of Paleo and vegan principles
- Emphasizes plant foods as 75% of the plate
- Includes limited whole grains (gluten-free) and legumes
- High-quality animal protein as a "condiment"

### Keto-Paleo
- Combines Paleo food restrictions with ketogenic macronutrient ratios
- Very low carbohydrate (<50g/day), high fat
- Eliminates most fruits and starchy vegetables

## Who It's For

- ✅ **May benefit:** people with metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance, or type 2 diabetes; those seeking to reduce processed food intake; people with non-celiac gluten sensitivity; individuals who respond well to structured, rule-based eating; those with mild autoimmune symptoms (under the AIP variant)
- ✅ **Popular among:** CrossFit athletes, fitness communities, biohackers, and people seeking weight loss through whole-food approaches
- ⚠️ **Caution for:** people with kidney disease (high protein load), those with eating disorder histories (rigid rules can trigger restriction), pregnant/lactating women (calcium and folate needs may be unmet without dairy and grains), budget-conscious individuals (grass-fed meat, wild fish, and nuts are expensive)
- ❌ **Not ideal for:** endurance athletes needing high carbohydrate intake (unless modified), vegans/vegetarians (very difficult without grains and legumes), people who require low-protein diets, and those who find highly restrictive diets unsustainable long-term

## Summary

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Origin** | Evolutionary medicine hypothesis (Eaton & Konner, 1985); popularized by Loren Cordain (2002) |
| **Key figures** | Loren Cordain, Robb Wolf, Mark Sisson |
| **Evidence grade** | **C** — Limited evidence; short-term RCTs show metabolic benefits, but evolutionary premise disputed |
| **Restrictiveness** | High (4/5) — Eliminates all grains, legumes, dairy, and processed foods |
| **Primary health focus** | Metabolic health, weight loss, reducing inflammation, autoimmune support |
| **Sustainability** | Moderate — Drop-out rates in studies are significant; social dining is challenging |
| **Cost** | High (grass-fed meat, wild-caught fish, nuts are expensive) |
| **Philosophy** | Evolutionary discordance: humans are genetically adapted to pre-agricultural foods |
