# Mediterranean Diet — Science & Evidence

> **Evidence Grade: A (Strong)** — The Mediterranean diet has the most robust evidence base of any dietary pattern, supported by multiple large RCTs, prospective cohorts, and meta-analyses. It is the gold standard in nutrition research.

---

## Evidence Grading

| Domain | Grade | Basis |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Cardiovascular disease prevention | **A** | PREDIMED RCT, Lyon Diet Heart Study, multiple meta-analyses |
| All-cause mortality reduction | **A** | Meta-analyses of prospective cohorts (>4 million participants) |
| Type 2 diabetes prevention/management | **A** | PREDIMED subgroup analysis; SUN cohort; PREDIMED-Plus |
| Cognitive decline / dementia prevention | **B** | PREDIMED-Navarra cognition substudy; observational data |
| Cancer risk reduction | **B** | EPIC cohort; Italian Moli-sani study |
| Weight management | **B** | PREDIMED, PREDIMED-Plus; comparison with low-fat diets |
| Metabolic syndrome reversal | **A** | PREDIMED; meta-analyses |

## Landmark Studies

### 1. The PREDIMED Trial (Prevención con Dieta Mediterránea)

**The single most important Mediterranean diet RCT.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Multicenter, randomized, controlled, parallel-group clinical trial |
| **Participants** | 7,447 individuals (55–80 years) at high cardiovascular risk but no CVD at enrollment |
| **Duration** | Median 4.8 years (stopped early due to efficacy) |
| **Groups** | (1) Mediterranean diet + extra-virgin olive oil (~1L/week), (2) Mediterranean diet + mixed nuts (30g/day), (3) Control: low-fat diet |
| **Primary outcome** | Major cardiovascular events (MI, stroke, CV death) |
| **Result** | **~30% relative risk reduction** in major CV events in both Mediterranean diet groups vs. control |
| **Publication** | Estruch R, et al. *New England Journal of Medicine*, 2013 |

> **Citation:** Estruch R, Ros E, Salas-Salvadó J, et al. "Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease with a Mediterranean Diet Supplemented with Extra-Virgin Olive Oil or Nuts." *New England Journal of Medicine*. 2018;378(25):e34.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMoa1800389
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29897819/

> **Note on the 2018 republication:** The original 2013 publication was retracted and republished in 2018 after it was discovered that randomization was not properly implemented at some sites (some participants were cluster-randomized by household/village rather than individually). The re-analysis showed the results remained robust — the Mediterranean diet still showed a ~30% reduction in CV events. The corrected version is the 2018 publication cited above.

**Key PREDIMED findings:**
- **Stroke risk reduced by ~39%** in the Mediterranean diet + olive oil group (HR 0.61)
- **Stroke risk reduced by ~34%** in the Mediterranean diet + nuts group (HR 0.66)
- No significant difference in MI or CV death individually (the composite endpoint was driven mainly by stroke reduction)
- **Type 2 diabetes incidence reduced by 52%** among participants without diabetes at baseline (Salas-Salvadó et al., 2011; 2014)
- **Metabolic syndrome prevalence decreased** and many participants reversed it

> **Citation (diabetes subgroup):** Salas-Salvadó J, Bulló M, Estruch R, et al. "Prevention of diabetes with Mediterranean diets: a subgroup analysis of a randomized trial." *Annals of Internal Medicine*. 2014;160(1):1-10.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.7326/M13-1725
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24573661/

### 2. The Lyon Diet Heart Study

**The first major RCT of the Mediterranean diet for secondary prevention of heart disease.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Randomized, controlled, single-blind |
| **Participants** | 605 patients who had survived a first MI |
| **Duration** | 46 months (mean follow-up 27 months) |
| **Groups** | (1) Mediterranean-style diet (high in alpha-linolenic acid from canola oil-based margarine), (2) Control: usual post-MI prudent diet |
| **Result** | **~50–70% reduction** in cardiac death and non-fatal MI in the Mediterranean group |
| **Publication** | de Lorgeril M, et al. *Circulation*, 1999 |

> **Citation:** de Lorgeril M, Salen P, Martin JL, Monjaud I, Delaye J, Mamelle N. "Mediterranean diet, traditional risk factors, and the rate of cardiovascular complications after myocardial infarction: final report of the Lyon Diet Heart Study." *Circulation*. 1999;99(6):779-785.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1161/01.CIR.99.6.779
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9989963/

**Significance:** This study was remarkable because the benefit was seen despite **no difference** in traditional risk factors (lipids, blood pressure, BMI) between groups — suggesting the diet had effects beyond what standard risk markers capture (likely anti-inflammatory, antithrombotic, and endothelial effects).

### 3. The Seven Countries Study

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Prospective cohort (longitudinal) |
| **Participants** | ~12,763 men, aged 40–59 at baseline |
| **Duration** | 1958–present (>60 years of follow-up) |
| **Countries** | US, Finland, Netherlands, Italy, former Yugoslavia, Greece (Crete/Corfu), Japan |
| **Key finding** | Saturated fat intake strongly predicted CHD mortality (r > 0.8); Mediterranean populations (Crete, Corfu, Italy) had the lowest CV mortality |

> **Citation:** Keys A. *Seven Countries: A Multivariate Analysis of Death and Coronary Heart Disease.* Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press; 1980.
> **Modern analysis:** Menotti A, et al. "Forty-year coronary mortality trends and changes in major risk factors in the first 10 years of follow-up in the seven countries study." *European Journal of Epidemiology*. 2007;22(11):747-754.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10654-007-9177-0

### 4. PREDIMED-Plus

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Multicenter RCT |
| **Participants** | 6,874 adults (55–75 years) with overweight/obesity and metabolic syndrome |
| **Intervention** | Energy-restricted Mediterranean diet + physical activity + behavioral support vs. Mediterranean diet alone (ad libitum) |
| **Result** | Greater weight loss (-3.2 kg vs -1.4 kg at 12 months), greater reduction in waist circumference, improved metabolic syndrome components |

> **Citation:** Salas-Salvadó J, et al. "Effect of a Lifestyle Intervention Program With Energy-Restricted Mediterranean Diet and Exercise on Weight Loss and Cardiovascular Risk Factors in Overweight or Obese Patients: The PREDIMED-Plus Randomized Clinical Trial." *JAMA*. 2019;322(15):1451-1462.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.2019.14688
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31609293/

---

### 5. The DIRECT-PLUS / Green Mediterranean Trial

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | 6-month RCT, 3 arms |
| **Participants** | 294 participants with abdominal obesity/dyslipidemia |
| **Groups** | (1) Healthy dietary guidelines, (2) Mediterranean diet, (3) **Green Mediterranean** diet (higher in polyphenols: Mankai duckweed, green tea, walnuts; minimal red meat) |
| **Result** | Green Mediterranean produced greater weight loss, greater reduction in waist circumference, and greater improvement in cardiometabolic markers than standard Mediterranean |

> **Citation:** Yaskolka Meir A, Rinott E, Tsaban G, et al. "Effect of Green-Mediterranean Diet on Weight Loss and Cardiometabolic Risk: A Randomized Clinical Trial." *JAMA Network Open*. 2022;5(4):e223591.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2022.3591

## Key Meta-Analyses

### Mediterranean Diet and All-Cause Mortality

> **Citation:** Sofi F, Macchi C, Abbate R, Gensini GF, Casini A. "Mediterranean diet and health status: an updated meta-analysis and a proposal for a literature-based adherence score." *Public Health Nutrition*. 2014;17(12):2769-2782.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1017/S1368980013003169
>
> **Findings:** A 2-point increase in Mediterranean diet adherence score was associated with:
> - **8% reduction in all-cause mortality** (RR 0.92)
> - **10% reduction in cardiovascular mortality** (RR 0.90)
> - **6% reduction in cancer incidence** (RR 0.94)
> - **13% reduction in neurodegenerative disease** (RR 0.87) (Parkinson's, Alzheimer's)

### Mediterranean Diet and Cardiovascular Disease (Meta-analysis of RCTs)

> **Citation:** Liyanage T, Ninomiya T, Wang A, et al. "Effects of the Mediterranean Diet on Cardiovascular Outcomes: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2016;100(4):987-994.

### Mediterranean Diet and Type 2 Diabetes

> **Citation:** Esposito K, Maiorino MI, Bellastella G, Chiodini P, Panagiotakos D, Giugliano D. "A journey into a Mediterranean diet and type 2 diabetes: a systematic review with meta-analyses." *BMJ Open*. 2015;5(8):e008222.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1136/bmjopen-2015-008222
>
> **Findings:** Mediterranean diet associated with:
> - Better glycemic control (HbA1c reduction of -0.30%)
> - Improved cardiovascular risk factors
> - **49% reduction in new-onset diabetes** (RR 0.51) in primary prevention

### Mediterranean Diet and Cognitive Decline

> **Citation:** Loughrey DG, Lavecchia S, Brennan S, Lawlor BA, Kelly ME. "The Impact of the Mediterranean Diet on the Cognitive Functioning of Healthy Older Adults: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis." *Advances in Nutrition*. 2017;8(4):571-586.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3945/an.117.015652
>
> **Findings:** Greater Mediterranean diet adherence associated with improved cognitive function, slower cognitive decline, and reduced risk of Alzheimer's disease.

### Mediterranean Diet and Longevity

> **Citation:** Eleftheriou D, Benetou V, Trichopoulou A, La Vecchia C, Bamia C. "Mediterranean diet and its components in relation to all-cause mortality: meta-analysis." *British Journal of Nutrition*. 2018;120(10):1081-1097.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1017/S000711451800269X

## Mechanisms of Action

The Mediterranean diet's health benefits are attributed to multiple synergistic mechanisms:

### 1. Lipid Profile Improvement
- **Olive oil (monounsaturated fat):** Increases HDL cholesterol, reduces LDL oxidation
- **Fish (omega-3 fatty acids):** Lowers triglycerides, reduces arrhythmia risk
- **Low saturated fat:** Reduces LDL cholesterol

### 2. Anti-Inflammatory Effects
- Olive oil polyphenols (oleocanthal, oleuropein) have COX-inhibiting anti-inflammatory activity similar to ibuprofen
- High antioxidant intake from vegetables, fruits, nuts, and olive oil reduces oxidative stress and CRP levels
- PREDIMED showed significant reductions in inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, ICAM-1, VCAM-1)

> **Citation:** Estruch R. "Anti-inflammatory effects of the Mediterranean diet: the experience of the PREDIMED study." *Proceedings of the Nutrition Society*. 2010;69(3):333-340.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1017/S0029665110001539

### 3. Endothelial Function
- Mediterranean diet improves flow-mediated dilation (a measure of blood vessel health)
- Olive oil polyphenols enhance nitric oxide bioavailability
- Reduced oxidative stress preserves endothelial integrity

### 4. Antithrombotic Effects
- Omega-3 fatty acids reduce platelet aggregation
- Moderate wine consumption (resveratrol, polyphenols) has mild anticoagulant effects

### 5. Gut Microbiome Modulation
- High fiber intake from legumes, whole grains, and vegetables promotes beneficial gut bacteria
- Short-chain fatty acid production (butyrate, propionate) has anti-inflammatory and metabolic benefits
- PREDIMED showed Mediterranean diet alters gut microbiome composition favorably

> **Citation:** Garcia-Mantrana I, et al. "Gut Microbiome Composition and Mediterranean Diet Adherence: The PREDIMED-Plus Study." *Molecular Nutrition & Food Research*. 2023;67(4):e2200618.

### 6. Insulin Sensitivity
- Mediterranean diet improves insulin sensitivity independent of weight loss
- High fiber content slows glucose absorption
- Monounsaturated fats improve beta-cell function

### 7. Gene Expression / Epigenetic Effects
- Olive oil polyphenols may modulate gene expression related to inflammation and oxidative stress
- Emerging research on microRNA modulation by Mediterranean diet components

## Comparison with Other Diets

| Comparison | Mediterranean vs. | Result |
|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| vs. Low-fat diet | **Mediterranean superior** for CV events, weight loss, and adherence (PREDIMED) |
| vs. Keto | Comparable short-term weight loss; Mediterranean better for long-term CV outcomes and LDL |
| vs. Vegan | Both effective; Mediterranean includes more B12, omega-3 (DHA/EPA); vegan better for LDL |
| vs. Paleo | Mediterranean has stronger evidence base and is less restrictive; both emphasize whole foods |

## Limitations & Caveats

1. **The 2018 PREDIMED correction:** The randomization irregularities don't invalidate the findings (results were robust), but they are worth noting when citing the study.
2. **Observational confounding:** Many Mediterranean diet studies are observational (cohort), meaning confounders (e.g., healthier lifestyle, higher SES, more physical activity) could partially explain associations. PREDIMED's RCT design largely addresses this.
3. **Sustainability of adherence:** Some participants struggle to maintain the diet long-term, particularly outside Mediterranean countries where the food culture differs.
4. **Cost accessibility:** Extra virgin olive oil, fresh fish, and nuts can be expensive, limiting accessibility.
5. **Mediterranean ≠ any "healthy diet":** The benefits appear to be specifically tied to the Mediterranean pattern (olive oil, fish, nuts, polyphenols), not just any plant-rich diet.

## Emerging Research Areas

- **Green Mediterranean / Green-MED:** Higher polyphenol content showing enhanced benefits
- **Microbiome-mediated effects:** How Mediterranean diet reshapes gut bacteria for health
- **Precision nutrition:** Genetic variations in response to Mediterranean diet (e.g., APOE ε4 carriers)
- **Mediterranean diet and depression:** SMILES trial and HELFIMED showing mood benefits
- **Mediterranean diet in pregnancy:** Reduced risk of gestational diabetes and adverse outcomes

> **Citation (depression):** Jacka FN, et al. "A randomised controlled trial of dietary improvement for adults with major depression (the 'SMILES' trial)." *BMC Medicine*. 2017;15:23.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-017-0791-y
