# Mediterranean Diet — Overview

## Definition

The Mediterranean diet is a dietary pattern inspired by the traditional eating habits of countries bordering the Mediterranean Sea — particularly Greece, southern Italy, Spain, and southern France in the mid-20th century (roughly the 1940s–1960s). It is not a single, prescriptive diet but rather a **food pattern** characterized by:

- Abundant plant foods (vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, seeds)
- Olive oil as the principal fat source
- Moderate amounts of fish and seafood
- Moderate consumption of poultry, eggs, and dairy (especially cheese and yogurt)
- Low to moderate amounts of red meat
- Moderate wine consumption with meals (optional)

It is widely regarded as the **most evidence-supported dietary pattern in nutrition science** for overall health, longevity, and cardiovascular disease prevention.

## History & Origins

### The Seven Countries Study (1958–Present)

The Mediterranean diet's scientific origins trace to **Ancel Keys**, an American physiologist who observed during post-WWII Europe that populations in southern Italy and Greece had remarkably low rates of heart disease despite a relatively high-fat diet. Keys launched the **Seven Countries Study** in 1958 — a landmark prospective cohort study following ~12,763 men across the US, Finland, Netherlands, Italy, former Yugoslavia, Greece, and Japan. It was the first to systematically compare diets and heart disease rates across cultures.

The study found that saturated fat intake was strongly correlated with coronary heart disease mortality, and that the Mediterranean dietary pattern was associated with the lowest cardiovascular mortality.

> **Key reference:** Keys A. *Seven Countries: A Multivariate Analysis of Death and Coronary Heart Disease.* Harvard University Press, 1980.

### Ancel Keys & the "Mediterranean Diet" Concept

Keys and his wife Margaret popularized the concept through their 1975 book *"Eat Well and Stay Well, the Mediterranean Way."* However, the term "Mediterranean diet" as a formal dietary construct was further developed through:

- **Walter Willett** (Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health), who formalized the modern Mediterranean diet pyramid in the 1990s
- **Oldways Preservation & Exchange Trust**, which, in collaboration with the Harvard School of Public Health and the WHO, published the **Mediterranean Diet Pyramid** in 1993
- **UNESCO**, which inscribed the Mediterranean diet on the **Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity** in 2010 (revised 2013)

### The Traditional Mediterranean Food Pattern

The "classic" Mediterranean diet as studied in research refers to the dietary pattern of Crete, much of the rest of Greece, and southern Italy circa 1960, when:
- Total dietary fat was ~40% of calories (but mostly from olive oil — monounsaturated fat)
- Saturated fat was <8% of calories
- The diet was largely plant-based with fish as the primary animal protein
- Physical activity was high (agrarian lifestyle)
- Rates of chronic disease were among the lowest in the world at that time

## Core Philosophy

The Mediterranean diet is fundamentally a **whole-food, plant-forward, balanced dietary pattern**. Unlike many modern diets, it is not defined by macronutrient ratios or rigid food restrictions. Instead, it is defined by:

1. **Food quality over macronutrient quantity** — Whole, minimally processed foods are emphasized regardless of their macronutrient composition.
2. **Plant-forward, not plant-exclusive** — Plants form the base of every meal, but animal foods (especially fish) are included in moderation.
3. **Fat is not the enemy** — The right types of fat (olive oil, nuts, fatty fish) are central, not restricted.
4. **Pleasure and social connection** — Meals are meant to be enjoyed with others, eaten slowly, and accompanied by moderate wine.
5. **Seasonal, local eating** — Traditional emphasis on eating what is fresh and locally available.
6. **Lifestyle integration** — The diet is part of a broader Mediterranean lifestyle that includes physical activity, rest (siesta), and social engagement.

## Key Components (The Mediterranean Diet Pyramid)

### Base — Eat at Every Meal
- **Whole grains:** bread, pasta, rice, couscous, bulgur, farro, oats
- **Vegetables:** all varieties, especially leafy greens, tomatoes, peppers, eggplant
- **Fruits:** all varieties, especially seasonal and local
- **Extra virgin olive oil:** the primary fat, used liberally
- **Herbs and spices:** garlic, basil, oregano, rosemary, thyme, turmeric (reduce salt)
- **Nuts and seeds:** almonds, walnuts, pine nuts, sesame seeds, flaxseeds
- **Water:** primary beverage

### Middle — Eat Daily to Weekly
- **Legumes and beans:** lentils, chickpeas, white beans, black beans (at least 2x/week)
- **Fish and seafood:** at least 2x/week, especially fatty fish (sardines, salmon, mackerel)
- **Poultry:** moderate portions (weekly)
- **Eggs:** moderate (up to 4 per week)
- **Dairy:** cheese and yogurt, moderate amounts (daily)
- **Olives:** as a snack or condiment

### Top — Eat Occasionally (in small amounts)
- **Red meat:** a few times per month
- **Processed meats:** rarely
- **Sweets and desserts:** occasionally, in small portions (fruit is the typical dessert)

### With Meals (Optional)
- **Wine:** 1 glass/day for women, 1–2 for men, with meals and in moderation

### Lifestyle Components
- Regular physical activity
- Adequate rest
- Meals shared with family/friends

## Variations & Sub-Types

### Geographic Variations
The Mediterranean basin is diverse, and the "Mediterranean diet" varies by region:

| Region | Distinctive Features |
|--------|---------------------|
| **Greek/Cretan** | Heaviest in olive oil; more wild greens; lamb occasionally |
| **Southern Italian** | Pasta-centric; tomato-based sauces; more bread |
| **Spanish** | More seafood; jamón (cured ham); gazpacho; more eggs |
| **Levantine/North African** | Couscous; chickpeas; more spices (cumin, coriander, cinnamon); tagines |
| **French (Niçoise)** | More vegetables (ratatouille); salade niçoise; less bread |

### Modern Adaptations
- **Green Mediterranean / MED-LC:** A variant higher in plant protein (Mankai duckweed, walnuts, green tea) and lower in red/processed meat. Studied in the DIRECT-PLUS trial.
- **MIND Diet:** A hybrid of Mediterranean + DASH optimized for brain health (see diet 11).
- **Pescatarian-Mediterranean:** The Mediterranean diet with fish as the ONLY animal protein (no meat or poultry).

## Who It's For

The Mediterranean diet is appropriate for **nearly the entire general population**. It is:

- ✅ **Endorsed by:** the World Health Organization, the American Heart Association, the Dietary Guidelines for Americans (2020–2025), the European Society of Cardiology, the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health, and U.S. News & World Report (#1 Best Diet for 7 consecutive years).
- ✅ **Suitable for:** adults, elderly, children, pregnant women (with minor modifications), people with cardiovascular disease, diabetes, metabolic syndrome, and those seeking general health optimization.
- ⚠️ **Caution for:** people with specific food allergies to core components (e.g., nut allergy, fish allergy), those requiring low-FODMAP diets (many Mediterranean staples are high FODMAP), and those with severe sodium restrictions (though the diet is moderate-sodium by default).
- ❌ **Not ideal for:** people who cannot access or afford fresh produce, fish, and olive oil (cost/accessibility is the main barrier).
