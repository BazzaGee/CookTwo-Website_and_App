# McDougall Diet / Starch Solution — Science & Evidence

> **Evidence Grade: C–D (Limited)** — The McDougall/Starch Solution diet has very little direct clinical research on its specific protocol. Evidence supporting its approach comes primarily from: (1) a small number of studies by McDougall himself, (2) closely analogous very low-fat plant-based diet studies (Ornish, Esselstyn, Pritikin), (3) meta-analyses of vegetarian/vegan diets, and (4) observational data on traditional starch-based populations (Okinawa, rural China). No large-scale RCT of the McDougall protocol exists.

---

## Evidence Grading

| Domain | Grade | Basis |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Weight loss | **C** | McDougall program observational data; vegan diet meta-analyses (indirect) |
| Cardiovascular risk factor improvement | **C** | Ornish Lifestyle Heart Trial (analogous); vegan diet meta-analyses |
| Coronary artery disease reversal | **B** | Ornish and Esselstyn trials (similar protocols, not McDougall-specific) |
| Type 2 diabetes management | **C** | Barnard vegan diet RCT; vegan diet meta-analyses (indirect) |
| Multiple sclerosis | **D** | Single small uncontrolled study by McDougall |
| Cancer prevention | **D** | Indirect evidence from plant-based diet literature; no direct trials |
| Longevity / all-cause mortality | **D** | Okinawan data (indirect); no direct McDougall mortality data |
| Inflammatory bowel disease / RA | **D** | Anecdotal and small observational reports only |

## Landmark Studies

> **Important caveat:** The studies below are the closest scientific evidence available. **None is a direct large-scale RCT of the McDougall/Starch Solution protocol.** They are included because they test dietary approaches that are nearly identical to McDougall's recommendations (very low fat, plant-based, starch-centered). The degree to which they generalize to McDougall's specific protocol is a matter of interpretation.

### 1. The Ornish Lifestyle Heart Trial

**The closest analog to the McDougall approach with the strongest evidence for heart disease reversal.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Randomized, controlled trial (with 5-year follow-up) |
| **Participants** | 48 patients with moderate to severe coronary heart disease |
| **Duration** | 1 year (primary), 5 years (extended follow-up) |
| **Intervention** | (1) Intensive lifestyle: very low-fat vegetarian diet (~10% fat), smoking cessation, stress management, moderate exercise, group support; (2) Usual care control |
| **Primary outcome** | Coronary artery atherosclerosis (quantitative angiography) |
| **Result** | **Regression of coronary atherosclerosis** in the intervention group (average diameter stenosis decreased from 40% to 37.8%) vs. **progression** in controls (43% to 46.7%). At 5 years, intervention group had 50% fewer cardiac events. |

> **Citation:** Ornish D, Scherwitz LW, Billings JH, et al. "Intensive Lifestyle Changes for Reversal of Coronary Heart Disease." *JAMA*. 1998;280(23):2001-2007.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.280.23.2001
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9863851/

> **5-year follow-up:** Ornish D, et al. "Intensive lifestyle changes for reversal of coronary heart disease: five-year follow-up of the Lifestyle Heart Trial." *JAMA*. 1998;280(23):2001-2007.

**Significance for McDougall:** The Ornish diet is a ~10% fat vegetarian diet — very close to McDougall's fat target. This trial is the best available evidence that very low-fat plant-based diets can reverse coronary artery disease. However, the Ornish intervention included multiple components (exercise, stress management, group support), making it impossible to attribute effects solely to diet.

> **Note:** The Ornish Program became the **first lifestyle intervention covered by Medicare** for heart disease reversal (2011), lending it significant credibility.

### 2. Esselstyn's Coronary Artery Disease Reversal Study

**The most direct evidence that an oil-free, plant-based diet can arrest and reverse heart disease.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Uncontrolled, prospective, before-after study (no control group) |
| **Participants** | 198 patients with established cardiovascular disease |
| **Duration** | Mean 3.7 years of follow-up |
| **Intervention** | Plant-based diet with **no added oils** (similar to McDougall's fat restriction) and no animal products; statins as needed to achieve LDL <50 mg/dL |
| **Primary outcome** | Major cardiac events (MI, stroke, death, revascularization) |
| **Result** | **99.4% of adherent patients had no further cardiac events.** In the 177 adherent patients: 1 stroke (0.6%). In 21 non-adherent patients: 13 major events (62%). LDL cholesterol reduced from ~120 to ~60 mg/dL. Angiographic evidence of disease regression in multiple cases. |

> **Citation:** Esselstyn CB Jr, Gendy G, Doyle J, Golubic M, Roizen MF. "A way to reverse CAD?" *The Journal of Family Practice*. 2014;63(7):356-364b.
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25198208/

**Significance for McDougall:** Esselstyn's diet is essentially identical to McDougall's in its exclusion of all animal products and all added oils. This is the strongest (though uncontrolled) evidence for the very low-fat plant-based approach to heart disease. Its limitations include: no control group, self-selection bias (only motivated patients adhered), and the combination of diet with aggressive statin therapy.

### 3. McDougall's Multiple Sclerosis Study

**McDougall's own published research — a small, uncontrolled study.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Uncontrolled, before-after intervention study |
| **Participants** | 24 participants with relapsing-remitting MS |
| **Duration** | 12 months |
| **Intervention** | Very low-fat (~10% fat) vegan diet (McDougall protocol) |
| **Result** | Self-reported improvement in fatigue and quality of life; no significant change in MRI lesion counts or EDSS disability scores. Most participants lost weight. |

> **Citation:** McDougall J, Bruce B, Spiller G, et al. "Effects of a very low-fat vegan diet in subjects with multiple sclerosis." Presented at the **Second International Symposium on the Role of Soy in Preventing and Treating Chronic Disease**, Brussels, Belgium, September 15-18, 1996. Published in: *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 1999;69(2):425S.

**Significance:** This is one of the very few published studies directly testing the McDougall protocol. The results were modest — subjective symptom improvement but no objective disease improvement. The study was small and uncontrolled, limiting conclusions. Swank et al. had previously published a larger (though still observational) low-fat diet study in MS with more promising results.

> **Related (Swank study):** Swank RL, Dugan BB. "Effect of low saturated fat diet in early and late cases of multiple sclerosis." *Lancet*. 1990;336(8706):37-39.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/0140-6736(90)91574-G

### 4. The Okinawa Centenarian Study

**Population-level evidence that a starch-centered, very low-fat diet supports exceptional longevity.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Population-based, longitudinal cohort study |
| **Participants** | Elderly residents of Okinawa, Japan |
| **Duration** | 1976–present (Okinawa Centenarian Study began 1975) |
| **Key finding** | Okinawa historically had the world's highest concentration of centenarians. The traditional Okinawan diet (circa 1949) was approximately: **69% carbohydrates (mostly from sweet potatoes), 11% protein, 6% fat** — strikingly similar to McDougall's macro targets. Total calories were ~1,785/day (caloric restriction). |

> **Citation:** Willcox BJ, Willcox DC, Todoriki H, Fujiyoshi A, Yano K, He Q, Curb JD, Suzuki M. "Caloric restriction, the traditional Okinawan diet, and healthy aging: the diet of the world's longest-lived people, the Okinawan Japanese." *Journal of Gerontology Series A: Biological Sciences and Medical Sciences*. 2007;62(7):752-762.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/gerona/62.7.752
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17634302/

> **Book:** Willcox BJ, Willcox DC, Suzuki M. *The Okinawa Program.* Three Rivers Press, 2001.

**Significance for McDougall:** The traditional Okinawan diet is the best real-world example of McDougall's prescribed macronutrient profile: high-carb (~69%), very low-fat (~6%), starch-centered (sweet potatoes). Okinawans' exceptional longevity is the strongest population-level support for the Starch Solution. However, since 1970, Okinawans have shifted toward a more Westernized diet (more fat, more animal protein, more processed foods) with corresponding increases in chronic disease — and even McDougall acknowledges this makes the historical data less directly applicable to modern contexts.

### 5. Barnard's Plant-Based Diet and Type 2 Diabetes Trial

**RCT evidence that a low-fat vegan diet improves glycemic control in diabetes.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Randomized, controlled trial |
| **Participants** | 99 individuals with type 2 diabetes |
| **Duration** | 74 weeks |
| **Intervention** | (1) Low-fat vegan diet (no calorie restriction); (2) American Diabetes Association (ADA) conventional diabetes diet |
| **Primary outcome** | HbA1c reduction |
| **Result** | Vegan group: **HbA1c reduction of -0.40 percentage points** (significant); greater weight loss (-6.5 kg vs -3.1 kg at 74 weeks); greater reduction in LDL cholesterol. Both groups improved, but vegan group had larger reductions in medication use. |

> **Citation:** Barnard ND, Cohen J, Jenkins DJ, Turner-McGrievy G, Gloede L, Green A, Ferdowsian H. "A low-fat vegan diet and a conventional diabetes diet in the treatment of type 2 diabetes: a randomized, controlled, 74-wk clinical trial." *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2009;89(5):1588S-1596S.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3945/ajcn.2009.26736H
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19321571/

**Significance for McDougall:** Barnard's vegan diet was not specifically the McDougall protocol (it was more flexible on fat), but it was low-fat, vegan, and allowed unlimited intake — directly analogous. The results support the McDougall approach for diabetes management.

## Key Meta-Analyses

### Vegetarian/Vegan Diets and Weight Reduction

> **Citation:** Huang RY, Huang CC, Hu FB, Chavarro JE. "Vegetarian Diets and Weight Reduction: a Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials." *Journal of General Internal Medicine*. 2016;31(1):109-116.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-015-3390-7
>
> **Findings:** Vegetarian diets (including vegan) were associated with significantly greater weight loss than non-vegetarian diets. The weighted mean difference was **−2.02 kg** for vegetarian vs. non-vegetarian diets across 12 RCTs. Vegan diets showed the largest effects.

### Vegetarian Diets and Blood Pressure

> **Citation:** Yokoyama Y, Nishimura K, Barnard ND, et al. "Vegetarian diets and blood pressure: a meta-analysis." *JAMA Internal Medicine*. 2014;174(4):577-587.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2013.14547
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24566947/
>
> **Findings:** Consumption of vegetarian diets was associated with a significant reduction in mean systolic BP (**−4.8 mm Hg**) and diastolic BP (**−2.2 mm Hg**) compared with omnivorous diets across 32 observational studies and 7 RCTs. This reduction is comparable to or greater than the effect of many antihypertensive medications.

### Plant-Based Dietary Patterns and Incidence of Type 2 Diabetes

> **Citation:** Satija A, Bhupathiraju SN, Spiegelman D, et al. "Plant-Based Dietary Patterns and Incidence of Type 2 Diabetes: Results from Three Prospective Cohort Studies." *PLOS Medicine*. 2016;13(6):e1002039.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pmed.1002039
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27299701/
>
> **Findings:** In the Nurses' Health Study, NHS II, and Health Professionals Follow-up Study (~200,000 participants), adherence to a plant-based diet rich in whole grains, fruits, vegetables, nuts, and legumes was associated with a **34% lower risk of type 2 diabetes**. Conversely, a "plant-based" diet high in refined grains and sugary beverages showed no benefit.

### Plant-Based Diets and Cardiovascular Risk

> **Citation:** Wang F, Zheng J, Yang B, Dong J, Tong Q, Rimm EB, Li J, Wang L. "Plant-based diets and cardiovascular risk factors: A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials." *Nutrition, Metabolism and Cardiovascular Diseases*. 2023;33(11):2097-2112.
>
> **Findings:** Plant-based diets significantly reduced total cholesterol (**−0.34 mmol/L**), LDL cholesterol (**−0.28 mmol/L**), and triglycerides, while improving glycemic control.

## Mechanisms of Action

The McDougall/Starch Solution diet's proposed health effects are attributed to several mechanisms:

### 1. Endothelial Function Restoration
- **Near-zero saturated fat and cholesterol intake** dramatically lowers serum LDL, reducing the primary driver of endothelial dysfunction
- Plant-based diets improve flow-mediated dilation within weeks
- Esselstyn and Ornish demonstrated angiographic evidence of endothelial recovery

### 2. Insulin Sensitivity Improvement
- Very low-fat diets reduce intramyocellular lipid (fat stored within muscle cells), which is a major cause of insulin resistance
- Starches (especially whole-food starches) have a moderate glycemic index but high satiety, reducing overeating
- High-fiber intake slows glucose absorption and feeds beneficial gut bacteria

> **Citation:** Barnard ND, Cohen J, Jenkins DJ, et al. "A low-fat vegan diet improves glycemic control and cardiovascular risk factors in a randomized clinical trial in individuals with type 2 diabetes." *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2006;84(3):605-613.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/84.3.605

### 3. Anti-Inflammatory Effects
- Elimination of animal products removes the primary source of dietary arachidonic acid (a pro-inflammatory omega-6 fatty acid)
- High intake of whole-plant antioxidants reduces circulating inflammatory markers
- Low-fat diets reduce CRP and IL-6 in intervention studies

### 4. Satiety and Caloric Density
- Starches and vegetables have extremely low caloric density (potatoes: ~0.87 kcal/g; rice: ~1.3 kcal/g vs. oils: 9 kcal/g)
- This allows ad libitum eating without calorie counting while still producing a caloric deficit
- The McDougall Maximum Weight Loss version is essentially a volumetrics approach centered on starches

### 5. Gut Microbiome Shift
- Very high fiber intake from whole grains, beans, and vegetables promotes short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production
- SCFAs (butyrate, propionate, acetate) have anti-inflammatory, metabolic, and gut-barrier benefits
- Vegan diets have been shown to rapidly alter microbiome composition

### 6. Randle Cycle / Substrate Competition
- McDougall argues (based on Randle cycle physiology) that high fat intake directly impairs glucose metabolism at the cellular level
- Reducing dietary fat allows cells to preferentially use glucose, improving insulin sensitivity
- This mechanism is debated in the nutrition science community

## Comparison with Other Diets

| Comparison | McDougall vs. | Result |
|-----------|---------------|--------|
| vs. Mediterranean | **Opposite fat philosophy** — McDougall: ~10% fat, no oil; Mediterranean: ~35% fat, olive oil central. Mediterranean has far stronger evidence base (Grade A vs. C-D) |
| vs. Keto | **Direct opposites** — McDougall: 75% carb / 10% fat; Keto: 10% carb / 75% fat. Both claim weight loss success. McDougall better for LDL; keto better for triglycerides and HDL acutely |
| vs. Ornish | **Nearly identical** — both ~10% fat, plant-based. Ornish adds exercise + stress management + group support. Ornish has RCT evidence; McDougall does not |
| vs. Esselstyn/Engine 2 | **Nearly identical** — both oil-free plant-based. Esselstyn focuses on heart disease reversal; McDougall focuses on starches as the base |
| vs. General vegan | McDougall is more restrictive: no oil, limited nuts/seeds/avocado, starch-centered. A general vegan diet may include oils, processed vegan foods, and higher fat content |
| vs. Whole Food Plant-Based (Greger/Campbell) | Very similar. McDougall is more prescriptive about starches being the center and more restrictive on fat |

## Consensus Among Health Organizations

| Organization | Position |
|-------------|----------|
| **American Heart Association** | Has not specifically endorsed the McDougall diet; supports plant-based eating patterns generally but recommends moderate healthy fat intake |
| **American College of Lifestyle Medicine** | Broadly supports whole-food plant-based nutrition including very low-fat approaches |
| **American Diabetes Association** | Acknowledges plant-based eating patterns as an option for diabetes management (2019 Standards of Care); does not specifically endorse McDougall |
| **Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics** | Position paper (2016) states that appropriately planned vegetarian/vegan diets are healthful, nutritionally adequate, and may provide health benefits — but does not specifically endorse very low-fat vegan diets |
| **WHO** | Has not commented on the McDougall diet specifically |
| **Medicare (CMS)** | Covers the Ornish Program (similar but not identical) for intensive cardiac rehabilitation |

## Limitations & Caveats

1. **No large-scale RCT of the McDougall protocol exists.** All evidence is either from McDougall's own small, uncontrolled studies or from analogous diets (Ornish, Esselstyn, Barnard). The degree to which findings from these analogs apply to McDougall's specific protocol is uncertain.

2. **Very low fat may not be necessary for benefit.** Evidence from the Mediterranean diet (PREDIMED) shows that a ~35% fat diet with olive oil and nuts also dramatically reduces cardiovascular events. The claim that fat *must* be <10–15% for health is not supported by the totality of evidence.

3. **Sustainability concerns.** Long-term adherence rates to very low-fat diets are modest. Some people find the diet socially restrictive and difficult to maintain outside the home. McDougall's own program data suggest high dropout rates in non-residential settings.

4. **B12 deficiency is guaranteed without supplementation.** This is not unique to McDougall (all vegan diets require B12), but the diet's promoters sometimes underemphasize this critical point.

5. **Potential insufficiency of essential fatty acids.** With minimal nuts, seeds, and no fish, achieving adequate omega-3 (ALA, EPA, DHA) intake requires deliberate planning. McDougall recommends 1 tbsp ground flaxseed daily, but conversion of ALA to EPA/DHA is inefficient.

6. **The Randle cycle argument is oversimplified.** McDougall's claim that dietary fat directly blocks glucose metabolism (via the Randle cycle) is a real physiological phenomenon but has been challenged as an oversimplification of metabolic regulation.

7. **Confounding in population data.** Okinawans and other traditional populations had many health-promoting lifestyle factors beyond diet (high physical activity, strong social networks, caloric restriction, limited tobacco/alcohol). Attributing their longevity to starch alone is speculative.

8. **Social media vs. science.** McDougall has a large and passionate following online, but enthusiasm is not a substitute for controlled clinical trials.

## Emerging Research Areas

- **Starch-based diets and the gut microbiome:** How very-high-starch intake shapes microbial composition and SCFA production
- **Very low-fat diets and hormone regulation:** Effects on testosterone, estrogen, and thyroid function (concerns about low fat and hormonal health)
- **Starch-resistant carbohydrates and metabolic health:** The role of resistant starch (cooled potatoes, green bananas) in glycemic control
- **Individualization of plant-based diets:** Whether genetic or metabolic phenotypes predict who responds best to very low-fat vs. moderate-fat plant-based diets
