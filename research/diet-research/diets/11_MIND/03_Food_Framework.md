# MIND Diet — Food Framework

> Practical guide to what to eat, avoid, and limit on the MIND diet, including macronutrient targets, serving guidelines, and sample meal structures. Based on the MIND diet score developed by Martha Clare Morris et al. at Rush University.

---

## Macronutrient Targets

The MIND diet does not prescribe specific macronutrient ratios as strictly as DASH or ketogenic diets. It inherits moderate macro distributions from its Mediterranean and DASH parent diets, with emphasis on food quality over macro targets.

| Macronutrient | Target (% of total calories) | Notes |
|---------------|:----------------------------:|-------|
| **Carbohydrates** | ~45–55% | Primarily from whole grains, vegetables, berries, beans/legumes |
| **Protein** | ~15–20% | From fish, poultry, beans, nuts, moderate dairy |
| **Total fat** | ~30–35% | Primarily from olive oil and nuts |
| **Saturated fat** | <10% (ideally <7%) | Minimize via restricting butter, cheese, red meat |
| **Fiber** | 25–35 g/day | From whole grains, vegetables, legumes, berries |
| **Sodium** | <2,300 mg/day | Inherited from DASH parent |
| **Added sugar** | Minimize (<25 g/day) | Pastries/sweets limited to <5 servings/week |

> The MIND diet's power comes from **specific food choices** (berries over other fruit, leafy greens daily, olive oil exclusively, fish weekly) rather than macronutrient manipulation. If you hit the food targets, the macros largely take care of themselves.

## Foods to Eat Daily / Weekly (10 Brain-Healthy Groups)

### 1. Green Leafy Vegetables — ≥6 servings/week (ideally 1+/day)

**The single most important MIND food group.** Rush University research showed daily leafy greens were associated with cognitive benefit equivalent to being 11 years younger.

| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|
| Spinach (raw or cooked) | 1 cup raw / ½ cup cooked |
| Kale (all varieties) | 1 cup raw / ½ cup cooked |
| Collard greens | ½ cup cooked |
| Swiss chard | ½ cup cooked |
| Arugula | 1 cup raw |
| Romaine lettuce | 1 cup raw |
| Mixed salad greens / mesclun | 1 cup raw |
| Mustard greens, turnip greens | ½ cup cooked |
| Beet greens | ½ cup cooked |
| Watercress | 1 cup raw |

> **Nutrient highlights:** Folate, vitamin K, lutein, beta-carotene, nitrates. These nutrients are specifically concentrated in leafy greens and are essential for brain health (vitamin K for sphingolipid synthesis, folate for reducing homocysteine, lutein accumulates in brain tissue).

### 2. Other Vegetables — ≥1 serving/day

| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|
| Broccoli, cauliflower | ½ cup cooked |
| Carrots | ½ cup cooked / 1 cup raw |
| Tomatoes (all forms) | 1 medium / ½ cup |
| Bell peppers | 1 medium |
| Brussels sprouts | ½ cup cooked |
| Green beans | ½ cup cooked |
| Eggplant, zucchini, squash | ½ cup cooked |
| Onions, garlic, leeks | ½ cup / 1 clove garlic |
| Cabbage | ½ cup |
| Mushrooms | ½ cup cooked |
| Asparagus | ½ cup cooked |

### 3. Nuts — ≥5 servings/week

| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|
| Walnuts (highest omega-3 ALA) | 1 oz (~14 halves) |
| Almonds (highest vitamin E) | 1 oz (~23 nuts) |
| Pistachios | 1 oz (~49 nuts) |
| Pecans | 1 oz (~19 halves) |
| Hazelnuts | 1 oz (~21 nuts) |
| Mixed nuts (unsalted) | 1 oz |
| Peanut butter (natural) | 2 tbsp |
| Flaxseeds, chia seeds | 1 tbsp |

> **Vitamin E highlight:** Nuts are the best dietary source of vitamin E, a potent antioxidant that protects neuronal membranes from oxidative damage. Morris et al. found that vitamin E intake was one of the strongest individual nutrient predictors of cognitive preservation.

### 4. Berries — ≥2 servings/week

**The only fruit specifically featured in the MIND diet.** This is what distinguishes MIND from its parent diets.

| Food | Serving Size | Key Brain Compound |
|------|:------------:|-------------------|
| **Blueberries** | 1 cup fresh / ½ cup frozen | Anthocyanins — strongest evidence for cognition |
| **Strawberries** | 1 cup sliced | Anthocyanins, vitamin C, fisetin |
| Blackberries | 1 cup | Anthocyanins, ellagic acid |
| Raspberries | 1 cup | Anthocyanins, ellagic acid |
| Cranberries | ½ cup | Proanthocyanidins |
| Goji berries | ¼ cup dried | Zeaxanthin |
| Açaí berries | ½ cup | Extremely high anthocyanins |

> **Why berries specifically?** The Nurses' Health Study (Devore et al. 2012) found that women consuming ≥2 servings/week of strawberries and blueberries had cognitive function equivalent to being 2.5 years younger. Berries' anthocyanins cross the blood-brain barrier and accumulate in brain regions critical for memory (hippocampus). Animal studies show blueberry supplementation improves memory and reverses age-related neuronal signaling deficits.

> **Frozen berries are fine:** Frozen berries retain their anthocyanin content and are often more affordable and convenient.

### 5. Beans and Legumes — ≥3 meals/week

| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|
| Lentils (all varieties) | ½ cup cooked |
| Chickpeas (garbanzo beans) | ½ cup cooked |
| Black beans | ½ cup cooked |
| Kidney beans | ½ cup cooked |
| Navy beans, cannellini beans | ½ cup cooked |
| Split peas | ½ cup cooked |
| Edamame (soybeans) | ½ cup |
| Hummus | ¼ cup |

> **Brain benefit:** Beans provide stable blood sugar (low glycemic index), B vitamins (especially folate), and fiber — all of which support brain energy metabolism and reduce neuroinflammation. The stable glucose supply is important because the brain relies almost exclusively on glucose for energy.

### 6. Whole Grains — ≥3 servings/day

| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|
| 100% whole wheat bread | 1 slice |
| Brown rice, wild rice | ½ cup cooked |
| Oats (steel-cut or rolled) | ½ cup cooked |
| Quinoa | ½ cup cooked |
| Barley | ½ cup cooked |
| Bulgur, farro | ½ cup cooked |
| Whole wheat pasta | ½ cup cooked |
| Whole grain cereal (high fiber, low sugar) | ¾–1 cup |
| Popcorn (air-popped, lightly salted) | 3 cups |
| Corn tortillas (whole grain) | 1 small |

### 7. Fish — ≥1 meal/week (aim for 2+)

| Food | Omega-3 (DHA/EPA) Content | Serving Size |
|------|:-------------------------:|:------------:|
| Salmon | ★★★ Very high | 3–4 oz |
| Sardines, anchovies | ★★★ Very high | 3–4 oz |
| Mackerel, herring | ★★★ Very high | 3–4 oz |
| Trout | ★★☆ High | 3–4 oz |
| Tuna (fresh or canned) | ★★☆ High | 3–4 oz |
| Cod, halibut | ★☆☆ Moderate | 3–4 oz |

> **DHA is critical:** DHA makes up ~60% of the polyunsaturated fatty acids in neuronal membranes. Low DHA is associated with cognitive decline. Fatty fish is the best dietary source of preformed DHA/EPA.

### 8. Poultry — ≥2 meals/week

| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|
| Skinless chicken breast | 3 oz |
| Skinless turkey breast | 3 oz |
| Ground turkey (lean) | 3 oz |
| Chicken thigh (skinless) | 3 oz |

> Poultry replaces red meat as the primary animal protein in MIND — it provides protein and B vitamins without the saturated fat and TMAO-production associated with red meat.

### 9. Olive Oil — Use as primary cooking oil

- Choose **extra virgin olive oil** for maximum polyphenol content (oleocanthal, oleuropein)
- Use for cooking, salad dressings, and finishing
- The MIND score gives full points if olive oil is used as the **primary** cooking oil

> **Oleocanthal:** This olive oil compound has been shown to have ibuprofen-like anti-inflammatory activity and to enhance clearance of amyloid-beta in laboratory studies.

### 10. Wine — 1 glass/day (optional)

| Population | Amount |
|------------|--------|
| Women | 1 glass/day (120–150ml) |
| Men | 1–2 glasses/day (120–150ml each) |
| Non-drinkers | **Do not start drinking for health reasons** |

> **Caveat:** Recent WHO guidance (2023) states that no level of alcohol consumption is completely safe for cancer risk. The MIND diet includes wine based on observational associations with cognitive benefit, but this should be weighed against alcohol risks. Wine is **optional** — non-drinkers should not begin drinking.

## Foods to Limit (5 Brain-Harmful Groups)

### 1. Red Meat — <4 servings/week

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|----------------|
| Beef, pork, lamb | Limit to <4 meals/week. Optimal: ≤1–2 meals/week. Small portions (3 oz). |

> Red meat is associated with cognitive decline via multiple mechanisms: saturated fat promotes atherosclerosis (reducing cerebral blood flow), heme iron may promote oxidative stress, and gut-microbial metabolism produces TMAO (linked to vascular disease).

### 2. Butter and Stick Margarine — <1 tbsp/day

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|----------------|
| Butter | <1 tbsp/day (ideally minimize entirely) |
| Stick margarine | Avoid entirely (trans fat content) |

> Replace butter with olive oil. The MIND score specifically tracks butter/margarine intake.

### 3. Cheese — <1 serving/week

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|----------------|
| All cheeses | Limit to <1 serving/week. This is stricter than the Mediterranean diet. |

> Cheese restriction is one of MIND's distinguishing features. The evidence linking cheese specifically to cognitive decline comes from observational data. This is more restrictive than the Mediterranean diet (which allows daily cheese).

### 4. Pastries and Sweets — <5 servings/week

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|----------------|
| Cookies, cakes, donuts, pastries | Limit to <5 servings/week |
| Candy, chocolate (non-dark) | Minimize |
| Ice cream, pudding | Small amounts occasionally |
| Sugary cereals | Replace with whole grain options |

### 5. Fried Food and Fast Food — <1 meal/week

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|----------------|
| Fried foods (french fries, fried chicken, etc.) | Limit to <1 meal/week (ideally avoid) |
| Fast food | Limit to <1 meal/week |

> Fried and fast foods contain trans fats (in some countries), oxidized lipids, advanced glycation end products (AGEs), and high levels of sodium and saturated fat — all neurotoxic and pro-inflammatory.

## Foods NOT Specifically Emphasized (Neutral in MIND)

These foods are not part of the MIND score and can be included in moderate amounts without penalty:

| Food | MIND Position | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| **Non-berry fruits** (apples, bananas, oranges, etc.) | Neutral | Not specifically scored; can be eaten but don't earn MIND points. The Mediterranean diet emphasizes these; MIND does not because berries have the strongest cognitive evidence. |
| **Eggs** | Neutral | Not scored in MIND; included in parent diets |
| **Low-fat dairy / milk / yogurt** | Neutral | Not specifically scored (DASH emphasizes these; MIND does not) |
| **Coffee / tea** | Neutral | Not scored in MIND; emerging evidence supports coffee/tea for cognition |
| **Dark chocolate** | Neutral | Not scored; some evidence for cocoa flavanols and cognition |

## Daily Eating Pattern (Sample Day)

### Breakfast
- Bowl of steel-cut oats with ½ cup blueberries (fresh or frozen), 2 tbsp chopped walnuts, and a drizzle of olive oil or honey
- *Or:* Green smoothie: 1 cup spinach, ½ cup strawberries, ½ banana, 1 tbsp flaxseed, unsweetened almond milk
- Coffee or green tea (unsweetened)

### Lunch
- Large mixed salad: 2 cups leafy greens (spinach, arugula, kale), ½ cup cherry tomatoes, ¼ cup chickpeas, 2 tbsp walnuts
- Dressing: 1 tbsp extra virgin olive oil + balsamic vinegar + herbs
- 3 oz grilled chicken breast (skinless)
- 1 slice 100% whole wheat bread
- Sparkling water with lime

### Snack (optional)
- 1 cup fresh berries (raspberries or blackberries)
- 1 oz unsalted mixed nuts
- *Or:* ½ cup hummus with raw vegetables (carrots, peppers, cucumber)

### Dinner
- 3–4 oz baked salmon with lemon and herbs
- ½ cup quinoa
- ½ cup steamed broccoli
- Side salad with mixed greens and olive oil dressing
- 1 glass red wine (optional, with meal)

### Dessert (optional)
- ½ cup berries (different from earlier in the day)
- *Or occasionally:* Small piece of dark chocolate (70%+ cocoa)

## Weekly Meal Structure (MIND-Style)

| Day | Lunch | Dinner |
|-----|-------|--------|
| Monday | Lentil and spinach soup, whole grain bread, side salad | Baked salmon with quinoa and roasted Brussels sprouts |
| Tuesday | Kale salad with chickpeas, walnuts, and olive oil dressing | Grilled chicken breast with brown rice and green beans |
| Wednesday | Mixed greens salad with tuna, tomatoes, olive oil | Black bean and vegetable chili with whole grain bread |
| Thursday | Spinach and strawberry salad with grilled chicken | Baked cod with roasted sweet potato and broccoli |
| Friday | Chickpea and vegetable stir-fry with brown rice | Turkey breast with quinoa and roasted carrots |
| Saturday | Lentil salad with olive oil and herbs, whole grain bread | Pasta puttanesca (whole wheat pasta, tomatoes, olives, olive oil) |
| Sunday | Large leafy green salad with walnuts, berries, and grilled chicken | Baked fish (salmon or trout) with brown rice and sautéed kale |

> **Note:** Every day includes leafy greens (at least one serving), berries (at least 2–3 times across the week), fish (at least 1–2 times/week), beans/legumes (at least 3 meals/week), whole grains (3+ servings/day), and nuts (5+ servings/week). Olive oil is used for all cooking and dressing.

## The MIND Diet Score (15 Points)

The validated scoring system used in MIND diet research. Each brain-healthy food group earns up to 1 point for meeting the target; each brain-harmful food group earns up to 1 point for staying under the limit.

### Brain-Healthy Components (max 10 points)

| Component | Criterion for 1 Point |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Green leafy vegetables | ≥6 servings/week |
| Other vegetables | ≥1 serving/day |
| Berries | ≥2 servings/week |
| Nuts | ≥5 servings/week |
| Olive oil | Primary cooking oil |
| Whole grains | ≥3 servings/day |
| Fish | ≥1 meal/week |
| Beans | ≥3 meals/week |
| Poultry | ≥2 meals/week |
| Wine | 1 glass/day |

### Brain-Harmful Components (max 5 points — earned by staying UNDER limits)

| Component | Criterion for 1 Point |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Red meat | <4 servings/week |
| Butter/margarine | <1 tbsp/day |
| Cheese | <1 serving/week |
| Pastries/sweets | <5 servings/week |
| Fried/fast food | <1 meal/week |

### Scoring Interpretation

| Score | Adherence Level | Alzheimer's Risk Reduction |
|:-----:|:----------------|:-------------------------:|
| 12–15 | **High** | **~53%** |
| 8.5–11.5 | **Moderate-High** | **~35–53%** |
| 6.5–8 | **Moderate** | **~35%** |
| <6.5 | **Low** | Baseline |

> **Citation:** Morris MC, Tangney CC, Wang Y, Sacks FM, Barnes LL, Bennett DA. "MIND diet associated with reduced incidence of Alzheimer's disease." *Alzheimer's & Dementia*. 2015;11(9):1007-1014.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jalz.2014.11.009

## Key Nutritional Considerations

### Nutrients Adequately Provided
- **Vitamin K:** Very high from daily leafy greens (essential for brain sphingolipid synthesis)
- **Folate:** High from leafy greens and legumes (reduces homocysteine, a dementia risk factor)
- **Vitamin E:** High from nuts, seeds, and whole grains (protects neuronal membranes)
- **Anthocyanins:** High from berries (cross blood-brain barrier, antioxidant)
- **DHA/EPA:** From weekly fish (structural brain component)
- **Polyphenols:** From olive oil, berries, vegetables (anti-inflammatory, antioxidant)
- **Fiber:** High from whole grains, beans, vegetables (gut-brain axis, stable blood sugar)
- **Magnesium:** From nuts, whole grains, leafy greens

### Nutrients to Monitor
- **Vitamin D:** Moderate risk — limited food sources. Include fatty fish, consider supplementation.
- **B12:** Adequate from fish, poultry. Vegans need supplementation.
- **Iron:** Adequate from poultry, beans, whole grains. Women of reproductive age should monitor.
- **Calcium:** May be lower than in DASH — MIND restricts cheese and doesn't emphasize dairy. Ensure adequate intake from yogurt (if included), fortified foods, leafy greens.
- **Iodine:** If fish/seafood intake is minimal, use iodized salt in moderation.
