# Carnivore Diet — Food Framework

> Practical guide to what to eat and avoid on the carnivore diet. Note: this is a description of the diet as practiced, **not a recommendation**. The carnivore diet has insufficient evidence for safety or efficacy, and no major health organization endorses it.

---

## Macronutrient Targets

### Typical Carnivore Diet Macros

The carnivore diet has no formal macronutrient prescription — practitioners eat animal foods to satiety. However, the resulting macronutrient profile typically falls in the following ranges:

| Macronutrient | Typical Range (% of calories) | Grams (on 2,000 cal diet) | Notes |
|---------------|:-----------------------------:|:-------------------------:|-------|
| **Carbohydrates** | 0–2% | **0–10g** | Near-zero; only trace carbs in meat, eggs, dairy |
| **Protein** | 20–35% | 100–175g | High; driven by meat consumption |
| **Fat** | 65–80% | 145–178g | Very high; primary energy source |
| **Fiber** | **0g** | **0g** | Zero — no plant foods consumed |
| **Sodium** | Variable | 3,000–6,000+ mg | Depends on salt use; typically liberal |

> **No calorie counting:** Most carnivore practitioners do not count calories or track macros. They eat meat to satiety. The high protein and fat content, combined with ketosis, naturally suppresses appetite for many (an effect shared with the ketogenic diet).

### Sub-Variant Macronutrient Profiles

| Variant | Protein | Fat | Notes |
|---------|:-------:|:---:|-------|
| **"Beef, Salt, Water" (Baker)** | ~25-30% | ~70-75% | High fat from fatty beef cuts |
| **Nose-to-Tail (Saladino original)** | ~20-25% | ~75-80% | More fat from organs + marrow |
| **PKD (Paleomedicina, 2:1 ratio)** | ~30% | ~70% | 2:1 fat:protein by weight — more prescribed |
| **Lean meat emphasis** | ~35-45% | ~55-65% | Risk of "rabbit starvation" (protein toxicity) if fat is too low |

> **⚠️ "Rabbit starvation" warning:** A diet of exclusively lean protein (without adequate fat) can cause nausea, diarrhea, and potentially death within weeks — historically observed in explorers who ate only lean rabbit meat. Fat intake must be adequate on a carnivore diet. Choose fatty cuts (ribeye, 80/20 ground beef, pork belly) or add tallow/butter.

## Foods to Eat (The Entire Diet for Strict Practitioners)

### Beef (The Foundation for Most Carnivores)

| Cut | Fat Content | Notes |
|-----|:-----------:|-------|
| Ribeye steak | High (marbled) | The "gold standard" carnivore cut — high fat, great taste |
| Ground beef (70-80% lean) | High | Affordable, versatile |
| Brisket | High | Slow-cooked; very fatty |
| Short ribs | High | Fatty, flavorful |
| Chuck roast | Moderate-High | Good for slow cooking |
| New York strip | Moderate | Good balance |
| Sirloin | Moderate | Leaner |
| Filet mignon / tenderloin | Low | Very lean — pair with added fat |
| Beef tallow (rendered fat) | 100% | Used for cooking or added to lean cuts |

### Other Red Meat

| Food | Fat Content | Notes |
|------|:-----------:|-------|
| Lamb (chops, leg, shoulder) | Moderate-High | Nutrient-dense; good alternative to beef |
| Pork (chops, belly, ribs) | Variable | Pork belly is very high fat |
| Bacon (no sugar added) | High | Very popular; check for sugar in cure |
| Venison, elk, bison | Variable | Wild game; typically leaner — add fat |

### Poultry

| Food | Fat Content | Notes |
|------|:-----------:|-------|
| Chicken thighs (with skin) | Moderate-High | Dark meat with skin is preferred |
| Chicken wings | Moderate | Often eaten with skin |
| Whole chicken (roasted) | Moderate | Eat the skin for fat |
| Duck | High | Naturally fatty |
| Turkey (dark meat) | Moderate | Leaner than chicken thighs |

> **Note:** Some strict carnivore practitioners (particularly those following ruminant-only protocols) avoid poultry and pork, believing ruminant meat (beef, lamb) is superior.

### Fish and Seafood

| Food | Fat Content | Notes |
|------|:-----------:|-------|
| Salmon (especially wild) | ★★★ Very high | Rich in omega-3 EPA/DHA |
| Mackerel | ★★★ Very high | Excellent omega-3 source |
| Sardines (canned in oil) | ★★★ Very high | Convenient, affordable |
| Herring | ★★★ Very high | |
| Tuna | ★☆☆ Moderate | Leaner; high mercury if eaten in large amounts |
| Cod, halibut | ★☆☆ Low | Very lean — add butter/tallow |
| Shrimp | ★☆☆ Low | |
| Oysters | Moderate | Very high in zinc and B12 |
| Mussels, clams | Moderate | Higher carb than most seafood (~4-7g/100g) |

### Organ Meats (Especially in Nose-to-Tail Variants)

> Organ meats are the most nutrient-dense foods available and address several potential deficiencies (vitamin A, folate, copper, vitamin C in trace amounts).

| Organ | Key Nutrients | Notes |
|-------|---------------|-------|
| **Liver** (beef, chicken, lamb) | Vitamin A (retinol), folate, B12, copper, iron, choline, trace vitamin C | The most nutrient-dense food; 100-200g/week is common |
| **Heart** | CoQ10, B vitamins, iron, zinc | Muscle-like texture |
| **Kidney** | Selenium, B12, riboflavin | Strong flavor |
| **Brain** | DHA, choline, B12 | Rarely available; food safety considerations |
| **Bone marrow** | Fat, CLA, vitamins K2, minerals | Delicious, very high fat |
| **Sweetbreads (thymus/pancreas)** | Vitamin C (trace), zinc, B vitamins | Delicacy |
| **Bone broth** | Collagen, gelatin, minerals (calcium, magnesium) | Not food per se, but commonly consumed |

### Eggs

| Food | Serving Size | Notes |
|------|:------------:|-------|
| Whole eggs | 2–8 per day | Nutrient-dense; include yolk (contains most nutrients) |
| Egg yolks | As desired | Choline, vitamin D, lutein, cholesterol |

### Animal Fats

| Fat | Use |
|-----|-----|
| **Beef tallow** | Primary cooking fat; rendered from suet |
| **Lamb fat** | Cooking |
| **Pork lard** | Cooking (if pork is included) |
| **Duck fat** | Cooking |
| **Butter** | Cooking, added to steak/vegetables; included by most non-strict carnivores |
| **Ghee** | Clarified butter; preferred by some who avoid milk proteins |
| **Bone marrow** | Eaten directly; very nutrient-dense fat |

### Optional Dairy (Varies by Practitioner)

| Food | Included By | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| **Butter** | Most carnivores | High fat, minimal lactose/protein |
| **Ghee** | Most carnivores | Clarified — virtually no lactose/casein |
| **Hard cheese** (cheddar, parmesan) | Many | Contains minimal lactose; higher in protein |
| **Soft cheese** (cream cheese, brie) | Some | |
| **Heavy cream** | Some | High fat, low lactose |
| **Sour cream** | Some | |
| **Milk** | Very few | Contains lactose (sugar) — most strict carnivores avoid |

> **Dairy controversy:** Some carnivores (especially Baker) avoid all dairy. Others include butter and cheese liberally. Dairy-sensitive individuals typically exclude it. The PKD protocol excludes all dairy.

### Flavoring (Minimal)

| Item | Status |
|------|--------|
| **Salt** (sea salt, Himalayan, kosher) | Included by almost all carnivores; used liberally |
| **Pepper** | Excluded by strict (it's a plant); included by some |
| **Herbs/spices** | Excluded by strict (plant-derived); included by "lenient" carnivores |
| **Hot sauce** | Excluded by strict (plant-derived); included by some |
| **Mustard** | Excluded by strict (contains seeds); included by some |
| **Soy sauce / tamari** | Excluded by strict (soy/fermented grain); included by some |

> **Strict carnivore purists** use only salt. No spices, no herbs, no condiments — just meat and salt.

### Beverages

| Beverage | Status |
|----------|--------|
| **Water** | Universal — the only beverage for strict practitioners |
| **Bone broth** | Widely included; valued for electrolytes and collagen |
| **Coffee** | Debated — excluded by strict (coffee is a plant extract); included by many |
| **Tea** | Excluded by strict (plant-derived); included by some |
| **Sparkling water** | Generally included |
| **Alcohol** | Excluded by strict; some include occasional dry wine or spirits |

|---------------|----------|---------------------|
| **All vegetables** | Leafy greens, broccoli, cauliflower, potatoes, sweet potatoes, carrots, tomatoes, peppers, onions, garlic, mushrooms, all squash | Plant foods excluded entirely |
| **All fruits** | Apples, bananas, berries, citrus, tropical fruit, dried fruit | Plant foods excluded entirely |
| **All grains** | Wheat, rice, oats, corn, barley, quinoa, millet | Plant foods excluded entirely |
| **All legumes** | Beans, lentils, chickpeas, peanuts, peas | Plant foods excluded entirely |
| **All nuts and seeds** | Almonds, walnuts, chia, flax, sunflower, pumpkin, etc. | Plant foods excluded entirely |
| **All plant oils** | Olive oil, coconut oil, avocado oil, all seed oils (canola, soybean, sunflower) | Plant-derived; use animal fats instead |
| **All sugar** | White sugar, honey, maple syrup, agave | Plant-derived; zero carb |
| **All plant-based sweeteners** | Stevia, monk fruit, erythritol | Plant-derived |

## Daily Eating Pattern (Sample Day — "Beef, Salt, Water" Protocol)

### Pattern 1: Two Meals Per Day (Common)

**Meal 1 (Late Morning / Lunch)**
- 2-3 ribeye steaks (225-340g each), cooked in tallow or butter
- Salted to taste
- Water or bone broth

**Meal 2 (Dinner)**
- 2-3 ribeye steaks OR ground beef (680-900g) with butter
- OR: salmon fillets (if including fish) with butter
- Salted to taste
- Water

### Pattern 2: Three Meals Per Day (Less Common)

**Breakfast**
- 4-6 eggs scrambled in butter
- 4-6 slices of bacon
- OR: ground beef (340g) with butter

**Lunch**
- Chicken thighs with skin (340-450g) OR pork chops
- Bone broth

**Dinner**
- Ribeye steak (340g) or brisket
- Optionally: liver (100-150g) if including organ meats

### Pattern 3: Nose-to-Tail (Saladino-Influenced)

**Meal 1**
- Ribeye steak (340g)
- Beef liver (100g) — 2-3x per week
- Bone marrow (50-100g)

**Meal 2**
- Ground beef (450g, 80/20) cooked in tallow
- Sardines (1 tin) or salmon (if including fish)

> **Note on meal frequency:** Many carnivore practitioners naturally adopt **one or two meals per day** (OMAD or TMAD), as the high satiety of meat and ketosis reduces hunger frequency. This is not required but common. Some eat 3-4 times per day, especially during the adaptation period.

## Transition and Adaptation Phase

### Weeks 1–4: "Carnivore Adaptation" / "Withdrawal"

Similar to keto adaptation but often more intense:

| Symptom | Duration | Cause |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Fatigue, low energy | 1–3 weeks | Transitioning from glucose to fat/ketone metabolism |
| Headache | 3–10 days | Electrolyte loss (sodium, potassium, magnesium) |
| Diarrhea or loose stools | 1–4 weeks | Gallbladder adapting to high fat intake; gut microbiome shift |
| Nausea | 1–2 weeks | High fat intake; sometimes from excessive protein |
| Sugar/starch cravings | 1–3 weeks | Dopamine withdrawal from eliminated foods |
| Irritability | 1–2 weeks | Carb withdrawal |
| "Brain fog" | 1–2 weeks | Transition to ketones as brain fuel |
| Reduced athletic performance | 2–6 weeks | Glycogen depletion; adaptation needed |

**Mitigation strategies used by practitioners:**
- Increase **salt** intake (3,000–5,000mg sodium/day)
- Ensure adequate **fat** intake (not just lean protein)
- Drink **bone broth** for electrolytes
- Eat **more frequently** during adaptation
- Be patient — adaptation takes 2–6 weeks

### Months 1–3: Settling In
- Energy typically stabilizes or improves
- Digestive issues usually resolve (though some report persistent changes in bowel habits)
- Appetite often decreases (satiety from protein/fat + ketones)
- Weight changes stabilize

## Monitoring Recommendations (If Practicing the Diet)

> **Since no health organization endorses this diet, these monitoring recommendations are harm-reduction measures for those who choose to try it.**

| Test | Frequency | Why |
|------|-----------|-----|
| **Comprehensive lipid panel** (total chol, LDL, HDL, TG) | Baseline, 3 months, 6 months, annually | LDL likely to elevate significantly; cardiovascular risk unknown |
| **ApoB / LDL particle number** | Baseline, 3–6 months | More accurate cardiovascular risk marker than LDL alone |
| **HbA1c / fasting glucose** | Baseline, 3–6 months | Monitor glycemic status |
| **Complete blood count (CBC)** | Annually | General health |
| **Comprehensive metabolic panel** (liver, kidney) | Annually | Monitor kidney function (high protein concern) and liver |
| **Vitamin C level (plasma)** | Baseline, 6–12 months | Monitor for deficiency |
| **Folate and B12** | Annually | Folate may be low without organ meats/plants |
| **Vitamin D (25-OH)** | Annually | May be adequate from fatty fish/eggs, but monitor |
| **Ferritin / iron studies** | Annually | Iron overload possible with very high red meat intake |
| **Homocysteine** | Annually | May be elevated on high-meat diets |
| **hs-CRP** | Annually | Monitor inflammation |
| **Blood pressure** | Regularly | Monitor cardiovascular risk |
| **Colonoscopy** | Per guidelines (age 45+, or earlier if risk factors) | Colorectal cancer screening especially important given high red meat intake |

## Key Nutritional Considerations

### Nutrients Likely Adequate
- **Protein and essential amino acids:** Abundant
- **Fat and essential fatty acids:** Abundant (though omega-3 depends on fish intake)
- **B12, B6, riboflavin, niacin, thiamine:** Abundant from meat
- **Iron (heme):** Highly bioavailable
- **Zinc:** Abundant from red meat, oysters
- **Selenium:** From meat, fish
- **Vitamin A (retinol):** From liver, egg yolks, dairy
- **Vitamin D:** From fatty fish, egg yolks, liver
- **Vitamin K2:** From organ meats, egg yolks, certain dairy
- **Choline:** From eggs, liver
- **Copper:** From liver

### Nutrients at Risk (Potential Deficiencies)
- **Vitamin C:** Negligible in muscle meat; trace in liver. Status unknown long-term.
- **Folate:** Low without liver or dairy. Muscle meat is a poor source.
- **Magnesium:** Suboptimal from meat alone. Supplementation recommended.
- **Potassium:** Present but may be lower than needed without conscious effort.
- **Calcium:** Very low without dairy or bones.
- **Fiber:** **Zero.** Long-term implications unknown.
- **Vitamin E:** Low in meat.
- **Vitamin K1:** Absent (plant-derived); K2 from animal sources may compensate.
- **Manganese:** Low without plant foods.
- **Various phytonutrients:** Entirely absent.

### Recommended Supplements (Harm Reduction)
> These are **not universally endorsed** but represent the theoretical "insurance" approach for someone insisting on this diet:
- **Magnesium** glycinate or citrate (200–400mg/day)
- **Electrolytes** (sodium, potassium) — especially during adaptation
- **Vitamin C** (500mg/day) — theoretical insurance, debated by practitioners
- **Omega-3 EPA/DHA** — if fatty fish intake is low
- **Calcium** — if dairy is excluded
