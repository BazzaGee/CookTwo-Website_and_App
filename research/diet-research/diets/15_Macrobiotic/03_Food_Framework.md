# Macrobiotic Diet — Food Framework

> Practical guide to what to eat, avoid, and limit on the macrobiotic diet, including macronutrient targets, portion guidelines, and sample meal structures. Based on the Standard Macrobiotic Diet as taught by Michio Kushi, with notes on variations.

---

## Macronutrient Targets

| Macronutrient | Target (% of total calories) | Notes |
|---------------|:----------------------------:|-------|
| **Carbohydrates** | 55–65% | Primarily from whole grains; also from vegetables and beans |
| **Protein** | 12–18% | From whole grains, beans, soy foods, sea vegetables, occasional fish |
| **Fat** | 15–25% | From whole plant foods; small amounts of sesame oil for cooking; nuts/seeds occasionally |
| **Saturated fat** | <5% | Very low from whole plant foods |
| **Fiber** | 30–50 g/day | Very high — from whole grains, vegetables, beans |
| **Sodium** | Variable | Can be high (miso, soy sauce/tamari, umeboshi, gomashio); aim for moderate intake |

> The macrobiotic diet is a **high-carbohydrate, moderate-to-low-fat, plant-based** diet. Its proportions are defined by **volume** rather than calories — this is a key distinction from most dietary frameworks. By volume, 40–60% of the food on the plate should be whole grains.

---

## The Standard Macrobiotic Diet Proportions

The foundational proportions as established by Michio Kushi:

| Component | % by Volume | Notes |
|-----------|:-----------:|-------|
| **Whole cereal grains** | **40–60%** | Brown rice is the primary grain; others rotated for variety |
| **Vegetables** | **20–30%** | Local, seasonal, varied preparation methods |
| **Beans & bean products** | **5–10%** | Adzuki, chickpeas, lentils, tofu, tempeh |
| **Sea vegetables** | **small daily amounts** | Nori, wakame, kombu, arame, dulse |
| **Soups** | **5–10% (1–2 bowls/day)** | Miso soup daily; vegetable soups |
| **Pickles / condiments** | **daily, small amounts** | Umeboshi, sauerkraut, gomashio, tekka |
| **Occasional foods** | **5–10%** | Seasonal fruit, nuts/seeds, fish (relaxed versions) |
| **Beverages** | **as needed** | Bancha/kukicha tea, water, grain coffee |

## Foods to Eat Daily (The Core)

### Whole Grains (40–60% by volume — the foundation of every meal)

| Food | Examples | Serving |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Brown rice** (primary grain) | Short-grain, medium-grain, long-grain brown rice | 1–3+ cups cooked per meal |
| **Barley** | Pearl barley, hulled barley | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Millet** | Whole millet | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Oats** | Steel-cut, rolled | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Buckwheat** | Buckwheat groats, soba noodles | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Quinoa** | All colors | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Wheat** | Wheat berries, cracked wheat, couscous (whole wheat) | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Corn** | Cornmeal (polenta), fresh corn | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Rye** | Whole rye, rye berries | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Mixed grain dishes** | Brown rice + millet; brown rice + barley | Combination |

> **Brown rice is the anchor of the macrobiotic diet.** Michio Kushi recommended short-grain brown rice as the most balanced food (near the center of the yin-yang spectrum). Ideally, brown rice should appear at least once per day, and many traditional practitioners eat it at every meal.

### Vegetables (20–30% by volume — with every meal)

| Category | Examples | Preparation |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Leafy greens** | Kale, bok choy, napa cabbage, watercress, turnip greens | Steamed, blanched, sautéed |
| **Root vegetables** | Carrots, daikon radish, burdock root, turnips, parsnips, onions | Boiled, steamed, nishime |
| **Round vegetables** | Cabbage, onions, pumpkin/winter squash | Steamed, boiled, sautéed |
| **Green vegetables** | Broccoli, green beans, snow peas, scallions | Lightly steamed |
| **Mushrooms** | Shiitake, maitake, oyster mushrooms | Sautéed, in soups |

> **Nishime** (style of cooking): A signature macrobiotic cooking technique where vegetables are cut into large pieces, placed in a heavy pot with a small amount of water, and cooked slowly on low heat with the lid on — preserving nutrients and creating a sweet, rich flavor.

### Beans and Bean Products (5–10% by volume)

| Food | Examples | Serving |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Adzuki beans** | The most macrobiotically favored bean | ½–1 cup cooked, several times/week |
| **Chickpeas (garbanzo beans)** | Whole or as hummus-style dip | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Lentils** | Brown, green, red | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Black beans, kidney beans** | In soups and stews | ½–1 cup cooked |
| **Tofu** | Firm or soft | 100–150g |
| **Tempeh** | Fermented soybean cake | 100–150g |
| **Natto** | Fermented soybeans (highly valued for vitamin K2) | 2–3 tbsp |

### Sea Vegetables (small amounts daily)

| Food | Use | Notes |
|------|-----|-------|
| **Nori** | Wrapping, garnish, in soups | Most common; sheets or flakes |
| **Wakame** | In miso soup, salads | Soft, mild flavor |
| **Kombu (kelp)** | Added to beans during cooking (improves digestibility) | Also in soups |
| **Arame** | Salads, side dishes | Mild flavor |
| **Dulse** | Garnish, snacks | Red seaweed |
| **Hijiki** | Side dishes | ⚠️ **HIGH ARSENIC — limit or avoid; food safety advisories in multiple countries** |

### Soups (1–2 bowls daily)

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Miso soup** | Daily — made with miso paste, wakame, vegetables (daikon, tofu, scallions, etc.) |
| **Vegetable soup** | Clear broth with vegetables, grains, or beans |
| **Bean soup** | Adzuki, lentil, or chickpea-based |
| **Grain soup** | Congee-style rice porridge, barley soup |

> **Miso soup** is the most important soup in macrobiotic practice. It should be made fresh daily — miso paste is added at the end of cooking (not boiled) to preserve the beneficial enzymes and probiotic bacteria from fermentation.

### Daily Condiments and Pickles (small amounts)

| Food | Description | Use |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Gomashio** | Roasted sesame seeds ground with sea salt (typically 15:1 or 18:1 ratio) | Sprinkle on grains |
| **Umeboshi plum** | Pickled Japanese apricot — extremely salty and sour | 1 small plum per day; considered medicinal |
| **Tekka** | Condiment made from root vegetables (burdock, carrot, lotus root) miso, and sesame oil | Sprinkle on grains |
| **Pickled vegetables** | Lacto-fermented vegetables (sauerkraut-style) | Small amount daily with meals |
| **Tamari / shoyu** | Natural soy sauce (fermented) | In cooking and at the table |
| **Brown rice vinegar** | Natural vinegar | In dressings and cooking |

## Foods to Eat Occasionally (A few times per week)

### Fish (Moderate/Relaxed Versions Only)

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|
| **White-fleshed fish** | Fresh, local, non-oily white fish (halibut, flounder, cod, sea bass): 2–3 servings per week in relaxed versions |
| **Avoid:** Red meat, poultry, eggs, dairy | Strict macrobiotic eliminates all animal foods except occasional fish |

### Fruits (Seasonal, Temperate Climate Only)

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|
| **Temperate fruits** | Apples, pears, berries, cherries, peaches, plums, grapes, melon: 2–4 servings per week |
| **Avoid tropical fruits** | Bananas, mangoes, papayas, pineapple, citrus (in strict versions) — considered too "yin" and non-local for temperate climates |
| **Dried fruits** | Raisins, dried apricots — occasional |

### Nuts and Seeds (Roasted, Lightly Salted)

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|
| **Sesame seeds** | The most macrobiotically favored (in gomashio) |
| **Pumpkin seeds, sunflower seeds** | Roasted, small amounts |
| **Almonds, walnuts, pecans** | Roasted, occasional small handfuls |
| **Avoid:** Excessive amounts, raw nuts (considered harder to digest) |

### Natural Sweeteners (Occasional)

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|
| **Rice syrup (amazake)** | Fermented rice sweetener — occasional |
| **Barley malt** | Grain-based sweetener — occasional |
| **Maple syrup** | In moderate amounts |
| **Avoid:** Refined white sugar, high-fructose corn syrup, artificial sweeteners |

## Foods to Limit or Avoid

### Strictly Avoid (Standard Macrobiotic)

| Food | Reason |
|------|--------|
| **Refined sugar and sweets** | Considered extreme yin; disrupts blood sugar; empty calories |
| **Refined grains (white rice, white flour)** | Stripped of nutrients and fiber; considered less balanced |
| **Processed foods** | Additives, preservatives, artificial ingredients |
| **Coffee** | Considered extreme yin; caffeine disrupts energy balance |
| **Alcohol** | Considered extreme yin; empty calories |
| **Hot spices** (chili, cayenne, black pepper) | Considered too stimulating/extreme yang |
| **Tropical fruits** | Not local/seasonal for temperate climates; considered too yin |
| **Nightshade vegetables** (potatoes, tomatoes, eggplant, peppers) | ⚠️ Considered too yin; macrobiotics avoids them — **no scientific basis for this restriction for most people** |
| **Dairy products** (milk, cheese, yogurt) | Considered too yin/yang-imbalanced; mucus-forming |
| **Eggs** | Considered extreme yang |
| **Red meat, poultry** | Considered extreme yang; associated with disease |
| **Chemical additives** | Artificial colors, flavors, preservatives |
| **Microwave ovens** | Believed to disrupt food's natural energy |

### Foods to Limit (Moderate Versions)

| Food | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|
| **Oils** | Small amounts of sesame oil or olive oil for cooking; avoid deep frying |
| **Salt** | Use natural sea salt; moderate amounts (miso, tamari, umeboshi contribute sodium) |
| **Baked flour products** | Bread, cookies, crackers — moderate amounts of whole grain versions |

## Daily Eating Pattern (Sample Day)

### Breakfast
- Bowl of cooked whole grain (brown rice, steel-cut oats, or millet)
- Miso soup with wakame, tofu, and scallions
- Steamed greens (kale or bok choy)
- Condiment: gomashio sprinkled on grain
- Beverage: Bancha (kukicha) tea or grain coffee

### Lunch
- Brown rice or mixed grain bowl with vegetables
- Bean dish (adzuki beans, lentils, or chickpeas)
- Nishime vegetables (carrots, burdock, onion, winter squash)
- Pickled vegetables (small side)
- Miso soup or vegetable soup
- Beverage: Bancha tea or water

### Snack (optional)
- Roasted seeds (pumpkin or sunflower)
- Fresh seasonal fruit (apple, pear, berries)
- *Or:* rice cakes with miso-tahini spread
- Beverage: herbal tea

### Dinner
- Brown rice or soba noodles
- Steamed or sautéed vegetables (broccoli, mushrooms, leafy greens)
- Tofu or tempeh dish
- Vegetable soup
- Pickled vegetables
- Beverage: Bancha tea

### Eating Practices
- Chew each mouthful **50+ times** (ideally 50–100)
- Eat in a calm, peaceful environment
- Express gratitude before meals
- Stop eating when 70–80% full
- Avoid eating within 3 hours of bedtime
- Do not eat while watching TV, reading, or distracted

## Weekly Meal Structure (Macrobiotic-Style)

| Day | Breakfast | Lunch | Dinner |
|-----|-----------|-------|--------|
| Monday | Brown rice, miso soup, steamed kale | Brown rice bowl with adzuki beans and nishime vegetables | Soba noodles with steamed vegetables and tofu |
| Tuesday | Steel-cut oats, miso soup, umeboshi | Brown rice with chickpea stew and steamed greens | Millet with lentils and roasted root vegetables |
| Wednesday | Brown rice, miso soup, steamed bok choy | Brown rice bowl with tempeh and sautéed mushrooms | Barley soup with vegetables and whole grain bread |
| Thursday | Millet porridge, miso soup, gomashio | Brown rice with black beans and nishime squash | Quinoa with steamed vegetables and tahini dressing |
| Friday | Brown rice, miso soup, natto | Brown rice bowl with lentils and steamed broccoli | Brown rice pasta with vegetables and sesame oil |
| Saturday | Oatmeal, miso soup, pickled vegetables | Brown rice with fish (relaxed version) and vegetables | Congee (rice porridge) with vegetables and seaweed |
| Sunday | Mixed grain bowl, miso soup, steamed greens | Brown rice with tofu scramble and vegetables | Hearty vegetable-bean stew with brown rice |

> **Note:** Miso soup appears at virtually every meal in traditional macrobiotic practice. Brown rice is the anchor grain, appearing at most meals. The diet emphasizes simplicity, repetition, and ritual rather than constant novelty.

## Seasonal Adjustments

Macrobiotics emphasizes eating **according to season and climate**:

### Spring
- Lighter cooking methods (steaming, quick sautéing)
- More leafy greens and sprouts
- Lighter grains (barley, millet)
- Less salt and oil

### Summer
- Lightest cooking (raw salads, quick steaming)
- More cooling vegetables (cucumber, summer squash, corn)
- Fresh fruits (in moderation)
- More liquids (light soups, teas)

### Autumn
- Heartier cooking (baking, pressure cooking)
- More root vegetables and winter squash
- Heavier grains (brown rice, oats, sweet rice)
- More beans

### Winter
- Heaviest cooking (long simmering, pressure cooking)
- Root vegetables, hardy greens (kale, collards)
- Hearty soups and stews
- Warming grains (brown rice, buckwheat)
- More miso and umeboshi (for warmth/immunity)

## Cooking Methods and Equipment

Macrobiotics prescribes specific cooking methods and even kitchen equipment:

| Method | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Pressure cooking** | Preferred for grains and beans — improves digestibility, saves time |
| **Steaming** | For vegetables — preserves nutrients |
| **Nishime** | Slow water-sautéing of vegetables in a heavy pot |
| **Sautéing** | With small amounts of sesame oil |
| **Boiling/simmering** | For soups and stews |
| **Baking** | For squashes, root vegetables, grain dishes |
| **Pickling/fermenting** | For vegetables (lacto-fermentation) |
| **Avoid:** | Deep frying, microwave cooking, electric cooking (gas flame preferred) |

| Equipment | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| **Heavy pots (nabe)** | For nishime and stews |
| **Pressure cooker** | For grains and beans |
| **Wooden cutting boards** | Preferred over plastic |
| **Gas stove** | Preferred over electric (considered more "natural") |
| **Cast iron / stainless steel cookware** | Preferred over aluminum and non-stick |

## Key Nutritional Considerations

### Nutrients Adequately Provided
- **Carbohydrates (energy):** Abundantly from whole grains
- **Fiber:** Among the highest-fiber diets (30–50 g/day)
- **Complex carbohydrates:** Sustained energy from whole grains
- **Phytoestrogens (lignans):** High from whole grains, flaxseed, soy
- **Fermented food benefits:** Probiotics from miso, natto, pickles
- **Trace minerals:** From sea vegetables (iodine, magnesium, iron)
- **Vitamin K2:** From natto, miso, and fermented foods

### Nutrients at Risk (Critical Concerns)

| Nutrient | Risk | Solution |
|----------|:----:|----------|
| **Vitamin B12** | **Critical — no reliable plant source** | Supplement (sublingual 1,000 mcg 2–3×/week); some claim fermented foods provide B12, but this is unreliable |
| **Vitamin D** | **High — no dietary source in strict macrobiotic** | Sunlight; supplement 1,000–2,000 IU/day |
| **Iron** | **High** — plant iron is less bioavailable | Beans, leafy greens, sea vegetables; pair with vitamin C; supplement if deficient |
| **Calcium** | **Moderate** — no dairy | Sesame seeds, leafy greens, calcium-set tofu; consider supplement |
| **Vitamin B2 (riboflavin)** | Moderate | Whole grains, leafy greens |
| **Zinc** | Moderate | Beans, whole grains, pumpkin seeds |
| **Protein** | Generally adequate, but children/pregnant women may need more | Ensure sufficient beans, soy foods |
| **Calories** | Can be insufficient for children and active adults | Increase grain portions; add more calorie-dense foods |

> **Citation (nutritional adequacy):** Dagnelie PC, van Staveren WA. "Macrobiotic nutrition and child health: results of a population-based, mixed-longitudinal cohort study in Europe." *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 1994;59(5 Suppl):1187S-1196S.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn/59.5.1187S
