# Flexitarian Diet — Food Framework

> Practical guide to what to eat, avoid, and limit on the flexitarian diet, including macronutrient targets, portion guidelines, level-based meat allowances, and sample meal structures.

---

## Macronutrient Targets

| Macronutrient | Target (% of total calories) | Notes |
|---------------|:----------------------------:|-------|
| **Carbohydrates** | ~50% | Primarily from whole grains, legumes, vegetables, fruits |
| **Protein** | ~18% | From legumes, soy, nuts, eggs, dairy, occasional meat/fish/poultry |
| **Fat** | ~32% | From olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado, moderate animal fats |
| **Saturated fat** | <8% | Low due to reduced meat intake |
| **Fiber** | 28–40 g/day | Higher than omnivorous diets; lower than strict vegan |
| **Sodium** | <2,300 mg/day | Moderate; mindful of processed foods |

> **Note:** Blatner's original flexitarian plan does not specify exact macro percentages — the ~50/18/32 distribution reflects the typical macronutrient profile observed in studies of semi-vegetarian and plant-forward eating patterns. The emphasis is on food choices rather than macro tracking.

## The Three Flexitarian Levels — Meat Allowance

| Level | Meatless Days/Week | Total Meat/Week | Daily Meat Equivalent | Typical Meals |
|-------|:------------------:|:---------------:|:---------------------:|---------------|
| **Beginner** | 2 days | ~26 oz (737g) | ~3.7 oz/day avg | Omnivorous but with 2 fully vegetarian days |
| **Advanced** | 3–4 days | ~18 oz (510g) | ~2.6 oz/day avg | Mostly vegetarian with 3–4 meat-containing meals |
| **Expert** | 5+ days | ~9 oz (255g) | ~1.3 oz/day avg | Near-vegetarian; meat used as a condiment/garnish |

> **Serving size reference:** 3 oz (85g) of meat ≈ the size of a deck of cards. Blatner recommends meat portions of 3–5 oz (85–140g) per meal when meat is included.

## Foods to Eat Daily (The Base)

### Vegetables (4+ servings/day)
Eat a wide variety, focusing on color diversity:

| Food | Examples | Serving Size |
|------|----------|:------------:|
| Leafy greens | Spinach, kale, collards, Swiss chard, arugula, romaine | 1 cup raw / ½ cup cooked |
| Cruciferous | Broccoli, cauliflower, cabbage, Brussels sprouts | ½ cup cooked |
| Alliums | Onions, garlic, leeks, shallots | ½ onion / 1-2 cloves garlic |
| Tomatoes, peppers | All varieties and colors | 1 medium / ½ cup |
| Root vegetables | Carrots, beets, parsnips, sweet potatoes | ½ cup cooked |
| Other vegetables | Zucchini, eggplant, green beans, asparagus, mushrooms | ½ cup cooked |

| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|

- Any preparation — scrambled, poached, hard-boiled, in frittatas, baking

## Foods to Eat Occasionally (Meat & Fish — 2–3 times per week)

### Fish & Seafood (prioritize when including animal protein)
| Food | Omega-3 Content | Serving Size |
|------|:---------------:|:------------:|
| Salmon, mackerel, sardines, herring | ★★★ Very high | 85–140g |
| Tuna (fresh or canned) | ★★☆ High | 85–140g |
| Trout, sea bass | ★★☆ High | 85–140g |
| Shrimp, mussels, clams, squid | ★☆☆ Moderate | 85–140g |
| Cod, halibut, tilapia | ★☆☆ Low-moderate | 85–140g |

> **Recommendation:** When including animal protein, **prioritize fish** (especially fatty fish) for omega-3 content, followed by poultry, with red meat least frequent.

### Poultry (occasional, weekly or less)
| Food | Serving Size |
|------|:------------:|
| Chicken breast, thighs | 85–120g |
| Turkey | 85–120g |

### Lean Red Meat (occasional, a few times per month)
| Food | Serving Size | Notes |
|------|:------------:|-------|
| Beef (lean cuts: sirloin, flank, tenderloin) | 85–120g | Occasional — few times/month |
| Pork (lean cuts: tenderloin, loin chop) | 85–120g | Occasional |
| Lamb | 85–120g | Occasional |

## Foods to Limit / Minimize

| Food | Reason |
|------|--------|
| **Processed meats** (sausages, bacon, hot dogs, deli meats) | AVOID — WHO Group 1 carcinogen; strongly linked to colorectal cancer and CVD |
| **Red meat (frequent)** | Limit to a few times per month; associated with CVD, cancer, and diabetes risk |
| **Refined grains** (white bread, white rice, regular pasta) | Replace with whole grain versions |
| **Added sugars & sweets** | Minimize — occasional treats only; fruit is the typical dessert |
| **Sugary drinks** (soda, sweetened beverages, juice with added sugar) | Avoid |
| **Fried foods** (french fries, fried chicken, fried snacks) | Minimize — high in unhealthy fats and calories |
| **Fast food & ultra-processed foods** | Minimize — low nutrient density, high sodium and unhealthy fats |
| **Industrial seed oils** (in large amounts) | Prefer olive oil and avocado oil |

## Blatner's "Flex Foods" — Smart Swaps

One of Blatner's key innovations is the concept of **"flex foods"** — specific foods that maximize nutrition per calorie and help transition away from meat-centric meals:

| Category | Flex Foods | Why |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| **Plant proteins** | Lentils, beans, tofu, tempeh, edamame, eggs, Greek yogurt | High protein, low saturated fat |
| **Flavor enhancers** | Nutritional yeast, miso, smoked paprika, liquid smoke, tamari, mushrooms | Replicate umami/savory flavors of meat |
| **Texture substitutes** | Portobello mushrooms, jackfruit, eggplant, cauliflower steaks | Hearty, satisfying textures |
| **Nutrient boosters** | Hemp seeds, chia seeds, flaxseed, walnuts, leafy greens | Omega-3, iron, calcium, protein density |
| **Satiety foods** | Beans, lentils, oats, potatoes, apples | High fiber/satiety per calorie |

## Daily Eating Pattern — Sample Day (Advanced Flexitarian)

### Breakfast (meatless)
- Oatmeal made with milk (cow's or fortified plant milk), topped with blueberries, walnuts, and a drizzle of honey
- *Or:* Greek yogurt with granola, banana, and chia seeds
- *Or:* Two eggs scrambled with spinach, tomato, and feta, with whole grain toast
- Coffee or tea

### Lunch (meatless)
- Buddha bowl: quinoa, roasted chickpeas, sweet potato, kale, avocado, tahini dressing
- *Or:* Large mixed salad with lentils, feta, olives, cherry tomatoes, cucumber, olive oil + lemon dressing
- *Or:* Lentil soup with whole grain bread and a side salad

### Snack (optional)
- Apple with almond butter
- *Or:* Handful of almonds and dark chocolate
- *Or:* Hummus with carrot and cucumber sticks
- *Or:* Greek yogurt with honey

### Dinner (with meat/fish — one of ~3 meat meals/week)
- Grilled salmon (120g) with roasted asparagus and brown rice
- *Or:* Chicken breast (100g) stir-fry with broccoli, bell peppers, mushrooms, and quinoa
- *Or:* Turkey meatballs (small portion) with whole wheat pasta and marinara sauce

## Daily Eating Pattern — Sample Day (Expert Flexitarian / near-vegetarian)

### Breakfast
- Chia pudding made with soy milk, topped with mango, coconut, and hemp seeds
- *Or:* Tofu scramble with mushrooms, bell peppers, spinach, and nutritional yeast
- *Or:* Oatmeal with banana, peanut butter, and flaxseed

### Lunch
- Black bean and corn salad with avocado, lime, cilantro, and whole grain tortilla chips
- *Or:* Minestrone soup with whole grain crackers and a side salad
- *Or:* Caprese sandwich (mozzarella, tomato, basil) on whole grain ciabatta

### Snack (optional)
- Handful of mixed nuts
- *Or:* Edamame with sea salt
- *Or:* Cottage cheese with peach slices

### Dinner (meatless, or with 2–3 oz meat as garnish)
- Sweet potato and black bean enchiladas with salsa and Greek yogurt
- *Or:* Large salad topped with grilled chicken (2 oz as garnish), walnuts, dried cranberries, vinaigrette
- *Or:* Pasta primavera with a few shrimp (2 oz) and lots of vegetables

## Key Nutritional Considerations

### Nutrients Adequately Provided
- **Fiber:** 28–40g/day — significantly higher than standard Western diet (~15g/day)
- **Antioxidants and polyphenols:** High from diverse plant foods
- **Potassium, magnesium, folate:** High from vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains
- **Vitamin C:** High from fruits and vegetables
- **B12:** Adequate from eggs, dairy, and occasional meat/fish — **no supplementation typically required** (major advantage over veganism)
- **Iron:** Adequate from both heme (occasional meat) and non-heme (legumes, leafy greens) sources
- **Calcium:** Adequate from dairy (if included), fortified alternatives, leafy greens
- **Omega-3 (DHA/EPA):** Adequate if fish is included 1–2x/week
- **Zinc, iodine:** Adequate from occasional meat/fish/dairy

### Nutrients to Monitor
- **Vitamin D:** May be insufficient without adequate sun exposure, fatty fish, or fortified dairy. Consider supplementing 1000–2000 IU/day.
- **Iron:** Women of reproductive age should ensure adequate iron intake, especially on meatless days. Pair plant iron with vitamin C.
- **Omega-3:** If fish intake is very low, consider an algae-based or fish oil DHA/EPA supplement.
- **Protein:** Ensure 2–3 protein-rich foods daily (legumes, eggs, dairy, tofu, fish/meat). For most people, protein intake is easily adequate.

> **Key advantage:** The flexitarian diet has the **lowest risk of nutrient deficiency** of all plant-based dietary patterns, because occasional animal products provide B12, highly bioavailable iron, DHA/EPA, calcium, zinc, and iodine. This is a major practical advantage over strict veganism.
