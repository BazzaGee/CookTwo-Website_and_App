# Flexitarian Diet — Overview

## Definition

The Flexitarian Diet — a portmanteau of **"flexible"** and **"vegetarian"** — is a predominantly plant-based dietary pattern that allows for **occasional consumption of meat, fish, and other animal products**. It is not a rigid set of rules but rather a **gradual, moderate approach** to eating more plants while retaining the flexibility to include animal foods when desired, socially convenient, or nutritionally beneficial.

The defining principle of the flexitarian diet is that it is **mostly vegetarian without being strict about it**. There is no hard threshold for what constitutes "occasional" meat consumption, but the general guideline is:

- Predominantly plant-based meals (the majority of meals)
- Meat/fish/poultry included in **moderate amounts**, typically ~2–3 meals per week
- No foods are strictly banned — but animal products are reduced, and whole plant foods are prioritized

> **Key distinction from "plant-based":** A "plant-based" diet (in its strict WFPB form) emphasizes minimal to no animal products and focuses on whole, unprocessed foods. The flexitarian diet is more relaxed about both — it permits regular (if infrequent) animal products and does not strictly exclude processed foods (though it encourages whole foods). The flexitarian approach is designed for **maximum adherence and real-world practicality**.

It is widely regarded as one of the **most sustainable and easiest-to-follow** healthy dietary patterns, and is consistently ranked among the top diets by U.S. News & World Report.

## History & Origins

### The Coining of "Flexitarian" (Early 2000s)

The term "flexitarian" first appeared in the early 2000s in popular media and was recognized by the American Dialect Society, which voted it among the most useful new words of 2003. The concept was driven by a growing number of people who wanted the health benefits of a plant-based diet without the rigidity of full vegetarianism or veganism.

### Dawn Jackson Blatner and "The Flexitarian Diet" (2009)

The diet was formally defined and popularized by **Dawn Jackson Blatner, RD** (Registered Dietitian), a spokesperson for the American Dietetic Association (now the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics), in her 2009 book:

> **Blatner DJ.** *The Flexitarian Diet: The Mostly Vegetarian Way to Lose Weight, Be Healthier, Prevent Disease and Add Years to Your Life.* New York: McGraw-Hill; 2009.

Blatner designed the diet around a **5-week meal plan** with a graduated approach, allowing people to progress from beginner to expert flexitarian levels. Her philosophy was rooted in evidence-based nutrition practice — emphasizing that the best diet is one people can actually stick with long-term.

### The "Meatless Monday" Movement (2003)

The flexitarian concept gained cultural traction through the **Meatless Monday** campaign, launched in 2003 by Sid Lerner in association with the Johns Hopkins Bloomberg School of Public Health. The campaign encourages people to skip meat one day a week for health and environmental benefits. It has since been adopted by schools, restaurants, and institutions in over 40 countries.

### The "Reducetarian" Movement (2015)

In 2015, **Brian Kateman** co-founded the **Reducetarian Foundation**, promoting a lifestyle of "deliberately reducing consumption of meat, eggs, and dairy" without necessarily eliminating them. This overlaps significantly with the flexitarian concept and reflects the growing mainstream appeal of moderate, plant-forward eating.

### The EAT-Lancet Commission (2019)

The flexitarian approach was strongly validated by the **EAT-Lancet Commission on Healthy Diets from Sustainable Food Systems** (2019), which proposed the **Planetary Health Diet** — a predominantly plant-based diet that includes small amounts of animal-source foods. The commission concluded that this dietary pattern is optimal for both human health and environmental sustainability, and estimated that a global shift to such a diet could prevent approximately 11 million deaths per year.

> **Citation:** Willett W, Rockström J, Loken B, et al. "Food in the Anthropocene: the EAT–Lancet Commission on healthy diets from sustainable food systems." *The Lancet*. 2019;393(10170):447-492.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/S0140-6736(18)31788-4

## Core Philosophy

The flexitarian diet is built on several pragmatic principles:

1. **Progress over perfection** — The goal is to eat *more* plant-based meals, not to achieve dietary purity. Any reduction in meat consumption is a step in the right direction. This eliminates the "all-or-nothing" mentality that undermines many diets.

2. **Plant-forward, not plant-exclusive** — Plants form the foundation of every meal, but meat, fish, and other animal products are welcome in moderation. This makes the diet adaptable to social situations, cultural traditions, and individual preferences.

3. **Flexibility for real life** — The diet acknowledges that strict rules lead to failure. Whether it's a holiday dinner, a business lunch, or travel, the flexitarian approach allows for graceful adaptation without guilt.

4. **Health through abundance, not restriction** — Rather than focusing on what to eliminate, the flexitarian diet emphasizes what to *add*: more vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, nuts, and seeds.

5. **Sustainability (both dietary and environmental)** — The diet is designed for lifelong adherence. It is also environmentally sustainable, as reduced meat consumption significantly lowers the carbon and water footprint of one's diet.

6. **No calorie counting required** — Blatner's original plan focuses on food choices and proportions rather than strict calorie tracking, though it includes portion guidance.

## Key Components (The Flexitarian Food Philosophy)

### Base — Eat Abundantly (Every Meal)
- **Vegetables:** all varieties — the more, the better
- **Fruits:** all varieties — aim for color diversity
- **Whole grains:** brown rice, quinoa, oats, barley, whole wheat, farro
- **Legumes and beans:** lentils, chickpeas, black beans, kidney beans, split peas (daily)
- **Nuts and seeds:** almonds, walnuts, flaxseeds, chia seeds, hemp seeds, pumpkin seeds
- **Plant proteins:** tofu, tempeh, edamame (regularly)

### Include Daily
- **Plant-based fats:** olive oil, avocado, nut butters
- **Dairy or fortified alternatives:** yogurt, cheese, fortified plant milks
- **Eggs:** up to ~4–6 per week (or less)

### Include Occasionally (2–3 times per week)
- **Fish and seafood:** especially fatty fish (salmon, sardines, mackerel) for omega-3
- **Poultry:** chicken, turkey
- **Lean red meat:** occasional, small portions (if desired)

### Limit / Minimize
- **Processed meats:** sausages, bacon, hot dogs, deli meats
- **Refined grains:** white bread, white rice, regular pasta
- **Added sugars:** sweets, sugary drinks
- **Ultra-processed foods:** fast food, packaged snacks

## The Three Levels of Flexitarian (Blatner's Framework)

Blatner's original plan includes three progressive levels:

| Level | Meatless Days/Week | Meat Meals/Week | Description |
|-------|:------------------:|:---------------:|-------------|
| **Beginner** | 2 | 5 (moderate portions) | Easiest entry point. Two meatless days per week (~26 oz meat/week total). |
| **Advanced** | 3–4 | 3–4 (moderate portions) | The most common flexitarian level. Three to four meatless days per week (~18 oz meat/week). |
| **Expert** | 5+ | ≤2 (small portions) | Near-vegetarian. Five or more meatless days per week (~9 oz meat/week). |

> **Note:** These levels are guidelines, not rigid categories. The flexitarian philosophy embraces any level of reduced meat consumption.

> Blatner recommends starting at the Beginner level and gradually progressing. Each level reduces total meat intake by ~50% from the previous level. The meat allowance at each level is based on ~3 oz (85g) servings.

## Variations & Sub-Types

### Related Concepts and Terms

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Semi-vegetarian** | Older term, largely synonymous with flexitarian. Sometimes used in research (AHS-2 uses this category). |
| **Reducetarian** | Coined by Brian Kateman (2015). Focuses on reducing (not eliminating) animal product consumption for health, environmental, and animal welfare reasons. Broader than diet — includes leather, wool, etc. |
| **Pescatarian** | Vegetarian + fish. A specific variant that includes all fish/seafood but no meat/poultry. Often classified as a flexitarian sub-type or adjacent pattern. |
| **Part-time vegetarian** | Colloquial term for someone who follows a vegetarian diet most of the time but eats meat occasionally. |
| **Weekday vegetarian** | A specific pattern: vegetarian Monday–Friday, omnivorous on weekends. Popularized by Graham Hill's 2010 TED Talk. |
| **Meatless Monday participant** | Someone who skips meat one day a week (minimum flexitarianism). |
| **Mediterranean-style flexitarian** | Combines flexitarian principles with Mediterranean diet staples (olive oil, fish, vegetables). |
| **Plant-forward** | An industry/culinary term for menus and eating styles that emphasize plants without necessarily excluding animal products. Increasingly used in food service and institutional dining. |

## Who It's For

The flexitarian diet is appropriate for **virtually everyone** and is often recommended as the **most practical entry point** to healthier eating. It is:

- ✅ **Endorsed by:** the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, U.S. Dietary Guidelines 2020–2025 (as part of the Healthy Vegetarian pattern), and consistently ranked in the top 5 diets by U.S. News & World Report. The EAT-Lancet Commission's Planetary Health Diet is essentially a flexitarian pattern.

- ✅ **Suitable for:** adults, elderly, children, pregnant women, athletes, people transitioning from a standard Western diet, people who want health benefits of plant-based eating without giving up meat, and anyone who values dietary flexibility.

- ✅ **Particularly ideal for:**
  - **Beginners** to healthy eating — the graduated levels make it approachable
  - **Families** with mixed dietary preferences — flexible enough for everyone
  - **People who have failed at stricter diets** — the lack of rigid rules supports long-term adherence
  - **Those with social/cultural dining needs** — no awkward refusals at gatherings
  - **Budget-conscious eaters** — replacing meat with beans and grains saves money
  - **Environmentally motivated individuals** — significant carbon footprint reduction without strict veganism

- ⚠️ **Caution for:**
  - People who need very specific dietary prescriptions (e.g., therapeutic diets for heart disease reversal — Ornish or Esselstyn are more targeted)
  - Those with legume/soy/nut allergies (core flexitarian protein sources may be challenging)
  - High-level athletes with very high protein needs (may need to be more intentional about protein)

- ❌ **Not ideal for:**
  - People who require highly restrictive diets for specific medical conditions (therapeutic ketosis, specific allergy avoidance)
  - Those following strict ethical frameworks (veganism for animal welfare — the flexibility would be philosophically incompatible)
