# Plant-Based / Vegan / Vegetarian Diet — Health Outcomes

> Comprehensive summary of demonstrated health benefits, risks, nutrient deficiencies, special populations, and medical guidelines for plant-based, vegan, and vegetarian diets.

---

## Demonstrated Health Benefits

### 1. Cardiovascular Disease Prevention (Grade B)

**One of the strongest and most consistent benefits of plant-based diets.**

| Outcome | Effect Size | Evidence Source |
|---------|------------|-----------------|
| Ischemic heart disease mortality | **~29% reduction** in vegetarians | Huang et al. 2012 meta-analysis |
| LDL cholesterol reduction | **~−15 mg/dL** | Yokoyama et al. 2017 meta-analysis |
| Total cholesterol reduction | **~−12 mg/dL** | Yokoyama et al. 2017 meta-analysis |
| CVD prevalence (AHS-2) | Significantly lower in vegans vs. non-vegetarians | Adventist Health Study-2 |
| Coronary artery disease regression | Demonstrated with Ornish WFPB program | Ornish et al. 1990, 1998 |

**Mechanisms:**
- Dramatically lower LDL cholesterol (no dietary cholesterol, very low saturated fat, high soluble fiber, plant sterols)
- Lower blood pressure (high potassium, magnesium, and polyphenol intake; lower BMI)
- Improved endothelial function (high nitrate intake, reduced oxidative stress)
- Reduced inflammation (high antioxidants, lower CRP)
- Lower TMAO (no carnitine/choline from red meat/eggs)
- Reduced platelet aggregation

> **Citation:** Jenkins DJ, Kendall CW, Marchie A, et al. "Direct comparison of a dietary portfolio of cholesterol-lowering foods with a statin in hypercholesterolemic participants." *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2005;81(2):380-387.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/ajcn.81.2.380

> **Ornish Program:** Ornish D, Brown SE, Scherwitz LW, et al. "Can lifestyle changes reverse coronary heart disease? The Lifestyle Heart Trial." *Lancet*. 1990;336(8708):129-133.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/0140-6736(90)91656-U

> The Ornish program (near-vegan, <10% fat, whole foods) demonstrated **regression of coronary atherosclerosis** — one of the only dietary interventions to show actual reversal of heart disease.

---

### 2. All-Cause Mortality (Grade B)

| Outcome | Effect Size | Evidence Source |
|---------|------------|-----------------|
| All-cause mortality (vegetarians) | **~12% reduction** (HR 0.88) | AHS-2 (Orlich et al. 2013) |
| All-cause mortality (EPIC-Oxford) | No significant difference (HR 1.02) | Key et al. 2009 |
| All-cause mortality (meta-analysis) | **~9% reduction** | Oomah et al.; Dinu et al. 2017 |

> **Citation:** Orlich MJ, Singh PN, Sabaté J, et al. "Vegetarian dietary patterns and mortality in Seventh-Day Adventists." *JAMA Internal Medicine*. 2013;173(13):1230-1238.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2013.6473

> The discrepancy between AHS-2 (benefit) and EPIC-Oxford (null) likely reflects the health-consciousness of the EPIC-Oxford comparison group and dietary quality differences. The overall evidence supports a modest mortality benefit for well-planned plant-based diets.

---

### 3. Blood Pressure Reduction (Grade A)

| Outcome | Effect Size |
|---------|------------|
| Systolic BP (RCTs) | **−4.8 mmHg** |
| Diastolic BP (RCTs) | **−2.2 mmHg** |
| Systolic BP (observational) | **−6.9 mmHg** |
| Diastolic BP (observational) | **−4.7 mmHg** |

> **Citation:** Yokoyama Y, Nishimura K, Barnard ND, et al. "Vegetarian diets and blood pressure: a meta-analysis." *JAMA Internal Medicine*. 2014;174(4):577-587.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/jamainternmed.2013.14547

> The blood pressure reduction is clinically significant — a 5 mmHg systolic reduction is associated with ~14% reduction in stroke mortality and ~9% reduction in heart disease mortality.

---

### 4. Type 2 Diabetes Prevention & Management (Grade B)

| Outcome | Effect Size | Evidence Source |
|---------|------------|-----------------|
| Diabetes prevalence (vegans, AHS-2) | **49% lower** vs. non-vegetarians | Tonstad et al. 2013 |
| Diabetes prevalence (lacto-ovo) | 38% lower | AHS-2 |
| HbA1c reduction (in existing T2DM) | **−0.40%** | Viguiliouk et al. 2019 meta-analysis |
| Fasting glucose reduction | Significant | Viguiliouk et al. 2019 |
| Weight loss (in T2DM patients) | **−2.15 kg** | Viguiliouk et al. 2019 |

> **Citation:** Tonstad S, Stewart K, Oda K, Batech M, Herring RP, Fraser GE. "Vegetarian diets and incidence of diabetes in the Adventist Health Study-2." *Nutrition, Metabolism and Cardiovascular Diseases*. 2013;23(4):292-299.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.numecd.2011.07.004

> **Citation:** Viguiliouk E, Kendall CW, Kahleová H, et al. "Effect of vegetarian dietary patterns on cardiometabolic risk factors in diabetes." *Clinical Nutrition*. 2019;38(3):1133-1145.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2018.05.032

> **Significance:** The ~50% lower diabetes prevalence in vegans is striking. Much of this is mediated by lower BMI, but plant-based diets also improve insulin sensitivity independent of weight loss — through reduced intracellular lipid accumulation, lower saturated fat intake, higher fiber intake, and improved gut microbiome.

---

### 5. Weight Management (Grade B)

| Outcome | Effect Size | Evidence Source |
|---------|------------|-----------------|
| Weight loss (vs. non-vegetarian diets) | **−2.02 kg** (mean, ~44 weeks) | Huang et al. 2016 meta-analysis |
| Weight loss (vegans specifically) | **−2.40 kg** | Huang et al. 2016 |
| BMI (AHS-2) | Vegans have lowest BMI (~23.6) vs. non-vegetarians (~28.8) | AHS-2 |

> **Citation:** Huang RY, Huang CC, Hu FB, Chavarro JE. "Vegetarian diets and weight reduction: a meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials." *Journal of General Internal Medicine*. 2016;31(1):109-116.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-015-3390-7

> Plant-based diets promote weight loss through multiple mechanisms: lower calorie density of plant foods (high fiber and water content → satiety at lower calories), higher thermic effect of food, improved insulin sensitivity (less fat storage), and reduced hyperpalatability (less engineered overeating).

### 6. Cancer Risk (Grade B-C — Mixed)

| Cancer Type | Effect | Notes |
|-------------|--------|-------|
| **Overall cancer incidence** | **~15% reduction** (RR 0.85) in vegetarians | Dinu et al. 2017 meta-analysis |
| **Overall cancer incidence (vegans)** | **~18% reduction** (RR 0.82) | Dinu et al. 2017 |
| **Colorectal cancer** | **~22% reduction** in lacto-ovo vegetarians | AHS-2 (Tantamango-Bartley et al.) |
| **Colorectal cancer (vegans)** | ~16% reduction (borderline significance) | AHS-2 — dairy may be protective |
| **Breast cancer** | No significant reduction | EPIC-Oxford; AHS-2 |
| **Prostate cancer** | **~35% reduction** in vegans (AHS-2) | Some studies, inconsistent |
| **Stomach cancer** | Possible increase in some vegan cohorts | Inconsistent; possibly due to high-salt fermented foods |

> **Citation (colorectal):** Tantamango-Bartley Y, Jaceldo-Siegl K, Fan J, Fraser GE. "Vegetarian diets and the incidence of cancer in a low-risk population." *Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention*. 2013;22(2):286-294.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-12-1060

> **Citation (overall cancer meta-analysis):** Dinu M, Abbate R, Gensini GF, Casini A, Sofi F. "Vegetarian, vegan diets and multiple health outcomes: A systematic review with meta-analysis of observational studies." *Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition*. 2017;57(17):3640-3649.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2016.1138447

> **Interpretation:** The cancer evidence is real but modest. Plant-based diets consistently reduce overall cancer risk by ~15–18%, likely through multiple mechanisms: lower IGF-1, higher fiber (colorectal protection), higher antioxidant intake, reduced carcinogenic compounds from meat processing/cooking, and lower obesity. However, the effects are cancer-type-specific and do not constitute a guarantee against cancer.

### 7. Gut Microbiome (Grade B — emerging)

- Plant-based diets dramatically increase microbial diversity
- Increase SCFA-producing bacteria (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Faecalibacterium prausnitzii)
- Higher butyrate production → improved gut barrier integrity, reduced systemic inflammation
- Reduced TMAO-producing bacteria
- Enhanced immune function via gut-microbiome-immune axis

> **Citation:** Wu GD, Compher C, Chen EZ, et al. "Comparative metabolomics in vegans and omnivores reveal constraints on diet-dependent gut microbiota metabolite production." *Gut*. 2016;65(1):63-72.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1136/gutjnl-2014-308209

### 8. Other Outcomes

| Condition | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| **Rheumatoid arthritis** | Plant-based diets may reduce symptoms and inflammation (McDougall program; WFPB studies) |
| **Kidney function** | Plant proteins produce less acid load and may slow CKD progression |
| **Cognitive health** | Limited evidence; some signal for reduced cognitive decline (lower inflammation, higher antioxidants) |

| **Fertility** | Plant-based diets associated with improved fertility outcomes in some studies |

## Risks & Potential Concerns

### 1. Vitamin B12 Deficiency (CRITICAL for Vegans)

**The most important nutritional risk of vegan diets. Vitamin B12 is produced only by bacteria and is found naturally only in animal products.**

| Consequence of B12 Deficiency | Mechanism |
|-------------------------------|-----------|
| Megaloblastic anemia | Impaired DNA synthesis in red blood cells |
| Peripheral neuropathy | Demyelination of peripheral nerves — **irreversible** if untreated |
| Cognitive decline and dementia | Central nervous system damage |
| Elevated homocysteine | Independent CVD risk factor |
| Subfertility and neural tube defects | Critical in pregnancy |

> **Critical note:** B12 deficiency can develop within **1–3 years** of starting a vegan diet (or faster in pregnant/lactating women). Elevated MMA (methylmalonic acid) and homocysteine are more sensitive markers than serum B12. **All vegans must supplement B12** — this is non-negotiable.

> **Citation:** Pawlak R, Lester SE, Babatunde T. "The prevalence of cobalamin deficiency among vegetarians assessed by serum vitamin B12: a review of literature." *European Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2014;68(5):541-548.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/ejcn.2014.46

### 2. Iron Deficiency

- Plant-based diets contain only **non-heme iron**, which is 2–3x less bioavailable than heme iron from meat
- Phytates in whole grains, legumes, and seeds further inhibit iron absorption
- **Risk:** Iron-deficiency anemia, especially in menstruating women, pregnant women, and children

**Mitigation:**
- Pair iron-rich plant foods with **vitamin C** (e.g., lentils + tomatoes, spinach + lemon juice) — increases absorption 2–6x
- Cook in **cast iron** cookware
- Avoid coffee/tea with iron-rich meals (tannins inhibit absorption)
- Soak/sprout grains and legumes (reduces phytates)

### 3. Zinc Deficiency

- Plant zinc is less bioavailable due to phytate content
- **Risk:** Impaired immune function, delayed wound healing, hair loss, taste/smell changes

**Mitigation:**
- Include zinc-rich foods daily: pumpkin seeds, hemp seeds, chickpeas, lentils, cashews, tofu, oats
- Soak/sprout/ferment to reduce phytate content
- Supplement if needed (15–25 mg/day)

### 4. Omega-3 (DHA/EPA) Insufficiency

- Plant-based diets provide ALA (from flax, chia, walnuts, hemp) but ALA → EPA/DHA conversion is inefficient
- **Risk:** Suboptimal EPA/DHA status, potentially affecting cardiovascular health, brain health, and inflammation
- **Particularly important** during pregnancy and lactation for fetal brain development

**Mitigation:**
- Maximize ALA intake (1–2 tbsp ground flaxseed, chia, walnuts daily)
- Minimize omega-6 intake (avoid excess seed oils — they compete for conversion enzymes)
- **Take an algae-derived DHA/EPA supplement** (200–300 mg/day)

> **Citation:** Saunders AV, Davis BC, Garg ML. "Omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids and vegetarian diets." *Medical Journal of Australia*. 2013;199(4 Suppl):S22-S26.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.5694/mja13.11007

### 5. Calcium and Vitamin D (Bone Health Risk)

- Vegans have lower calcium intake if not consuming fortified foods or calcium-set tofu
- EPIC-Oxford showed **higher fracture risk in vegans** (particularly hip fractures)
- Vitamin D is naturally found mainly in animal foods (fatty fish, egg yolks, fortified dairy)

> **Citation:** Tong TY, Appleby PN, Armstrong ME, et al. "Vegetarian and vegan diets and risks of total and site-specific fractures: results from the prospective EPIC-Oxford study." *BMC Medicine*. 2020;18(1):35.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1186/s12916-020-1493-9

**Mitigation:**
- Consume calcium-set tofu, fortified plant milks, leafy greens (collards, kale, bok choy), tahini, fortified orange juice
- Supplement calcium if intake is below ~700mg/day
- Supplement vitamin D (1000–2000 IU/day) — universally recommended, not just for vegans

### 6. Iodine Deficiency

- Plant-based diets are often low in iodine (seafood and dairy are the main dietary sources)
- **Risk:** Hypothyroidism, goiter

**Mitigation:**
- Use **iodized salt** (in moderation)
- Include seaweed (nori, wakame, kelp — but **avoid excess kelp** due to potential iodine toxicity)
- Consider a supplement (150 mcg/day) if intake is uncertain

### 7. Protein Adequacy (Often Overstated but Worth Attention)

- **Myth:** "You can't get enough protein on a plant-based diet." **Reality:** Well-planned plant-based diets easily meet protein needs (~0.8–1.0 g/kg body weight)
- **Legitimate concern:** Elderly, athletes, pregnant women, and those recovering from illness may need **1.0–1.6 g/kg**
- High-protein plant foods: tofu, tempeh, seitan, lentils, beans, edamame, hemp seeds, nutritional yeast, pea protein

**Mitigation:**
- Ensure 2–3 servings of legumes/soy foods daily
- Include protein at every meal
- For athletes or high-protein needs: add plant protein powder (pea, rice, or soy isolate — 20–30g per serving)

### 8. "Junk Food Vegan" Risk

- A vegan diet consisting of French fries, vegan donuts, plant-based meat alternatives, and processed snacks is nutritionally poor
- Ultra-processed vegan foods can be high in sodium, refined oils, and additives
- **These foods undermine the health benefits** of plant-based eating
- **The evidence for health benefits is primarily for whole-food plant-based diets**, not technically-vegan junk food

## Special Populations

### Pregnancy & Lactation
- ✅ The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics states well-planned vegan/vegetarian diets are appropriate during pregnancy and lactation
- ⚠️ **Mandatory:** B12 supplementation (2.6 mcg/day pregnant, 2.8 mcg/day lactating — but therapeutic doses of 1000+ mcg are often used)
- ⚠️ **Critical:** DHA/EPA (algae-based, 200–300 mg/day) for fetal brain development
- ⚠️ **Monitor:** Iron (ferritin), folate (from supplements + leafy greens), iodine (150 mcg/day supplement)
- ⚠️ **Ensure:** Adequate calories and protein (caloric needs increase ~340–450/day in 2nd/3rd trimester)
- ⚠️ **Calcium:** 1000 mg/day — fortified plant milks, tofu, supplements if needed

> **Citation:** Sebastiani G, Herranz Barbero A, Borrás-Novell C, et al. "The effects of vegetarian and vegan diet during pregnancy on the health of mothers and offspring." *Nutrients*. 2019;11(3):557.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.3390/nu11030557

### Infants and Children
- ✅ The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics and the American Academy of Pediatrics support well-planned vegetarian/vegan diets for children
- ⚠️ **Energy density:** Plant foods are high-volume, low-calorie — children may struggle to eat enough volume to meet calorie needs. Include energy-dense foods (nut butters, avocado, hummus)
- ⚠️ **B12:** Mandatory supplement for vegan children
- ⚠️ **Iron and zinc:** Monitor closely; deficiencies can affect growth and cognitive development
- ⚠️ **Growth:** Studies show vegetarian children grow normally but may be slightly leaner. Severe restriction (raw vegan, macrobiotic) can impair growth
- ❌ **Raw vegan diets are NOT appropriate for infants and children** — risk of severe malnutrition

> **Note:** The case of severe malnutrition in exclusively breastfed infants of poorly-nourished vegan mothers has been reported (vitamin B12 deficiency causing irreversible neurological damage). **B12 supplementation in breastfeeding vegan mothers is absolutely critical.**

### Elderly
- ✅ Plant-based diets beneficial for chronic disease prevention in the elderly
- ⚠️ **Protein needs increase** with age (1.0–1.2 g/kg) — harder to achieve on plant-based diets
- ⚠️ **B12 absorption decreases** with age (atrophic gastritis) — all elderly benefit from B12 supplementation regardless of diet
- ⚠️ **Bone health:** Ensure adequate calcium, vitamin D, and weight-bearing exercise
- ⚠️ **Calorie sufficiency:** Reduced appetite in the elderly may make it hard to consume enough high-volume plant foods

### Athletes
- ✅ Well-planned plant-based diets can support athletic performance at all levels
- ⚠️ **Protein:** Aim for 1.2–2.0 g/kg depending on sport and training intensity
- ⚠️ **Iron:** Endurance athletes (especially female) are at higher risk of iron deficiency; monitor ferritin
- ⚠️ **Creatine:** Vegans have lower muscle creatine stores; supplementation (3–5g/day) may improve performance in high-intensity exercise
- ⚠️ **Calorie sufficiency:** High training volumes require adequate caloric intake; plant-based diets' low calorie density can be a challenge

> Notable vegan athletes: Venus Williams (tennis), Lewis Hamilton (F1), Novak Djokovic (tennis), Patrik Baboumian (strongman), Scott Jurek (ultramarathon)

### People with Diabetes
- ✅ **Strongly supported** by the American Diabetes Association — plant-based eating patterns are a recommended option
- ✅ Significant HbA1c improvements (−0.40%) and weight loss (−2.15 kg)
- ✅ May reduce or eliminate medication needs (under medical supervision)
- ⚠️ Monitor blood glucose closely when transitioning (medication adjustments may be needed)

### People with Cardiovascular Disease
- ✅ **Strong evidence** for prevention and potential regression (Ornish, Esselstyn programs)
- ✅ Dramatic LDL reduction
- ⚠️ Esselstyn's protocol (no added oils, no nuts) is stricter than general WFPB — discuss with cardiologist

## Cost-Benefit Summary

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Health benefits** | Strong for CVD, BP, diabetes, weight; moderate for cancer and mortality |
| **Evidence quality** | Grade B (strong observational + meta-analysis support; fewer long-term RCTs) |
| **Long-term sustainability** | Moderate-High — ethical vegans have excellent adherence; health-motivated adherence varies |
| **Safety** | Safe for all life stages **if properly planned and supplemented** (B12, D, DHA/EPA) |
| **Restrictiveness** | Moderate-High (especially vegan and WFPB) — social and dining-out challenges |
| **Cost** | Low-Moderate (beans, rice, seasonal produce are among the cheapest foods) |
| **Environmental impact** | **Lowest of any dietary pattern** (50–75% lower GHG emissions, water use, land use) |
| **Key risk** | B12 deficiency (preventable with supplementation) |
| **Overall recommendation** | **Recommended** — appropriate and beneficial for most people when properly planned |
