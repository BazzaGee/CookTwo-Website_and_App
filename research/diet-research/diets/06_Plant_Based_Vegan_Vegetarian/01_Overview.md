# Plant-Based / Vegan / Vegetarian Diet — Overview

## Definition

The plant-based diet family encompasses a spectrum of dietary patterns that emphasize foods derived from plants — vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and seeds — while reducing or eliminating animal products. This entry covers **three distinct but related sub-types**:

### Sub-Types at a Glance

| Sub-Type | Definition | Animal Products |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Vegetarian (Lacto-Ovo)** | No meat, poultry, or fish. Eggs and dairy are included. | Eggs ✅, Dairy ✅, Honey ✅ |
| **Vegan** | No animal products of any kind. Excludes all meat, fish, dairy, eggs, honey, gelatin, and other animal-derived ingredients. | None |
| **Plant-Based (WFPB)** | Predominantly whole, minimally processed plant foods. May include minimal animal products (flexible interpretation). The term "whole-food plant-based" (WFPB) emphasizes unprocessed foods. | Minimal / optional |

> **Terminology note:** "Plant-based" is sometimes used interchangeably with "vegan," but the distinction matters. A vegan diet excludes all animal products (an ethical/identity-based definition), while a whole-food plant-based (WFPB) diet emphasizes food *quality* (minimally processed plants) and may include trace animal products. Oreos and French fries are technically vegan but are not whole-food plant-based.

It is one of the **most studied dietary patterns** in nutrition science, with a large body of evidence from prospective cohorts (Adventist Health Studies, EPIC-Oxford), meta-analyses, and a growing number of randomized controlled trials.

## History & Origins

### Ancient Roots (6th Century BCE — 19th Century)

Plant-based eating has deep historical and philosophical roots spanning thousands of years:

- **Pythagoras (c. 570–495 BCE):** The Greek philosopher and mathematician was one of the earliest recorded advocates of vegetarianism in the Western tradition. He and his followers (the "Pythagoreans") abstained from meat based on beliefs about the transmigration of souls (metempsychosis) — the idea that human souls could reincarnate in animals. For nearly 2,000 years, vegetarians in the Western world were commonly called **"Pythagoreans."**

- **Buddhism and Jainism (c. 6th–5th century BCE):** In Asia, the principle of **ahimsa** (non-violence toward all living beings) formed the spiritual basis for vegetarianism. Jainism, in particular, prescribes strict vegetarianism (and in some sects, avoidance of root vegetables to prevent harm to soil organisms). Buddhist monastic traditions in East Asia (especially China, Vietnam, Japan) developed rich vegetarian cuisines.

- **Hinduism:** The Vedic traditions of India, particularly from the medieval period onward, promoted lacto-vegetarianism (dairy allowed, eggs and meat excluded). India remains the country with the largest vegetarian population in the world.

- **19th century:** The term **"vegetarian"** was coined in 1847 at the inaugural meeting of the **Vegetarian Society** in England, derived from the Latin *vegetus* (meaning "lively, vigorous" — not "vegetable"). Prior to this, plant-based eaters were called "Pythagoreans" or "vegetists."

### The Birth of Veganism (1944)

In November 1944, **Donald Watson** (1910–2005), a Yorkshire woodworker and former secretary of the Leicester Vegetarian Society, co-founded the **Vegan Society** with Elsie Shrigley. They coined the word **"vegan"** by taking the first three and last two letters of "vegetarian" — describing "the beginning and end of vegetarian."

> *"Veganism is a philosophy and way of living which seeks to exclude — as far as is possible and practicable — all forms of exploitation of, and cruelty to, animals for food, clothing or any other purpose; and by extension, promotes the development and use of animal-free alternatives for the benefit of animals, humans and the environment. In dietary terms it denotes the practice of dispensing with all products derived wholly or partly from animals."* — The Vegan Society, formal definition (1979, refined from Watson's original 1944 definition)

### The Modern Plant-Based Movement (1990s–Present)

The term **"plant-based"** was popularized in the 1990s–2000s, notably by:

- **T. Colin Campbell** (Cornell University), who used "plant-based diet" in his research on the **China-Cornell-Oxford Project** (the "China Study"), published in 2005
- **Caldwell Esselstyn Jr.** (Cleveland Clinic), who advocated a WFPB diet for heart disease reversal in his 2007 book *Prevent and Reverse Heart Disease*
- **Dean Ornish** (Preventive Medicine Research Institute), whose Program for Reversing Heart Disease (1990) used a near-vegan, very-low-fat approach
- **Michael Greger** (founder of NutritionFacts.org), whose 2015 book *How Not to Die* brought WFPB eating to mainstream audiences
- **The Planetary Health Diet** (EAT-Lancet Commission, 2019), which proposed a predominantly plant-based diet for both human and planetary health

## Core Philosophy

Plant-based eating is underpinned by several intersecting philosophies:

1. **Health optimization** — Plant foods are rich in fiber, antioxidants, phytochemicals, and beneficial plant compounds that are largely absent from animal products. Whole-food plant-based diets minimize processed foods, added oils, and refined sugars.

2. **Ethical / animal welfare** (particularly for veganism) — The conviction that sentient animals should not be exploited, harmed, or killed for human use when alternatives exist.

3. **Environmental sustainability** — Animal agriculture is a leading driver of greenhouse gas emissions, deforestation, water pollution, and biodiversity loss. Plant-based diets have a significantly lower environmental footprint (the EAT-Lancet Commission estimates a >50% reduction in food-system emissions).

4. **Food as medicine** — The WFPB movement emphasizes that dietary patterns can prevent, manage, and in some cases reverse chronic diseases (particularly cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes).

5. **Wholism over reductionism** — WFPB advocates argue that the benefits of plants come from the whole food matrix (synergistic effects of fiber, phytochemicals, and nutrients) rather than isolated nutrients or supplements.

## Key Components (The Plant-Based Plate)

### Base — Eat Abundantly (Every Meal)
- **Vegetables:** all varieties — leafy greens, cruciferous vegetables, root vegetables, nightshades, alliums
- **Fruits:** all varieties — berries, citrus, stone fruit, tropical fruit, apples, pears
- **Whole grains:** brown rice, oats, quinoa, barley, buckwheat, millet, whole wheat, corn
- **Legumes:** beans (black, kidney, pinto, navy), lentils (all colors), chickpeas, peas, soy foods (tofu, tempeh, edamame)
- **Nuts and seeds:** almonds, walnuts, cashews, Brazil nuts, flaxseeds, chia seeds, hemp seeds, pumpkin seeds, sunflower seeds
- **Tubers and starchy vegetables:** potatoes, sweet potatoes, squash, yams

### Moderate — Eat Daily (Vegan/WFPB)
- **Plant oils:** olive oil, avocado oil (WFPB purists minimize or exclude all added oils)
- **Plant milks:** fortified soy milk, oat milk, almond milk, pea milk
- **Plant-based protein concentrates:** seitan (wheat gluten), nutritional yeast, plant-based protein powders (pea, rice, hemp)
- **Fermented foods:** sauerkraut, kimchi, miso, kombucha
- **Soy foods:** tofu, tempeh, natto, soy milk (1–3 servings/day)

### Included by Sub-Type
| Food | Vegetarian (Lacto-Ovo) | Vegan | Plant-Based (flexible) |
|------|:---------------------:|:-----:|:---------------------:|
| Eggs | ✅ | ❌ | Minimal |
| Dairy (milk, cheese, yogurt) | ✅ | ❌ | Minimal |
| Fish | ❌ | ❌ | Optional (minimal) |
| Poultry | ❌ | ❌ | Optional (minimal) |
| Meat | ❌ | ❌ | Optional (minimal) |
| Honey | ✅ | ❌ | Variable |

### Flavoring
- **Herbs and spices:** unlimited — turmeric, cumin, coriander, paprika, cinnamon, ginger, garlic, basil, oregano, thyme, rosemary
- **Umami enhancers:** nutritional yeast, miso, tamari/soy sauce, mushrooms, tomato paste
- **Acids:** lemon juice, lime juice, vinegars (apple cider, balsamic, rice)

### Beverages
- Water (primary), coffee, tea (green, black, herbal)

## Variations & Sub-Types

### Dietary Sub-Types Within the Plant-Based Family

| Sub-Type | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Lacto-ovo vegetarian** | Most common Western vegetarian type. Excludes meat/fish/poultry; includes eggs and dairy. |
| **Lacto-vegetarian** | Excludes meat/fish/poultry/eggs; includes dairy. Common in India (Hindu tradition). |
| **Ovo-vegetarian** | Excludes meat/fish/poultry/dairy; includes eggs. Rare. |
| **Vegan** | Excludes all animal products. |
| **WFPB (Whole-Food Plant-Based)** | Emphasizes minimally processed plant foods; minimizes or eliminates added oils, refined sugars, and processed foods. May or may not be fully vegan. |
| **Raw vegan** | Vegan diet consisting of uncooked (or dehydrated below 118°F/48°C) plant foods. |
| **Junk-food vegan** | Vegan but reliant on ultra-processed meat/dairy alternatives, fries, snacks. Technically vegan but nutritionally poor. |
| **Macrobioitic** | Flexible plant-based diet emphasizing whole grains (~50–60%), vegetables (~25–30%), legumes/sea vegetables (~5–10%), with small amounts of fish/seafood occasionally. |
| **80/10/10 (Raw till 4 / HCLF)** | High-carb, low-fat raw or mostly-raw fruit-based diet (~80% carbs, 10% protein, 10% fat from fruit). Extremely restrictive. |

### Plant-Based Variants Emphasizing Specific Foods
| Variant | Distinctive Features |
|---------|---------------------|
| **Starch-based (McDougall)** | Centered on starchy foods (potatoes, rice, corn) as calorie base; very low fat (<10%) |
| **Nutritarian (Fuhrman)** | Emphasizes nutrient density scoring (ANDI); large volumes of green vegetables, beans, onions, mushrooms, berries, seeds (G-BOMBS) |
| **Ornish** | Near-vegan, very low fat (<10%), whole foods; also includes stress management and exercise |
| **Esselstyn** | Strictly WFPB, no added oils, no nuts for heart disease patients |

## Who It's For

Plant-based diets are appropriate for **most of the general population** when properly planned. They are:

- ✅ **Endorsed by:** the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics (the world's largest organization of food and nutrition professionals), which states that appropriately planned vegetarian and vegan diets are "healthful, nutritionally adequate, and may provide health benefits for the prevention and treatment of certain diseases" and are "appropriate for all stages of the life cycle, including pregnancy, lactation, infancy, childhood, adolescence, and older adulthood, and for athletes."

- ✅ **Suitable for:** adults, elderly, children, pregnant women (with attention to B12, iron, DHA), athletes (with attention to protein and calories), people with cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and those seeking weight management.

- ⚠️ **Caution for:**
  - People with **soy allergy** (many plant-based staples rely on soy)
  - Those with **nut/seed allergies** (a major protein/fat source in plant-based eating)
  - People requiring **high-calorie diets** (plant-based diets are often high-volume, low-calorie density — weight maintenance can be challenging without intentional calorie density)
  - Those with **GI conditions** (IBS/IBD) who may not tolerate high-fiber/high-FODMAP legumes and certain vegetables
  - People prone to **iron-deficiency anemia** (non-heme iron is less bioavailable)

- ❌ **Not ideal for:**
  - Individuals unwilling or unable to supplement **vitamin B12** (non-negotiable for vegans)
  - People in food environments with **limited access to plant-based protein sources** or fresh produce
  - Those with severe **malabsorption conditions** (e.g., short bowel syndrome, severe celiac disease) who need highly bioavailable animal protein

## Summary

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Origin** | Ancient (Pythagoras, Buddhism, Jainism — 6th century BCE); modern veganism (Donald Watson, 1944) |
| **First described scientifically** | Seventh-Day Adventist studies (1958–present); EPIC-Oxford (1993–present) |
| **Evidence grade** | **B** for cardiovascular disease, weight management, diabetes prevention; **B-C** for cancer (mixed) |
| **Restrictiveness** | Moderate-High (3/5) — Depends on sub-type (WFPB most restrictive; lacto-ovo less so) |
| **Primary health focus** | Cardiovascular disease prevention, metabolic health, weight management, cancer risk reduction |
| **Sustainability (adherence)** | Moderate — Ethical vegans have high adherence; health-motivated WFPB adherence varies |
| **Environmental impact** | Lowest of any dietary pattern (50–75% lower GHG emissions than omnivorous diets) |
| **Cost** | Low-Moderate (beans, rice, seasonal produce are inexpensive; specialty products can add cost) |
| **Key supplement** | **Vitamin B12** (mandatory for vegans; recommended for all plant-based eaters) |
| **AND position** | "Appropriate for all stages of the life cycle" (Melina et al., 2016) |
