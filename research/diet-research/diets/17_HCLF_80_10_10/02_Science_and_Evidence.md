# 80/10/10 (HCLF) Diet — Science & Evidence

> **Evidence Grade: D (Insufficient)** — The 80/10/10 diet has **no published clinical trials** testing its safety or efficacy. No studies have evaluated the diet as a whole for health outcomes. The evidence base consists of: (1) the author's theoretical framework (Graham 2006), which is not peer-reviewed science; (2) studies on low-fat plant-based diets more broadly (Ornish, McDougall, Esselstyn), which differ critically from 80/10/10 in that they allow cooked foods and are not exclusively fruit-based; and (3) observational studies on raw foodists, which show concerning rates of deficiencies and health problems. **The 80/10/10 diet as specifically practiced should be considered an untested, experimental dietary pattern.**

---

## Evidence Grading

| Domain | Grade | Basis |
|--------|-------|-------|
| The 80/10/10 diet itself (as a clinical intervention) | **D** | No clinical trials; only the author's book and anecdotal reports |
| Low-fat plant-based diets (Ornish, McDougall — related but different) | **B** | RCTs exist, but these diets allow cooked foods and are not fruit-based |
| Raw food diets (general) | **C** | Observational studies showing nutritional deficiencies and health concerns |
| Very high fruit intake | **C–D** | No long-term studies; dental erosion and blood sugar concerns |
| Vegan diet health outcomes (general) | **B** | Evidence supports well-planned vegan diets, but 80/10/10 is more extreme |
| Sustainability/adherence | **D** | Surveys show very high dropout rates from raw food diets |
| Safety of ≤10% fat intake | **C** | Ornish showed cardiovascular benefits, but his diet is not raw or fruit-only |

> **Critical distinction:** There IS evidence that **low-fat, whole-food plant-based diets** (Ornish, McDougall, Esselstyn) can improve cardiovascular health. However, these diets **differ fundamentally** from 80/10/10: they allow cooked foods, are based on **starches** (not primarily fruit), and are studied under medical supervision. The 80/10/10 diet is more extreme and **has not been tested**.

---

## The Evidence Gap

### What Does NOT Exist

1. **No RCT of the 80/10/10 diet.** No study has randomized participants to a raw vegan, fruit-based, ≤10% fat diet and compared health outcomes to a control diet.
2. **No long-term prospective cohort of 80/10/10 practitioners.** No study has followed raw fruit-based dieters over years to assess disease incidence, mortality, or nutritional status.
3. **No published meta-analysis of the 80/10/10 diet.** There is nothing to meta-analyze.
4. **No safety studies.** No study has systematically evaluated the risks of consuming 2,500–5,000+ calories per day from raw fruit over months or years.

### What the Author Cites (and Its Limitations)

Graham's book (*The 80/10/10 Diet*, 2006) cites a mixture of:
- **Animal studies** on high-fat diets (not directly applicable to humans)
- **Biochemical mechanisms** (e.g., fat-induced insulin resistance) — real but extrapolated beyond evidence
- **Personal anecdotes and testimonials** — not scientific evidence
- **Selective citations** from the low-fat plant-based literature (Ornish, Pritikin) — relevant to low-fat diets in general but not to the raw, fruit-specific 80/10/10 approach

## Related Studies (Low-Fat Plant-Based Diets — NOT 80/10/10, but relevant)

### 1. The Ornish Lifestyle Heart Trial

**The most-cited evidence for very low-fat plant-based eating.** Important: this diet is NOT raw and NOT exclusively fruit-based.

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Randomized controlled trial |
| **Participants** | 48 patients with moderate to severe coronary heart disease |
| **Duration** | 5 years |
| **Intervention** | ≤10% fat vegetarian diet + exercise + stress management + smoking cessation |
| **Result** | Regression of coronary atherosclerosis in the intervention group; **no change or progression** in control. 91% reduction in angina episodes. |
| **Key difference from 80/10/10** | Allowed cooked foods, included egg whites and some dairy; not fruit-based; not raw |

> **Citation:** Ornish D, Scherwitz LW, Billings JH, et al. "Intensive lifestyle changes for reversal of coronary heart disease." *JAMA*. 1998;280(23):2001-2007.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/jama.280.23.2001

> **Relevance to 80/10/10:** Demonstrates that ≤10% fat diets can improve cardiovascular health. However, the Ornish diet is cooked, includes some animal protein, and is medically supervised. It does NOT validate the raw, fruit-only 80/10/10 approach.

---

### 2. The McDougall Program

Dr. John McDougall's high-carb, very-low-fat plant-based diet — based on **cooked starches** (potatoes, rice, corn), not raw fruit.

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Approach** | Starch-centered vegan diet, ~70% carb, ~10% fat, ~20% protein |
| **Key difference from 80/10/10** | Cooked foods allowed; starches (not fruit) are the calorie base; more sustainable and nutritionally adequate |

> **Citation:** McDougall J, Litzau K, Haver E, Saunders V, Spiller GA. "Rapid reduction of serum cholesterol and blood pressure by a twelve-day, very low fat, strictly vegetarian diet." *Journal of the American College of Nutrition*. 1995;14(5):491-496.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/07315724.1995.10718529

---

### 3. Esselstyn's Plant-Based Diet for Heart Disease

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Approach** | Very low-fat (<10%) whole-food plant-based diet; NO oils, nuts, or avocados |
| **Result** | In a small observational study of 198 patients, those who adhered had very low rates of cardiovascular events over 12+ years |
| **Key difference from 80/10/10** | Cooked foods allowed; vegetables and whole grains (not just fruit) form the base; medically supervised |

> **Citation:** Esselstyn CB Jr, Gendy G, Doyle J, Golubic M, Roizen MF. "A way to reverse CAD." *Journal of Family Practice*. 2014;63(7):356-364b.
> **PubMed:** https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25198208/

## Studies on Raw Food Diets (General — Including Fruit-Based)

### 4. The Giessen Raw Food Study

**One of the largest studies of raw food diets.** Found significant health concerns.

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Design** | Cross-sectional study (part of the European Prospective Investigation into Cancer and Nutrition — EPIC) |
| **Participants** | 513 men and women consuming raw food diets (70%+ of intake raw) for ≥2 years |
| **Key findings** | (1) **Body weight decreased** with increasing % raw food; (2) **30% of women under 45 had amenorrhea** (loss of menstrual periods); (3) **Very low vitamin B12 levels**; (4) **Low bone mass** in long-term raw foodists |

> **Citation:** Koebnick C, Garcia AL, Dagnelie PC, et al. "Long-term consumption of a raw food diet is associated with favorable serum LDL cholesterol and triglycerides but also with elevated plasma homocysteine and low serum HDL cholesterol in humans." *Journal of Nutrition*. 2005;135(10):2370-2380.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/135.10.2370

> **Citation (bone health):** Fontana L, Shew JL, Holloszy JO, Villareal DT. "Low bone mass in subjects on a long-term raw vegetarian diet." *Archives of Internal Medicine*. 2005;165(6):684-689.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/archinte.165.6.684

**Significance:** This study showed that while raw food diets can produce favorable cholesterol levels, they are associated with **serious concerns**: menstrual disruption (indicating insufficient energy or nutrients), elevated homocysteine (indicating B12 deficiency), and low bone mass. These findings are directly relevant to 80/10/10 practitioners.

---

### 5. Vitamin B12 Deficiency in Vegans

**B12 deficiency is the single most documented nutritional risk of all vegan diets, including 80/10/10.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Finding** | Untreated vegans have very high rates of B12 deficiency. B12 is not present in any plant food. Raw fruit-based diets are particularly deficient because they exclude fortified foods. |
| **Consequences** | Megaloblastic anemia, peripheral neuropathy, irreversible neurological damage, cognitive impairment, elevated homocysteine (cardiovascular risk) |

> **Citation:** Pawlak R, Lester SE, Babatunde T. "The prevalence of cobalamin deficiency among vegetarians assessed by serum vitamin B12: a review of literature." *European Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2014;68(5):541-548.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/ejcn.2014.46

> **Citation:** Donaldson MS. "Metabolic vitamin B12 status on a mostly raw vegan diet with follow-up using tablets, nutritional yeast, or probiotic supplements." *Annals of Nutrition and Metabolism*. 2000;44(5-6):229-234.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1159/000046989

> **Position of major organizations:** The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics states that **all vegans must supplement with B12**.

> **Citation (position paper):** Melina V, Craig W, Levin S. "Position of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics: Vegetarian Diets." *Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics*. 2016;116(12):1970-1980.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jand.2016.09.025

## Key Meta-Analyses (Related Evidence)

### Vegan and Vegetarian Diets and Health (General)

> **Citation:** Dinu M, Abbate R, Gensini GF, Casini A, Sofi F. "Vegetarian, vegan diets and multiple health outcomes: A systematic review with meta-analysis of observational studies." *Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition*. 2017;57(17):3640-3649.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1080/10408398.2016.1138447
>
> **Findings:** Vegetarian and vegan diets are associated with reduced risk of ischemic heart disease and cancer incidence. However, this meta-analysis includes all types of vegetarian/vegan diets (including cooked, supplemented, medically-monitored) — it does NOT validate the raw, fruit-specific 80/10/10 diet specifically.

### Dietary Fat and Health

> **Citation:** De Souza RJ, Mente A, Maroleanu A, et al. "Intake of saturated and unsaturated fatty acids and risk of all cause mortality, cardiovascular disease, and type 2 diabetes: systematic review and meta-analysis of observational studies." *BMJ*. 2015;351:h3978.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1136/bmj.h3978
>
> **Findings:** This meta-analysis found that saturated fat was not clearly associated with all-cause mortality, and the relationship between total fat intake and health is more nuanced than "low fat is always better." This partially undercuts the 80/10/10 premise that all dietary fat above 10% is harmful.

## Mechanisms Claimed by the Diet (and Scientific Assessment)

### Claim 1: "Fat Causes Insulin Resistance"
- **What the diet claims:** Dietary fat, especially saturated fat, accumulates in muscle cells (intramyocellular lipid), impairing insulin signaling. Therefore, very low fat diets restore insulin sensitivity.
- **Scientific assessment:** **Partially supported.** There is evidence that high saturated fat intake contributes to insulin resistance (e.g., the work of Gerald Reaven, Neal Barnard). However, the claim that *all* fat above 10% is harmful is an oversimplification. Monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats (olive oil, nuts, avocados, fish oil) are consistently associated with improved metabolic health.

> **Citation (mechanism):** Cornwall AG, Gow ML, Garg ML, et al. "Long-chain n-3 PUFA and insulin sensitivity in overweight and obese adults: a meta-analysis." *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2015;102(2):375-385.

### Claim 2: "Humans Are Frugivores"
- **What the diet claims:** Human anatomy (teeth, digestive tract, inability to synthesize vitamin C) indicates we evolved to eat primarily fruit.
- **Scientific assessment:** **Not supported.** Anthropological evidence shows humans have been omnivores for at least 2 million years. Cooking was adopted ~1–2 million years ago and provided a major evolutionary advantage (increased calorie availability, reduced toxin exposure). Our short colon, relatively small teeth, and brain evolution are all linked to omnivorous, cooked-food diets.

> **Citation:** Wrangham R. *Catching Fire: How Cooking Made Us Human.* Basic Books; 2009.

### Claim 3: "Cooking Destroys Nutrients and Enzymes"
- **What the diet claims:** Heating food above 118°F destroys enzymes and reduces nutritional value.
- **Scientific assessment:** **Partially true but misleading.** Cooking does reduce some heat-sensitive nutrients (vitamin C, some B vitamins). However, cooking also: (1) increases bioavailability of other nutrients (lycopene, beta-carotene, lutein); (2) destroys anti-nutrients (phytic acid, lectins, oxalates); (3) kills pathogens; (4) makes many foods digestible that would otherwise be nearly inedible raw (grains, legumes, starchy tubers). The body produces its own digestive enzymes; plant enzymes are not needed for human digestion.

### Claim 4: "Fruit Sugar Is Fundamentally Different From Refined Sugar"
- **What the diet claims:** The fructose and glucose in whole fruit is healthy and can be consumed in unlimited quantities.
- **Scientific assessment:** **Partially supported.** Whole fruit contains fiber, water, vitamins, and antioxidants that moderate fructose absorption and provide health benefits. However, consuming massive quantities of fructose (e.g., 200+ grams/day from 20+ bananas or large quantities of dates) may strain hepatic fructose metabolism. The long-term effects of extremely high fruit sugar intake have not been studied.

> **Citation:** Hannou SA, Haslam DE, McKeown NM, Herman MA. "Fructose metabolism and metabolic disease." *Journal of Clinical Investigation*. 2018;128(2):545-555.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI96702

## Comparison with Evidence-Based Diets

| Comparison | 80/10/10 vs. | Key Difference |
|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| vs. Ornish | Ornish allows cooked foods, some egg whites/dairy, is evidence-based (RCT) and medically supervised; 80/10/10 is raw, fruit-only, and untested |
| vs. McDougall | McDougall is cooked-starch-based, not raw; more nutritionally adequate and sustainable; 80/10/10 is far more restrictive |
| vs. Mediterranean | Mediterranean has robust A-grade evidence (PREDIMED); 80/10/10 has D-grade evidence; Mediterranean includes healthy fats (olive oil, nuts, fish) |
| vs. General vegan | Well-planned vegan diets can be healthy with B12 supplementation; 80/10/10 is an extreme subset that is much harder to sustain nutritionally |

## Consensus Among Health Organizations

| Organization | Position on 80/10/10 specifically |
|-------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics** | Does not recommend raw food or fruit-based diets; states well-planned *cooked* vegan diets can be healthy with B12 supplementation |

| **Dietary Guidelines for Americans** | Does not recommend; guidelines include a wide range of fat intake (20–35%) |
| **No major medical body** | Endorses the 80/10/10 diet |

## Limitations & Caveats

1. **No direct evidence:** The single greatest limitation is the **complete absence of clinical trials** testing the 80/10/10 diet. All claims about its effects are extrapolated from other diets or based on anecdotes.
2. **Author's credentials:** Graham is a chiropractor, not a registered dietitian, medical doctor, or PhD-level nutrition researcher. His work has not been published in peer-reviewed nutrition journals.
3. **Selection bias in testimonials:** The people who succeed on 80/10/10 long enough to become advocates may be self-selected; those who fail (due to deficiencies, weight gain, health problems) are less visible. Several prominent former advocates have publicly abandoned the diet.
4. **Confounding in raw food studies:** Observational studies of raw foodists include diverse practices (not all are 80/10/10). Many raw foodists also consume significant fat from nuts, seeds, and oils — not representative of 80/10/10.
5. **Sustainability:** No study has demonstrated long-term (>2 years) adherence to a strict raw fruit-based diet in a significant population. Surveys of raw foodists consistently show high dropout rates.
6. **Potential for harm:** The combination of B12 deficiency, very high sugar intake, possible amenorrhea in women, and dental erosion represents real health risks that have been documented in raw food populations.

---

## Emerging Research Areas

- **Long-term effects of very high fruit intake on metabolic health** (fructose metabolism, NAFLD risk)
- **Raw food diets and the gut microbiome** (how large quantities of raw fruit/vegetables alter microbial composition)
- **Dental erosion from high-fruit diets** (case reports exist; systematic studies needed)
- **Bone health in long-term raw foodists** (Fontana et al. found low bone mass; more research needed)
- **Psychological aspects of very restrictive diets** (orthorexia, social isolation, relationship with food)
