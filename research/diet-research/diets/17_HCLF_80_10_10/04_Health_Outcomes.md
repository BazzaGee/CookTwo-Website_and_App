# 80/10/10 (HCLF) Diet — Health Outcomes

> Comprehensive summary of claimed and demonstrated health benefits, known risks, nutrient deficiencies, and medical guidelines for the 80/10/10 raw vegan diet. **This diet has NOT been clinically tested. Health claims are extrapolated from related diets or based on testimonials.**

---

## Claimed Health Benefits (With Evidence Assessment)

### 1. Weight Loss / Weight Management (Grade C — Anecdotal)

**Claim:** The diet naturally promotes weight loss due to low fat intake, high fiber, and high satiety from fruit volume.

| Outcome | Claimed Effect | Evidence Source | Scientific Assessment |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Initial weight loss | Rapid weight loss in first weeks–months | Testimonials; social media | **Plausible** — due to calorie deficit (many under-eat initially) and water weight from reduced sodium |
| Long-term weight management | Stable weight at high calorie intakes | Author's claims; anecdotes | **Unproven** — no long-term studies; some practitioners report weight gain when eating ad libitum |

> **Scientific context:** While high-fiber, low-fat plant-based diets can support weight management (evidenced by Ornish and McDougall studies), the claim that one can eat unlimited calories without weight gain is **not supported by thermodynamics or clinical evidence**. Some 80/10/10 practitioners consume 3,000–5,000+ calories per day; sustained caloric surplus on any diet will lead to weight gain.

---

### 2. Improved Insulin Sensitivity (Grade C — Plausible Mechanism, Untested for 80/10/10)

**Claim:** Very low fat intake (<10%) improves insulin sensitivity by reducing intramyocellular lipid accumulation.

| Outcome | Claimed Effect | Evidence Source | Scientific Assessment |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Insulin sensitivity | Improvement | Ornish and McDougall data (related diets); theoretical mechanism | **Plausible** — low-fat plant-based diets DO improve insulin sensitivity. But 80/10/10 specifically is untested, and the extremely high sugar intake raises concerns. |

> **Citation (related evidence):** Barnard ND, Cohen J, Jenkins DJ, et al. "A low-fat vegan diet improves glycemic control and cardiovascular risk factors in a randomized clinical trial in individuals with type 2 diabetes." *Diabetes Care*. 2006;29(8):1777-1783.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.2337/dc06-0606

> **Note:** Barnard's low-fat vegan diet was cooked, included soy products and legumes, and was NOT raw or fruit-based. It cannot be assumed that 80/10/10 produces identical results.

---

### 3. Improved Cardiovascular Risk Markers (Grade C — Indirect)

**Claim:** Very low fat, no cholesterol intake → optimal lipid panel.

| Outcome | Claimed Effect | Evidence Source | Scientific Assessment |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Total cholesterol | Very low (often <150 mg/dL) | Giessen Raw Food Study; raw foodist case reports | **Supported** — raw food diets do produce very low cholesterol |
| LDL cholesterol | Very low | Same | **Supported** |
| HDL cholesterol | **Also low** | Giessen Raw Food Study | ⚠️ **Concerning** — very low HDL may not be beneficial |
| Triglycerides | Favorable | Giessen study showed favorable TG | **Mixed** — some raw foodists have elevated triglycerides from high fructose |

> **Citation:** Koebnick C, Garcia AL, Dagnelie PC, et al. "Long-term consumption of a raw food diet is associated with favorable serum LDL cholesterol and triglycerides but also with elevated plasma homocysteine and low serum HDL cholesterol in humans." *Journal of Nutrition*. 2005;135(10):2370-2380.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1093/jn/135.10.2370

> **Note the title:** "...favorable LDL and triglycerides BUT ALSO elevated homocysteine AND low HDL." The elevated homocysteine indicates B12 deficiency (cardiovascular risk factor), and the low HDL is concerning — the relationship between very low HDL and cardiovascular risk is debated but is generally not considered optimal.

---

### 4. Increased Energy and Athletic Performance (Grade D — Anecdotal Only)

**Claim:** Followers report increased energy, mental clarity, and athletic performance.

| Outcome | Claimed Effect | Evidence Source | Scientific Assessment |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Energy levels | Increased | Testimonials | **Anecdotal** — no controlled studies; placebo and selection bias likely |
| Athletic performance | Enhanced endurance | Author's claims; anecdotes | **Unproven** — some athletes report initial improvement, others report fatigue from under-eating |

> **Note:** Graham has coached some elite athletes (e.g., tennis players) on raw food approaches. However, no peer-reviewed study has demonstrated athletic performance benefits from a raw fruit-based diet.

---

### 5. Reduced Inflammation (Grade D — Theoretical Only)

**Claim:** High antioxidant intake from fruit reduces inflammation.

| Outcome | Claimed Effect | Evidence Source | Scientific Assessment |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| CRP, inflammatory markers | Reduced | Theoretical (high fruit/antioxidant intake) | **Plausible but untested** for 80/10/10 specifically. Other plant-based diets do reduce inflammation. |

---

### 6. Digestive Health (Grade C — Mixed)

**Claim:** High fiber, enzyme-rich raw foods promote optimal digestion.

| Outcome | Claimed Effect | Evidence Source | Scientific Assessment |
|---------|---------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Constipation | Prevented | Abundant fiber + water from fruit | **Supported** — extremely high fiber intake prevents constipation |
| Bloating/gas | Some report improvement; many report worsening | Anecdotes | **Mixed** — enormous fruit volumes can cause significant GI distress, bloating, and gas, especially initially |
| Nutrient absorption | Claimed to be optimal | Theoretical (raw enzymes) | **Unproven** — raw foods may have LOWER bioavailability for some nutrients due to intact cell walls and anti-nutrients |

## Known Risks & Serious Health Concerns

### 1. Vitamin B12 Deficiency — ❗ CRITICAL

**The most serious and well-documented risk of all vegan diets, including 80/10/10.**

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Risk** | Essentially 100% without supplementation |
| **Timeline** | Stores may last 1–5 years; deficiency develops gradually |
| **Consequences** | Megaloblastic anemia, peripheral neuropathy (tingling, numbness), irreversible neurological damage, cognitive impairment, dementia-like symptoms, elevated homocysteine (cardiovascular risk) |
| **Detection** | Serum B12, methylmalonic acid (MMA), homocysteine |

> **Citation:** Pawlak R, Lester SE, Babatunde T. "The prevalence of cobalamin deficiency among vegetarians assessed by serum vitamin B12: a review of literature." *European Journal of Clinical Nutrition*. 2014;68(5):541-548.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1038/ejcn.2014.46

> **Warning:** B12 neurological damage can be **irreversible** if not caught early. All 80/10/10 practitioners must supplement.

---

### 2. Amenorrhea (Loss of Menstrual Periods) in Women — ❗ SERIOUS

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Risk** | The Giessen Raw Food Study found **30% of women under 45 had amenorrhea** |
| **Causes** | Insufficient total calories, insufficient fat (needed for hormone synthesis), low body fat percentage, stress |
| **Consequences** | Infertility, bone loss (osteoporosis risk), hormonal disruption, cardiovascular effects |

> **Citation:** Koebnick C, et al. *Journal of Nutrition*. 2005;135(10):2370-2380 (Giessen Raw Food Study).

> This is one of the most concerning findings from raw food diet research. Amenorrhea is a sign that the body does not have sufficient energy/nutrients to support reproductive function — it is not a sign of "cleansing" or health as some raw food advocates claim.

---

### 3. Low Bone Mass / Osteopenia — ❗ SERIOUS

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Risk** | Long-term raw foodists showed significantly lower bone mass |
| **Causes** | Low calcium intake, low vitamin D, low protein, possible low BMI, amenorrhea in women |
| **Consequences** | Osteoporosis, fracture risk |

> **Citation:** Fontana L, Shew JL, Holloszy JO, Villareal DT. "Low bone mass in subjects on a long-term raw vegetarian diet." *Archives of Internal Medicine*. 2005;165(6):684-689.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1001/archinte.165.6.684

---

### 4. Dental Erosion — ⚠️ SIGNIFICANT

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Risk** | High and frequent consumption of acidic and sugary fruit erodes tooth enamel |
| **Causes** | Citrus (low pH), constant snacking on fruit throughout the day, sticky dried fruit (dates) |
| **Consequences** | Enamel erosion, tooth sensitivity, cavities, eventual tooth loss |

> While not extensively studied in clinical trials, dental erosion from high-fruit diets is widely reported by dentists treating raw food practitioners. The combination of acidity (citrus, pineapple) and sugar (bananas, dates) consumed frequently throughout the day creates an ideal environment for enamel destruction.

> **Citation (related):** Bashir E, Lagerlöf F, Sundin B, Birkhed D. "Effect of consumption of soft drinks on [Ca2+] in dental plaque and on saliva." *Caries Research*. 1995;29(2):145-149.

---

### 5. Elevated Homocysteine — ⚠️ CARDIOVASCULAR RISK

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Risk** | The Giessen study found elevated plasma homocysteine in raw foodists |
| **Cause** | B12 deficiency (homocysteine metabolism requires B12) |
| **Consequences** | Independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease and stroke |

> **Citation:** Koebnick C, et al. *Journal of Nutrition*. 2005;135(10):2370-2380.

---

### 6. Extremely High Fructose Intake — ⚠️ UNRESOLVED CONCERN

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Intake** | 200–500+ grams of sugar (mostly fructose and glucose) per day from fruit |
| **Concern** | Potential strain on hepatic fructose metabolism; possible non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) risk with very high chronic fructose intake |
| **Counter-argument** | Whole fruit fiber slows absorption; fruit provides antioxidants that may offset fructose damage |
| **Scientific status** | **No long-term studies** of fruit-based diets at these fructose intake levels. The effects are genuinely unknown. |

> **Citation:** Hannou SA, Haslam DE, McKeown NM, Herman MA. "Fructose metabolism and metabolic disease." *Journal of Clinical Investigation*. 2018;128(2):545-555.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1172/JCI96702

---

### 7. Social Isolation and Psychological Impact — ⚠️ SIGNIFICANT

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Risk** | Extreme dietary restriction can lead to social isolation, difficulty dining out, travel challenges, relationship strain |
| **Orthorexia risk** | The rigid rules of 80/10/10 may contribute to orthorexia nervosa (unhealthy obsession with "clean" eating) |
| **Quality of life** | Significant impact on social, professional, and family life |

---

### 8. Under-eating and Malnutrition — ⚠️ COMMON

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Risk** | Many beginners cannot physically eat enough fruit to meet caloric needs → chronic calorie deficit → fatigue, weight loss, muscle loss, hormonal disruption |
| **Causes** | Fruit is bulky and filling; eating 2,500+ calories of fruit requires enormous physical volume |
| **Frequency** | Very common in the first months; some never adapt |

---

### 9. Very Low Sodium Intake — ⚠️ POTENTIAL CONCERN

| Detail | |
|--------|---|
| **Intake** | Often <500 mg sodium/day (from produce alone) |
| **Concern** | While low sodium is generally beneficial, extremely low sodium can cause hyponatremia, especially with high water intake and sweating (athletes) |
| **Symptoms** | Fatigue, headache, nausea, muscle cramps, in severe cases seizures |

## Common Nutrient Deficiencies (Comprehensive)

| Nutrient | Risk Level | Consequence | Solution |
|----------|:---------:|-------------|----------|
| **Vitamin B12** | ❗ **Critical** | Neurological damage, anemia | Supplement (250–500 mcg/day) — **MANDATORY** |
| **Vitamin D** | ❗ **High** | Bone loss, immune dysfunction | Supplement D3 (1,000–4,000 IU/day); sun exposure |
| **Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)** | ❗ **High** | Cardiovascular, cognitive, inflammatory effects | Algae-based DHA/EPA supplement (200–300 mg/day) |
| **Calcium** | ⚠️ **Moderate-High** | Osteoporosis, bone fractures | Large volumes of greens; calcium supplement if inadequate |
| **Iron** | ⚠️ **Moderate** (higher in women) | Anemia, fatigue | Monitor; pair plant iron with vitamin C (abundant) |
| **Zinc** | ⚠️ **Moderate** | Immune dysfunction, impaired wound healing | Monitor; supplement if low |
| **Iodine** | ⚠️ **Moderate** | Thyroid dysfunction | Kelp/iodine supplement or use iodized salt sparingly |
| **Selenium** | ⚠️ **Moderate** (soil-dependent) | Thyroid, immune function | 1–2 Brazil nuts/day (if allowed) or supplement |
| **Vitamin K2** | ⚠️ **Moderate** | Bone and cardiovascular health | Fermented foods (if allowed) or supplement |
| **Protein (quality)** | ⚠️ **Low-Moderate** | Muscle loss at inadequate calorie intake | Adequate total calories; ensure amino acid diversity |

## Special Populations

### Pregnancy & Lactation
- ❌ **NOT RECOMMENDED** during pregnancy or lactation without close medical supervision
- B12, iron, calcium, vitamin D, DHA, and iodine requirements increase dramatically during pregnancy
- The physical volume of food required may make it impossible to meet increased caloric needs
- Amenorrhea risk indicates reproductive dysfunction — women who have amenorrhea on this diet cannot conceive
- **Risk of irreversible fetal neurological damage from B12 deficiency**

### Children & Adolescents
- ❌ **NOT RECOMMENDED** for children without expert pediatric and nutritional supervision
- Growing children have high needs for calories, protein, calcium, iron, zinc, B12, and vitamin D
- The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics states that well-planned vegetarian/vegan diets CAN be appropriate for children, but 80/10/10 is far more restrictive than a standard vegan diet
- Case reports of severe malnutrition in children fed raw food diets exist

### Elderly
- ⚠️ **High risk** — decreased digestive capacity, difficulty consuming large volumes of food, increased B12 malabsorption with age, bone health concerns
- Not recommended without medical supervision

### Athletes
- ⚠️ **Mixed** — some endurance athletes report good results; many report fatigue and under-recovery
- The high carbohydrate content could theoretically support endurance training
- Protein needs for strength athletes may not be met
- Extreme caloric volume can cause GI distress during training
- Sodium depletion is a risk with sweating + very low sodium intake

### People with Diabetes
- ⚠️ **Caution** — the extremely high sugar/carbohydrate intake (80%+ carbs, 200+g sugar) makes glycemic management very challenging
- Although whole fruit has fiber that moderates blood sugar response, the sheer quantity consumed is extraordinary
- Some 80/10/10 advocates claim the diet "cures" diabetes — this is **not supported by evidence**
- **Anyone with diabetes should NOT undertake this diet without endocrinologist supervision**

### People with Eating Disorder History
- ❌ **NOT RECOMMENDED** — the extreme rigidity and food rules of 80/10/10 can trigger or worsen disordered eating patterns
- The diet's emphasis on food purity, restriction, and large-volume eating can mask or reinforce eating disorder behaviors

## Medical Organization Guidelines

| Organization | Position on 80/10/10 / Raw Vegan Fruit-Based Diets |
|-------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| **Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics** | Well-planned *cooked* vegan diets are appropriate for all life stages; **does not endorse raw food or fruit-based diets**; mandates B12 supplementation for all vegans |
| **American Heart Association** | Does not recommend; the diet's ≤10% fat target is more restrictive than AHA guidance and eliminates beneficial fats |
| **American Diabetes Association** | Does not recommend; extremely high carbohydrate intake complicates glycemic management |
| **WHO** | Does not recommend; advocates for balanced diet including healthy fats |
| **Dietary Guidelines for Americans** | Does not recommend; guidelines specify 20–35% fat intake |
| **No major medical body** | Endorses or recommends the 80/10/10 diet |

> **Citation (vegan position paper):** Melina V, Craig W, Levin S. "Position of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics: Vegetarian Diets." *Journal of the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics*. 2016;116(12):1970-1980.
> **DOI:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jand.2016.09.025

## Risk-Benefit Summary

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|-----------|
| **Evidence quality** | Very Low (Grade D) — no clinical trials of the diet itself |
| **Health benefits (demonstrated)** | None directly demonstrated for 80/10/10; related low-fat plant-based diets (Ornish, McDougall) have benefits but differ critically |
| **Nutritional adequacy** | Poor — multiple deficiencies without aggressive supplementation |
| **Safety** | Concerning — documented risks including B12 deficiency, amenorrhea, low bone mass, elevated homocysteine, dental erosion |
| **Sustainability** | Very Low — extremely difficult to maintain long-term; high dropout rates |
| **Cost** | Very High — large volumes of quality fresh fruit required daily |
| **Restrictiveness** | Extreme (5/5) — raw vegan, no cooked foods, fruit-based |
| **Social impact** | Significant — dining out, travel, social events all extremely challenging |
| **Overall recommendation** | **Not recommended** as a dietary pattern. Individuals who choose to follow it must supplement with B12 (mandatory), vitamin D, and DHA/EPA; undergo regular blood monitoring; and work with a healthcare provider |
