# 80/10/10 (HCLF) Diet — Overview

## Definition

The 80/10/10 diet — also known as the **High-Carb Low-Fat (HCLF) raw vegan diet** — is a dietary pattern created by **Dr. Douglas N. Graham**, a chiropractor and raw food advocate. The name refers to the target macronutrient ratio:

- **80% carbohydrates**
- **10% protein**
- **10% fat**

(as percentages of total caloric intake)

It is a **raw vegan** diet, meaning:
- **No animal products** of any kind (no meat, fish, dairy, eggs, honey)
- **No cooked foods** — foods are not heated above approximately 118°F (48°C)
- The diet is based overwhelmingly on **raw fruit**, which provides the vast majority of calories
- Supplemented with **tender leafy greens** and a very small amount of **raw nuts, seeds, and avocado** (overt fats)

> The 80/10/10 diet is one of the most restrictive dietary patterns in popular practice. It represents the extreme end of the plant-based spectrum and has a limited and controversial evidence base.

## History & Origins

### Douglas Graham and the Development of 80/10/10

| Year | Milestone |
|------|-----------|
| **1980s** | Douglas Graham, DC (chiropractor), begins promoting raw food and fruit-based diets through lectures and coaching, drawing on earlier Natural Hygiene movement principles |
| **1990s** | Graham refines his approach, emphasizing the 80/10/10 macronutrient ratio and the concept that humans are "frugivores" |
| **2006** | Publication of ***The 80/10/10 Diet*** by Douglas N. Graham — the definitive text for the diet |
| **2010s** | The diet gains significant online visibility through social media personalities, most notably **Freelee the Banana Girl** (Freelee Fruition) and **Durianrider** (Harley Johnstone), who popularized the "banana girl" movement and Raw Till 4 variant |
| **2015–2020s** | Increased scrutiny from dietitians and medical professionals about nutritional adequacy and long-term health effects; some early adopters publicly abandon the diet |

### Intellectual Lineage

The 80/10/10 diet draws on several traditions:

- **Natural Hygiene Movement:** A 19th–20th century health philosophy (Sylvester Graham, Herbert Shelton, T.C. Fry) that advocated raw plant foods, fasting, and natural living. Graham (no relation to Douglas Graham) was an early advocate of whole grains and vegetarianism.
- **Raw foodism:** The belief that cooking destroys nutrients and enzymes, and that humans are biologically adapted to eat raw plant foods.
- **Fruitarianism:** A subset of raw veganism where fruit provides the majority of calories. Historical advocates include Arnold Ehret (1866–1922).
- **Low-fat plant-based diets:** Related to but more extreme than Nathan Pritikin's <10% fat diet and Dr. John McDougall's starch-based diet. Unlike McDougall (which allows cooked starches) and Pritikin (which allows some animal protein), 80/10/10 is strictly raw and strictly vegan.

### The Book

> **Reference:** Graham DN. *The 80/10/10 Diet: Balancing Your Health, Your Weight, and Your Life One Luscious Bite at a Time.* Keyport, NJ: FoodnSport Press; 2006.

The book outlines the theoretical framework for the diet, including:
- The argument that humans are **physiological frugivores** (fruit is our optimal food)
- The claim that fat intake above ~10% of calories is harmful
- Detailed guidance on how to eat 2,000–5,000+ calories from raw fruit
- Meal plans and food combining rules

## Core Philosophy

The 80/10/10 diet is built on several core beliefs:

### 1. Humans Are Frugivores
Graham argues that human anatomy, physiology, and evolutionary history indicate that we are **biologically adapted to eat primarily fruit**, with some tender greens. He cites:
- Our relatively simple digestive tract (compared to herbivores)
- Our sweet-seeking taste receptors
- Our inability to synthesize vitamin C (like other fruit-eating primates)
- The ease with which humans can obtain calories from sweet fruit

> **Scientific critique:** Most biologists and anthropologists classify humans as **omnivores**. The frugivore argument is not supported by mainstream evolutionary biology or nutritional science.

### 2. Carbohydrates Are the Preferred Fuel
The diet holds that glucose (from fruit carbohydrates) is the body's **preferred and optimal fuel source**, and that dietary fat above ~10% of calories impairs cellular function, insulin sensitivity, and oxygen transport in the blood.

### 3. Fat Is the Enemy
Graham argues that dietary fat — even from "healthy" sources like nuts, seeds, avocado, and oils — should be minimized to ≤10% of calories. He contends that:
- High fat intake causes insulin resistance (by causing intracellular lipid accumulation)
- High fat intake impairs blood flow and oxygen delivery
- Nuts and seeds are difficult to digest and should be eaten rarely
- Oils are refined, processed foods that should be avoided entirely

### 4. Cooking Is Harmful
The diet maintains that cooking food above ~118°F (48°C):
- Destroys naturally occurring enzymes (the body's own digestive enzymes are claimed to be insufficient)
- Creates toxic compounds (advanced glycation end products, acrylamide, etc.)
- Destroys vitamins and antioxidants
- Makes food less recognizable to the body

> **Scientific critique:** While some nutrients are lost in cooking, cooking also increases the bioavailability of many nutrients (e.g., lycopene in tomatoes, beta-carotene in carrots) and destroys harmful bacteria and anti-nutrients. The raw food premise is not supported by mainstream nutrition science.

### 5. Caloric Abundance, Not Restriction
Unlike most diets that emphasize calorie restriction, 80/10/10 followers are encouraged to eat **large quantities** of food — often 2,500–5,000+ calories per day, primarily from fruit. The idea is that by eating low-fat, high-carbohydrate foods, excess calories do not cause weight gain.

> **Scientific critique:** The laws of thermodynamics apply regardless of macronutrient composition. While very low-fat, high-fiber diets can make overeating harder due to volume, sustained caloric surplus will lead to weight gain on any diet.

## Key Components (What You Actually Eat)

### The Core Foods

| Food Category | Role in Diet | % of Calories |
|---------------|--------------|:-------------:|
| **Sweet fruit** | Primary calorie source | ~70–90% |
| **Tender leafy greens** | Mineral and micronutrient source | ~2–5% of calories (large by volume) |
| **Non-sweet fruit** (tomatoes, cucumbers, bell peppers) | Volume and variety | Small % of calories |
| **Other vegetables** (celery, etc.) | Minerals, volume | Minimal calories |
| **Overt fats** (avocado, nuts, seeds, young coconut) | Limited fat source | <10% of calories |

### What a Typical Day Looks Like

| Meal | Typical Foods | Approximate Calories |
|------|---------------|:--------------------:|
| Breakfast | 10–15 bananas (mono-meal) or 1–2 large melons | 1,000–1,500 |
| Lunch | 8–12 bananas OR large quantity of dates, mangoes, grapes | 1,000–1,500 |
| Dinner | Large green salad with fruit-based dressing + more fruit (e.g., 5+ mangoes) | 800–1,200 |
| **Overt fats** | ½ avocado OR 1 oz nuts/seeds (a few times per week maximum) | 100–250 |

> **Total daily intake:** Typically 2,500–4,000+ calories, with some practitioners reporting 5,000+ calories per day.

### The "Mono-Meal"

A signature practice of the 80/10/10 diet is the **mono-meal** — eating a single type of fruit in large quantity as an entire meal. The most famous example is the "banana mono-meal" (10–30 bananas in one sitting). Advocates claim mono-meals are easiest to digest and most nutritionally efficient.

## Variations & Sub-Types

| Variation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Classic 80/10/10** | Strictly raw vegan, ≤10% fat, ≤10% protein. The original Graham protocol. |
| **Raw Till 4** | A popular variant that allows a **cooked vegan dinner** (typically high-carb starches like rice, potatoes, or pasta) while eating raw fruit during the day. Popularized by Freelee the Banana Girl. Not technically 80/10/10 but closely related. |
| **Raw Till 4 (high-fat variant)** | Some Raw Till 4 practitioners include more fat in their cooked evening meal. |
| **Cooked HCLF (McDougall-style)** | High-carb, low-fat plant-based but **not raw** — based on cooked starches (potatoes, rice, corn). Much more sustainable than 80/10/10. |
| **Starch Solution** | Dr. John McDougall's version of HCLF — cooked starch-based, allows some cooked vegetables, still low-fat. |

### Relationship to Other Diets

| Diet | Relationship to 80/10/10 |
|------|--------------------------|
| **McDougall** | Similar macro ratio but allows cooked starches; more sustainable |
| **Pritikin** | Similar low-fat principle but allows cooked foods and some animal protein |
| **Ornish** | Similar low-fat (≤10%) principle but allows cooked foods and is evidence-based |
| **Standard vegan** | 80/10/10 is a subset of veganism (raw + HCLF) but much more restrictive |

## Who It's For

- ⚠️ **Not recommended by mainstream medical or nutritional organizations.** No major health organization endorses a raw vegan, fruit-based diet as optimal for health.
- ⚠️ **Sustainability concerns:** The diet is extremely difficult to maintain long-term. Published case studies and surveys of raw foodists show high attrition rates.
- ⚠️ **Nutritional adequacy:** Without supplementation, the diet is deficient in vitamin B12, vitamin D, and likely iron, zinc, calcium, iodine, and omega-3 fatty acids.
- ❌ **Not suitable for:** Children, pregnant or lactating women, people with diabetes (due to extremely high sugar intake), people with eating disorder history, elderly individuals, or anyone with malabsorption issues — without close medical supervision.
- ⚠️ **Requires supplementation:** Vitamin B12 supplementation is **mandatory** on this diet (as with all vegan diets). Additional supplementation (vitamin D, algae-based DHA/EPA, possibly zinc and iodine) is strongly recommended.
- ⚠️ **Cost and logistics:** Consuming 2,500–4,000+ calories from fresh, ripe fruit daily is expensive and requires constant access to large quantities of high-quality produce.
