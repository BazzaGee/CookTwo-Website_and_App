# 80/10/10 (HCLF) Diet — Food Framework

> Practical guide to what to eat, avoid, and limit on the 80/10/10 raw vegan diet, including macronutrient targets, food categories, and sample meal structures.

---

## Macronutrient Targets

| Macronutrient | Target (% of total calories) | Notes |
|---------------|:----------------------------:|-------|
| **Carbohydrates** | **≥80%** | Overwhelmingly from raw sweet fruit |
| **Protein** | **~10%** (range 5–10%) | From fruit, greens, small amounts of nuts/seeds |
| **Fat** | **≤10%** (ideally ~5%) | From occasional avocado, nuts, seeds; NO oils |
| **Saturated fat** | <3% | Naturally very low |
| **Fiber** | 40–70+ g/day | Extremely high (from large fruit/vegetable intake) |
| **Sodium** | <500 mg/day (often) | Naturally very low — no added salt |
| **Water** | 3–5+ liters/day | From fruit + intentional drinking |

> The 80/10/10 ratio is **prescribed by the diet** and is its defining feature. Followers track their intake (often using apps like Cronometer) to ensure they meet the 80/10/10 target. The fat target is the hardest to stay under — most whole plant foods contain some fat, so even a diet of all fruit and greens typically provides 4–8% of calories from fat.

### Typical Daily Caloric Intake

| Activity Level | Estimated Calories | Approximate Fruit Equivalent |
|----------------|-------------------:|------------------------------|
| Sedentary | ~2,000–2,500 | 15–20 bananas or equivalent |
| Active | ~2,500–3,500 | 20–30 bananas or equivalent |
| Very active (athletes) | ~3,500–5,000+ | 30–50 bananas or equivalent |

> **Note:** The diet does NOT emphasize calorie restriction. Followers are encouraged to eat **abundantly** to ensure sufficient calorie intake from low-calorie-density fruit.

## Foods to Eat (The Core)

### Sweet Fruit — The Primary Calorie Source (Eat Abundantly)

Sweet fruit provides **70–90% of total calories** on the 80/10/10 diet. Followers eat fruit in very large quantities.

| Fruit | Calories per Unit | Typical Daily Quantity | Notes |
|-------|----------------:|----------------------:|-------|
| **Bananas** | ~105 per medium | 10–30/day | The staple of the diet; "banana girl" fame |
| **Dates** | ~66 per date (Medjool) | 10–30/day | Calorie-dense; used for calorie needs |
| **Mangoes** | ~200 per mango | 5–10/day | Popular in season |
| **Grapes** | ~104 per cup | 5–10 cups/day | |
| **Watermelon** | ~46 per cup | ½–1 whole melon/day | High volume, lower calorie |
| **Cantaloupe** | ~54 per cup | ½–1 whole melon/day | |
| **Papaya** | ~120 per medium | 2–5/day | |
| **Pineapple** | ~82 per cup | 1–2 whole/day | |
| **Peaches, nectarines** | ~60 per medium | 5–15/day | Seasonal |
| **Apples** | ~95 per medium | 5–15/day | |
| **Oranges** | ~62 per medium | 8–15/day | |
| **Figs** | ~37 per fig | 10–20/day | Seasonal |
| **Persimmons** | ~118 per medium | 5–10/day | Seasonal; popular in fall |
| **Berries** | ~50–80 per cup | Several cups/day | Lower calorie — eat larger volumes |
| **Cherimoya, sapodilla, jackfruit** | Varies | Varies | Tropical/exotic fruits emphasized |
| **Durian** | Varies | Occasional | High in calories and fat; controversial |

### Tender Leafy Greens — Mineral Source (Eat Daily, Large Volume)

Leafy greens provide calcium, iron, magnesium, and other minerals. They contribute very few calories but are eaten in large volumes.

| Food | Serving Size | Frequency |
|------|:------------:|-----------|
| Romaine lettuce | 1 large head | Daily |
| Spinach | 2+ cups | Daily |
| Kale | 2+ cups | Daily |
| Arugula | 2+ cups | Daily |
| Mixed greens | Large bowl | Daily |

### Non-Sweet Fruit & Vegetables (Eat Daily)

| Food | Role | Frequency |
|------|------|-----------|
| Tomatoes | Volume, vitamins, savory flavor | Daily |
| Cucumbers | Hydration, volume | Daily |
| Bell peppers | Vitamin C, volume | Daily |
| Celery | Sodium source (natural), minerals | Daily |
| Zucchini | Volume | Daily |

### Overt Fats (Eat Sparingly — ≤10% of Calories)

| Food | Max Quantity | Frequency | Fat Contribution |
|------|:------------:|-----------|:----------------:|
| Avocado | ½ medium | 1–3x/week | ~15g fat per ½ |
| Nuts (walnuts, almonds, etc.) | 1 oz (~28g) | 1–3x/week | ~14g fat per oz |
| Seeds (sunflower, pumpkin, hemp) | 1 oz | 1–3x/week | ~14g fat per oz |
| Young coconut meat | Small amount | Occasionally | Moderate fat |
| Olives | Small amount | Occasionally | Moderate fat |

> **Critical:** Even one avocado (~30g fat, ~240 cal) on a 3,000-calorie day provides ~9% of calories from fat in that one food. Combined with the trace fat in fruit and greens, this can push total fat above the 10% target. Most days on strict 80/10/10 have **no overt fats at all**.

## Food Combining Rules (Per the Diet)

The 80/10/10 diet includes specific food combining principles:

| Rule | Rationale (per the diet) |
|------|--------------------------|
| Eat fruit alone, on an empty stomach | Fruit digests quickly; mixing with other foods causes "fermentation" |
| Wait 20–30 minutes after fruit before eating other foods | Allows fruit to pass through stomach |
| Leafy greens can be eaten after fruit | Greens digest well with fruit |
| Do NOT combine fruit with fats | Fat slows digestion; fruit "ferments" |
| Do NOT combine sweet fruit with acid fruit | Impaired digestion (per the diet) |
| Melons should be eaten alone | "Digest very quickly" |
| Mono-meals (one food per meal) are ideal | Simplest digestion |

> **Scientific assessment:** Food combining theory is **not supported by evidence**. The stomach and intestines handle mixed meals efficiently. The concept of fruit "fermenting" in the stomach is physiologically inaccurate — the acidic stomach environment inhibits fermentation.

## Daily Eating Pattern (Sample Day)

### Breakfast (Mono-Meal)
- **15–20 ripe bananas** (blended into a smoothie with water, or eaten whole)
- *Or:* 1 large watermelon or cantaloupe
- *Or:* 8–10 mangoes
- *Or:* 15–20 dates blended with water

### Lunch (Mono-Meal or Simple Combo)
- **10–15 bananas** (smoothie or whole)
- *Or:* 1–2 large bunches of grapes
- *Or:* 10+ peaches/nectarines (in season)
- *Or:* Large quantity of pineapple + berries

### Dinner (Green Salad + Fruit)
- **Large green salad:**
  - 1 head romaine lettuce or large bowl mixed greens
  - 2–3 tomatoes, diced
  - 1 cucumber, sliced
  - 1 bell pepper, sliced
  - Celery sticks
- **Fruit-based dressing:**
  - Blended mango + tomato + celery (savory-sweet dressing)
  - *Or:* Blended orange + dates + tomatoes
- **Additional fruit after salad:**
  - 5+ bananas or equivalent
  - *Or:* more mangoes, grapes, or dates
- **Overt fat (occasionally, 1–3x/week):**
  - ½ avocado, cubed into the salad
  - *Or:* 1 oz walnuts sprinkled on salad

### Snacks (If needed)
- Fresh fruit (any type)
- Dates

### Beverages
- Water (3–5+ liters/day)
- Fresh fruit juice (not pasteurized/bottled)
- *No coffee, tea (heated), or alcohol*

## MANDATORY Supplementation

The 80/10/10 diet, like all vegan diets, **requires supplementation** of certain nutrients:

| Supplement | Why | Dose | Status |
|-----------|-----|------|--------|
| **Vitamin B12** | Not present in any plant food; deficiency causes irreversible neurological damage | 250–500 mcg/day or 1,000–2,000 mcg 2–3x/week | ❗ **MANDATORY** |
| **Vitamin D** | Limited sun exposure in many climates; no dietary source in raw vegan diet | 1,000–4,000 IU/day (D3 from lichen source) | ❗ **Strongly recommended** |
| **Algae-based DHA/EPA (omega-3)** | No direct source in raw fruit/greens; conversion from ALA (in greens/trace amounts) is inefficient | 200–300 mg/day | ⚠️ **Recommended** |
| **Iodine** | Low in most produce unless grown in iodine-rich soil | 150 mcg/day (kelp supplement or iodized salt — but salt is restricted) | ⚠️ **Monitor** |
| **Zinc** | Bioavailability from raw plant sources is reduced by phytic acid | Monitor intake; supplement if low | ⚠️ **Monitor** |
| **Calcium** | Large volumes of greens help, but may still be inadequate | Monitor; fortified foods (if allowed) or supplement | ⚠️ **Monitor** |
| **Iron** | Non-heme iron from plant sources is less bioavailable | Monitor; pair with vitamin C (abundant from fruit) | ⚠️ **Monitor** |

> **Critical warning:** B12 deficiency can develop within months to years on a vegan diet. Symptoms may be irreversible. **All 80/10/10 practitioners must supplement with B12.** Graham's original book was criticized for downplaying B12 supplementation; later editions and the broader raw food community now acknowledge its necessity.

## Key Nutritional Considerations

### Nutrients Adequately Provided (in abundance)
- **Vitamin C:** Extremely high intake from fruit (often 500–1,000+ mg/day)
- **Potassium:** Very high from bananas and other fruit
- **Fiber:** 40–70+ g/day — among the highest of any diet
- **Water:** 3–5+ liters/day from fruit and intentional drinking
- **Folate:** High from greens and fruit
- **Antioxidants:** Very high from fruit polyphenols
- **Carbohydrates:** Abundant

### Nutrients at HIGH Risk of Deficiency
- **Vitamin B12:** ❗ **No plant source. Deficiency is essentially guaranteed without supplementation.**
- **Vitamin D:** No dietary source; dependent on sun exposure
- **Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA):** Negligible without algae-based supplement
- **Calcium:** May be inadequate despite large green intake
- **Zinc:** Reduced bioavailability from raw plant sources (phytic acid)
- **Iron:** Non-heme iron is less bioavailable; women at particular risk
- **Iodine:** Low in most produce
- **Protein:** Adequate in absolute grams at high calorie intakes, but amino acid profile of fruit-dominated diets may be suboptimal
- **Selenium:** Depends on soil content of produce; may be low
- **Vitamin K2:** Not present in significant amounts in fruit/greens

## Practical and Logistical Challenges

| Challenge | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Cost** | 2,500–4,000+ calories of fresh, ripe, quality fruit daily is very expensive ($15–40+/day) |
| **Sourcing** | Requires constant access to large quantities of ripe, quality fruit; challenging in cold climates or food deserts |
| **Ripeness** | Fruit must be fully ripe for optimal digestion and taste; requires advance planning (e.g., ripening bananas for several days) |
| **Storage** | Large volumes of fruit require significant storage space; ripe fruit spoils quickly |
| **Preparation** | While cooking is eliminated, the sheer volume of food requires frequent shopping, washing, and peeling |
| **Social isolation** | Eating raw fruit-based meals makes dining out, social events, and travel extremely difficult |
| **Dental health** | Constant exposure to fruit acids and sugars throughout the day increases risk of dental erosion |
| **Carrying food** | Many practitioners carry large quantities of fruit (e.g., 10–15 bananas) to work/social events daily |
| **Digestive volume** | The physical volume of food required to meet calorie needs can cause bloating and discomfort, especially initially |
